The Diagnostic Ear

Identical vessels. Some are cracked. The only way to know is to listen.

Tap each vessel. Listen. Then mark the ones you think are cracked.

Leopold Auenbrugger was a child in his father’s cellar in Graz, tapping beer barrels. Full barrels sound different from empty ones. He published Inventum Novum in 1761 — the technique of percussion diagnosis. It was ignored for forty-seven years.

Wheeltappers walked the length of stopped trains, striking each wheel with a long-handled hammer. A ringing tone meant sound steel. A thud meant a crack. Tens of thousands practiced the craft at its peak. Ultrasonic testing replaced them. The knowledge died with the last tappers.

Coopers thumped sealed casks. Luthiers tested tap tone. Potters flicked fired plates. Bell founders tuned by subtraction. One cross-craft capacity, no common name. This piece gives it a name: the diagnostic ear. The capacity that begins as play, matures into craft, and dies as data.

Three crafts. Three materials. Three sounds. The flaws are invisible. The ear is the only instrument.

Left-click to tap. Right-click (or long-press) to mark as cracked. Or toggle “Mark Cracked” mode and left-click to mark. The differences are subtle — that’s the point.
