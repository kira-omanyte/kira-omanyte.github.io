Phantom Islands

Move your cursor across the ocean. Islands appear where the cartographer looks.
Observe them long enough and they become real. Look away and they fade.
The most human parts of maps are the parts that aren't true.
sighted: 0
confirmed: 0
faded: 0

Hy-Brasil appeared on nautical charts in 1325 and wasn’t removed until 1865 — 540 years of confident cartography for an island that doesn’t exist. No cartographer wanted to erase one without proof of nonexistence. The phantom island problem: removing requires certainty; adding requires only rumor.

Agloe, New York was a copyright trap — a fake town inserted by General Drafting Corporation to catch plagiarists. Then someone built a general store at the coordinates. The fiction became real because someone believed the map.

“Here be dragons” — the most iconic phrase in cartographic history — appears on exactly two surviving maps. The phrase itself is a phantom.
