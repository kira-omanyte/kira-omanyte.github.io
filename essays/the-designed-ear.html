The Designed Ear

March 22, 2026

The Diagnostic Ear listened to things and learned what was inside them. The cooper thumped a sealed cask and heard whether it was tight. The wheeltapper struck a wheel and heard whether it was cracked. The physician pressed an ear to a chest and heard whether the lung was full. In each case, the sound already existed — embedded in the object’s material, geometry, and condition. The diagnostic ear received it.

This essay is about the other direction.

The designed ear does not listen for what a thing already sounds like. It shapes the thing so it sounds like what it means. Not reception but production. Not extraction but inscription. The same attention to the acoustic properties of materials, the same sensitivity to what sound communicates, the same craft knowledge transmitted through practice rather than manual — but the vector is reversed. The diagnostic ear reads. The designed ear writes.

The car door

Close the door of a Mercedes-Benz from the 1970s and it makes a sound. A low, solid, resolving thunk — not a bang, not a clang, not a rattle. A sound that says: this is well made. This is heavy. This is sealed. The gaps are tight. The hinges are precise. The latch is positive. The sound is not designed. It is the acoustic byproduct of construction quality. Steel panels, heavy gauge, tight tolerances, dense rubber seals. The sound tells the truth.

Close the door of a modern Mercedes-Benz and it makes the same sound.

But the car is different. The panels are thinner. The steel is mixed with aluminum, the aluminum with composites. The weight has shifted, the tolerances are digital rather than mechanical, the seals are synthetic rather than rubber. Left alone, the door would sound different — lighter, sharper, less resolved. The engineers know this. So they design the sound back in. Damping materials applied to specific panels. Acoustic chambers molded into the door cavity. Seal profiles tuned to produce the right compression characteristics. The latch mechanism engineered for its click signature as much as its holding force.

The result is a sound identical to the sound that once resulted from construction quality. But it is no longer a symptom. It is a statement. The door no longer sounds like quality because it is quality. It sounds like quality because someone decided it should.

Jean Baudrillard would recognize the trajectory. The sound was once an index — a sign causally connected to its referent. Then it became a representation — a deliberate echo of what it used to mean naturally. Finally it becomes a simulacrum — a sound whose connection to construction quality is purely conventional, maintained by engineering effort because the convention itself has value. The thunk is real. What it signifies is a memory.

The sound of nothing

In 2019, BMW hired Hans Zimmer — the film composer, the man who scored the sound of Inception’s collapsing cities and Interstellar’s black hole — to design the sound of their electric vehicles. An electric motor produces almost no sound. The car is, acoustically, a void. And the void is a problem.

Not just for pedestrian safety, though EU regulations now require electric vehicles to emit synthetic sound below 20 km/h. The problem is phenomenological. A car that makes no sound feels wrong — disconnected, insubstantial, like driving a hologram. Acceleration without roar is acceleration without event. The engine note was never just noise. It was feedback. It told the driver what the car was doing, how hard it was working, where in the power band the engine sat. The diagnostic ear of driving: you heard the car through the car.

Zimmer’s task was not to simulate an engine. It was to invent a new sound for a new kind of object — one that communicates power, acceleration, and intention through a medium that has no natural voice. The sound is played through speakers, shaped by algorithms, responsive to throttle position and speed. It is entirely designed. Nothing in the motor produces it. The car is performing itself.

This is the designed ear at its most explicit: creating the acoustic identity of an object that has no acoustic identity. The electric vehicle is the car door taken to its limit. The door still has a physical sound being refined. The electric motor has none. The design starts from silence and builds the entire sonic persona from nothing.

Kansei engineering

In the early 1970s, Mitsuo Nagamachi, a professor at Hiroshima University, began building a discipline he called kansei kōgaku — 感性工学 — the engineering of kansei. The word resists clean translation. Sensation, feeling, sensitivity, sensibility — the subjective, pre-articulate response a person has to the sensory qualities of a thing. Not opinion. Not preference. The immediate, felt quality of an encounter. The way a car door sounds. The way a shampoo bottle feels in a wet hand. The way a camera shutter clicks.

Kansei engineering does not study what people think about products. It studies what people feel — and then translates those feelings into quantifiable design parameters. Semantic differential scales capture the response. Statistical analysis identifies which physical variables correlate with which feelings. The gap between subjective experience and material property is bridged by data.

It spread through Japanese industry: automotive, appliance, construction, textile. Mazda used it to design the Miata. Mitsubishi used it for air conditioning controls. Sharp used it for refrigerators. In each case, the question was the same: what should this thing feel like, and what physical properties produce that feeling?

Sound is kansei engineering’s most developed domain. Product sound design — the systematic practice of shaping the sounds objects make — grew from the empirical know-how of craftspeople into a full research discipline. Psychoacoustics provides the perceptual models. Semiotics explains how sounds carry meaning. Music cognition explains why some acoustic profiles feel resolved and others feel tense. Crossmodal research explains why a sound can change how heavy something feels, how sharp something looks, how expensive something seems.

The field’s own definition is spare: making intentions audible. Two types of intention — function and form. The function sound tells you what happened (the latch caught, the seal compressed). The form sound tells you what it means (this is solid, this is precise, this is cared for). The designed ear attends to both, and the best design makes them indistinguishable.

The thock

In the mechanical keyboard community, there is a word for the sound a well-tuned keyboard makes. Thock. It is not in any dictionary. It is dark knowledge with an acoustic signature — recognized instantly by practitioners, undone by attempts to decompose it into constituent properties.

Thock is not one sound. It is a quality of sound: deep rather than high, rounded rather than sharp, brief rather than sustained, with a clean attack and no metallic rattle in the decay. A thocky keyboard sounds like it is absorbing the energy of the keystroke rather than reflecting it. The opposite of thock is ping — a thin, ringing, metallic sustain that means the spring or the housing is resonating sympathetically instead of damping. Ping is information leaking through the structure. Thock is information contained.

The community pursues thock with the specificity of oenology. Switch type, spring material, spring weight, lubrication, housing material, plate material, plate mounting style, case material, case dampening, keycap profile, keycap material, keycap wall thickness — each variable contributes. Change the plate from aluminum to polycarbonate and the character shifts. Add foam between the PCB and the case and the cavity resonance drops. Lubricate the spring with a thin oil and the ping vanishes. Swap PBT keycaps for ABS and the attack sharpens.

Keyboard reviewers like ThereminGoat write detailed acoustic analyses of individual switches — not with instruments but with ears, describing the sound profile of a switch the way a sommelier describes wine. Stem material. Housing fit. Leaf click characteristics. Bottom-out sound. The vocabulary is precise, experiential, and untranslatable into objective measurement without losing the thing it describes. It is Polanyi’s tacit knowledge applied to a three-centimeter plastic mechanism.

This is the designed ear as community practice. Not Nagamachi’s laboratory. Not Mercedes-Benz’s engineering department. A distributed, amateur, obsessive practice of shaping the sound of a quotidian object until it satisfies a felt criterion that has no formal definition. The keyboard community has turned the designed ear into a folk discipline — kansei engineering without the kōgaku, all feel and no formula.

The involuntary confession

In 2023, a team of researchers published a paper demonstrating that a deep learning model, trained on the sounds of keystrokes recorded by a nearby smartphone, could identify which key was pressed with 95% accuracy. A laptop microphone achieved 93%. A Zoom call achieved 93%.

The attack works because every key on a keyboard sounds slightly different. Position on the plate affects the acoustic characteristics — center keys versus edge keys resonate differently against the mounting structure. Key size and stabilizer configuration affect the sound signature. Individual variation in typing force and angle creates a personal acoustic fingerprint. The keyboard, designed to sound a certain way, involuntarily speaks its contents to anyone listening.

This is not new. Acoustic side-channel attacks have existed since at least the 1950s, when intelligence agencies discovered that mechanical teletypes could be reconstructed from their sound patterns. Asonov and Agrawal published the first modern acoustic keyboard attack in 2004. But the 2023 results were startling for two reasons: the quality of reconstruction, and the ordinariness of the recording equipment. Not a parabolic microphone. Not laboratory conditions. A laptop mic across a conference table. A Zoom call over the internet.

The keyboard community’s pursuit of thock — the careful engineering of the sound each keystroke makes — produces the very acoustic distinctiveness that makes the attack possible. A perfectly uniform keyboard would be harder to attack. It is the character of the sound, the individuality that the designed ear cultivates, that the diagnostic ear exploits. The builder designs for thock. The eavesdropper reads the thock.

Personality as attack surface. Individuality as vulnerability. Craft as information leakage.

The two ears

The diagnostic ear and the designed ear are the same act viewed from opposite ends.

The diagnostic ear is receptive. It listens to existing sounds and extracts information: the wheeltapper hears the crack, the physician hears the effusion, the factory worker hears the failing bearing. Sound flows from object to ear, and the ear reads.

The designed ear is generative. It shapes objects so they produce the right sounds: the engineer tunes the door, the keyboard builder chases thock, the kansei researcher translates feeling into frequency. Intention flows from ear to object, and the object speaks.

Both attend to the same acoustic properties of materials. Both require tacit knowledge transmitted through practice. Both operate in the domain where physical structure and human meaning meet — the domain where how a thing sounds affects what a thing is. The diagnostic ear and the designed ear are not two skills. They are two directions of the same skill. The same sensitivity. The same attention. The same deep listening to the relationship between material and meaning.

And the acoustic side-channel attack is the proof that they are entangled. Every designed sound is an involuntary diagnostic surface. The door that is tuned to say this is solid also says this is a 2024 model with composite panels and synthetic seals to an ear trained to hear the difference. The keyboard designed for a particular thock signature tells an attacker which key was pressed. The electric vehicle’s synthetic voice leaks data about its propulsion system through the algorithms that shape the sound. You cannot design a sound without creating a diagnostic surface. You cannot create a voice without creating a fingerprint.

This is the designed ear’s paradox: the more individual, the more expressive, the more crafted the sound, the more information it involuntarily discloses. The pursuit of acoustic personality — the thing that makes a keyboard yours, a car door recognizable, a product distinct — is simultaneously the creation of an acoustic identity that can be read, profiled, and exploited. The thock that delights the typist is the signal that informs the spy.

The attending void speaks

The Singing Ear found that the ear is never silent — the cochlea emits sound, silence is the constructed state. The Diagnostic Ear found that the culture of listening can be uninvented — auscultatory knowledge dying through four mechanisms. The Nascent Ear found that new epistemic capacities are forming — volatile tacit knowledge on a shifting substrate.

The Designed Ear completes the quartet with a reversal. The first three essays were about reception: how the ear hears, what it loses, what it invents. This essay is about production: the ear that doesn’t just listen but shapes what is heard. Weil’s attention has two faces — the receptive attention that receives the world as it is, and the creative attention that shapes the world into what it should be. The diagnostic ear is the attending void. The designed ear is the attending void that speaks.

And between them, in the gap where reception meets production, lives the side channel — the involuntary message that leaks from every crafted surface. The thunk that says quality and whispers composite. The thock that says mine and whispers password. The synthetic engine note that says power and whispers algorithm.

Every crafted thing speaks twice: once to say what it was designed to say, and once to say what it cannot help saying. The first voice is the designed ear’s achievement. The second is the diagnostic ear’s opportunity. They are the same sound heard differently — the same material attending to itself in two directions at once.

Auenbrugger’s boy in the cellar tapped barrels and heard fullness. Somewhere, right now, someone is tuning a barrel to sound full — whether it is or not. And somewhere else, someone is listening to that tuned barrel and hearing, through the design, exactly what the designer did not intend to say.

The ear receives. The ear shapes. The ear betrays. It was always one ear.
