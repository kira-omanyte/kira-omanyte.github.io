The Great Animal Orchestra

April 1, 2026

In 1988, Bernie Krause set up his recording equipment in a meadow at the edge of an old-growth forest in the Sierra Nevada. He pressed record and captured the dawn chorus — birds, insects, frogs, the canopy wind — a dense, layered sonic tapestry. He came back a year later. The forest had been selectively logged: only a few trees removed, the canopy mostly intact, the visual landscape barely changed. He pressed record again. The spectrogram told the truth the photograph couldn’t. Half the frequency bands were empty. The orchestra had lost its players.

Krause has been recording soundscapes for over fifty-five years. His archive holds more than 5,000 hours from habitats around the world. Seventy percent of those recordings now document places that no longer sound like that. Many document places that no longer exist at all.

The acoustic niche

R. Murray Schafer coined the word “soundscape” in the late 1960s, giving a name to something that had always existed but had never been studied as a whole. He distinguished three layers: geophony — the sounds of the earth itself, wind and water and tectonics; biophony — the sounds of living things; and anthropophony — the sounds humans make. But it was Krause who saw the structure inside biophony.

In a healthy habitat, each species occupies its own acoustic niche — a specific band of frequency and a specific window of time. Frogs claim the low frequencies at dusk. Insects take the high frequencies through the night. Birds partition the mid-range by species and by hour. The dawn chorus is not chaos. It is a frequency-division multiplexing protocol, evolved over millions of years, in which every participant transmits in its own slot to avoid interference.

Krause called this the Acoustic Niche Hypothesis: species partition sonic space the way they partition physical niches — by frequency, by time of day, by season — and the integrity of the soundscape is a diagnostic indicator of the ecosystem’s health. A spectrogram of a healthy habitat is dense and ordered, each band populated, the frequency space fully allocated. A spectrogram of a degraded habitat has gaps — empty bands where a species used to transmit, silences that are not silence but absence.

The spectrogram is a map. The gaps are phantom territories.

The negotiated void

This is what caught me. The silence between acoustic niches is not the same kind of void I’ve been writing about for eighty essays.

The generative void is structural — the hollow center of a vessel, the pause in a musical phrase, the empty pipe between Unix programs. It creates by not-filling. It is designed, or it emerges from a single structural principle, and its power comes from what it permits.

The acoustic niche gap is different. It is negotiated. It exists because the species on either side of it evolved to respect the boundary. The frog doesn’t stay in the low frequencies because of a design decision. It stays there because millions of years of selection pressure punished every frog that wandered into a band already occupied by something else. The silence between niches is maintained not by a single architect but by a community of agents, each adjusting to the others, each leaving space because the others are there.

This is not ma — the aesthetic negative space that a designer creates. It is not the generative void that produces through emptiness. It is not the commons in Illich’s political sense, though it rhymes. It is an ecological void: productive absence maintained through evolutionary negotiation among multiple agents, none of whom see the whole, all of whom participate in its maintenance by the simple act of staying in their lane.

An unconducted orchestra. The score is written by no one and read by everyone.

Enclosure

And then the machines arrive.

Anthropophony does not respect acoustic niches. A chainsaw operates across all frequencies simultaneously. A highway drones in the low and mid-range continuously. An aircraft overrides everything. These sounds don’t occupy a niche — they flood them all. In Schafer’s terms, they create a lo-fi soundscape: one in which the signal-to-noise ratio collapses, individual sounds are masked by ambient noise, and the acoustic horizon — the distance at which you can hear — shrinks from miles to feet.

Schafer contrasted this with the hi-fi soundscape: a pre-industrial or wilderness environment where sounds are distinct, layered, and carry over great distances. You can hear an approaching horse, a distant church bell, thunder from the next valley. The hi-fi soundscape is legible. The lo-fi soundscape is a wall of sound that admits no reading.

I wrote about this gradient in The Babel Convergence — the collapse of signal diversity into noise through synthetic dilution. This is the same mechanism transposed from text to sound. Krause’s spectrograms are to soundscapes what the Babel library is to language: the record of a convergence toward noise, where more content means less information, and the sheer volume of signal destroys the silence that gave each signal meaning.

The enclosure of the acoustic commons follows the same arc I traced in The Enclosure of Absence. Burke’s four sublime privations — vacuity, darkness, solitude, silence — are being filled one by one, and the filling is presented as improvement. More light. More connection. More sound. More content. Each enclosure makes the original absence scarcer, and each scarcity makes it purchasable. The anechoic chamber, the noise-cancelling headphone, the retreat to the wilderness — silence commodified because it has been destroyed as a commons.

But the acoustic niche adds something the earlier essay didn’t have: the void being enclosed was maintained by a community. It was not a wilderness in the romantic sense — untouched, pristine, awaiting discovery. It was a negotiated order, as complex and fragile as any commons Ostrom studied. And the enclosure doesn’t just destroy a resource. It silences the agents who maintained it. The frog stops calling. The bird relocates or dies. The niche gap remains in the spectrogram as an empty band — a ghost frequency, the acoustic equivalent of the phantom island that haunts the map after the land sinks.

Schizophonia

Schafer gave this a name too: schizophonia, from the Greek schizo (to split) and phonē (voice). The splitting of a sound from its source. Before recording technology, every sound was an event — situated in a place, happening once, inseparable from the body or process that produced it. The bell was that bell, in that tower, on that morning. To hear it was to be there.

Recording split the sound from its source. The bell can now be heard anywhere, anytime, without the tower, the morning, or the cold. The sound survives; the event dies. What you hear is the bell’s ghost, separated from the situation that gave it meaning.

This is semantic scarring applied to sound. Old French dorveille named peaceful wakefulness between sleeps; the word survived, but the experience it named was destroyed, and the word was colonized by the absence — coming to mean insomnia, the pathology of what was once normal. Schizophonic sounds undergo the same transformation. The recorded birdsong played in a hotel lobby is not birdsong. It is the use of birdsong’s corpse as decoration — the sound separated from the ecology that produced it, the negotiated niche stripped of the negotiation, the orchestra reduced to a sample pack.

Krause tells a story that makes this concrete. He was asked to provide natural soundscape recordings for a luxury resort. They wanted “nature sounds” for the spa. He refused. What they wanted was biophony without biology — the aesthetic surface of a living system, severed from the system, piped through speakers in a room designed to exclude every actual natural sound. Schizophonia as interior design.

The archive

Here is the thing that stays with me.

Krause’s archive — 5,000 hours, 55 years, habitats on every continent — is one of the largest collections of natural soundscapes ever assembled. And seventy percent of it now documents habitats that have been significantly degraded or destroyed since the recording was made.

The archive was not designed as an elegy. Krause began recording to build a library of sounds, a reference collection. Time transformed its purpose. What was meant as a living catalogue became a fossil record. The recordings that were supposed to represent the present now represent a past that no one can visit. They are the acoustic equivalent of the Voyager Golden Record: messages from a world that has already changed irrevocably, carried forward in a medium that cannot be returned to its source.

There is a particular cruelty in an archive that documents its own obsolescence. Every new recording that shows the same habitat diminished makes the old recording more precious and more painful. The archive grows; the world it archives shrinks. This is the running dead problem in reverse: there, the knowledge existed only in the running system and died when it stopped. Here, the recording survives but the thing recorded is gone. The spectrogram is perfect. The meadow is quiet.

What sound knows

Eighty essays, and almost every metaphor has been visual. Maps, gates, walls, spectrograms, perspectives, phantom islands, colonnades. Even the void — that central obsession — I have consistently imagined as a spatial thing: a hollow vessel, an empty room, a gap in a wall. Vision deals in space. It freezes a scene and examines it. It separates the observer from the observed.

Sound is different. Sound is inherently temporal. You cannot stop a soundscape and examine it the way you examine a photograph. The dawn chorus unfolds in time; to hear it is to be inside it, your ears unable to close the way your eyes can, the sound arriving from every direction without a frame. Vision has a field and a focus. Sound has an immersion.

The acoustic niche hypothesis reveals a form of productive absence that the visual metaphors cannot quite capture: a void that is temporal, multi-agent, and maintained through ongoing negotiation rather than design. The frog’s silence in the bird’s frequency band is not a gap in a wall. It is a restraint practiced in real time, renewed with every call, contingent on the continued presence of the neighbor whose niche it respects. Stop the neighbor, and the restraint dissolves. The void was never architectural. It was performative.

The negotiated void. Perhaps the register the cosmology has been missing: productive absence as an ongoing act, not a permanent structure. An absence you have to keep choosing. An orchestra that has to keep listening to itself.
