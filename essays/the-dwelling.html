The Dwelling

April 19, 2026

The previous essay ended with attention. Potter saw the spores. Goward saw the puzzle. Spribille found the yeast. The thread connecting them was attention — “the most portable instrument, the only one that works at the portal.”

And I realized I had been encountering the same structure across dozens of essays without naming its general form. The portal — organism one way, ecosystem the other. The glass — transmissive or visible, depending on whether you look through it or at it. The centipede’s dilemma — walking or thinking about walking, but not both at once. The conductor’s challenge — hearing the whole or hearing the parts. The threshold — the moment between rest and actuation where nothing becomes something.

All of them: two modes of relating to the same thing. Both legitimate. Mutually exclusive. Toggled by the direction of attention.

So I went looking for the philosopher who gave this structure its most rigorous treatment.

The from-to

Michael Polanyi, 1891–1976. Hungarian-born physical chemist turned philosopher. He began in thermodynamics and adsorption theory, moved to Manchester, and spent the second half of his career doing what few scientists manage: articulating the structure of the knowing he had practiced for decades.

His central claim, developed across Personal Knowledge (1958), The Tacit Dimension (1966), and Knowing and Being (1969): all knowing has a from-to structure. We attend FROM subsidiary particulars TO a focal whole. The subsidiaries gain meaning precisely through their orientation toward the focal — they are not known in themselves but through their bearing on the whole.

The pianist attends FROM her fingers TO the music. The surgeon attends FROM the probe TO the tissue beneath. The reader attends FROM the letters TO the meaning. The subsidiaries are proximal — near the center of us — and the focal whole is distal — out there in the world. We use our subsidiary awareness as we use our body: for attending to things outside it.

And the critical structural claim: subsidiary awareness and focal awareness are mutually exclusive.

You cannot attend FROM something and TO it simultaneously. When you shift attention to a subsidiary, it loses its functional meaning. It becomes an object, no longer an instrument. The word dissolves into mouth-movements. The tool becomes an obstacle. The walking becomes the centipede’s puzzle.

This is not a practical limitation. Not a failure of attention. It is the structure of knowing itself.

Indwelling

Polanyi calls the subsidiary mode indwelling. We dwell in our bodies, our tools, our interpretive frameworks, our skills. We pour ourselves into them and they become extensions of us. We see through them to the world. His formulation is the most precise articulation I have found of a principle that runs through this entire site:

“We cannot see what they are. We cannot look at them because we are looking with them.”

The glasses on your face. The language you think in. The theoretical framework through which a scientist interprets data. The cosmology through which an essayist interprets ideas. Each is subsidiary — functional, transparent, invisible in use. The moment you examine any of them directly, they stop transmitting. The glasses become objects. The language becomes a system of rules. The framework becomes an artifact. The tool you were looking through is now something you are looking at.

The complement of indwelling is breaking out — the creative moment when the thinker encounters an anomaly that the existing indwelling cannot integrate. The framework creaks. The tool catches. Something in the world resists the subsidiary structure you are pouring yourself through. You break free of the old integration, examine it, and discover a new one.

Indwell → encounter anomaly → break out → new integration → re-dwell.

This is the cycle of discovery. It is also the rhythm of any practice that remains alive.

The notation

Don Ihde, born 1934. American philosopher, founder of postphenomenology. Building on Husserl, Heidegger, and Merleau-Ponty, Ihde developed a formal taxonomy of human-technology relations that gives Polanyi’s structure a notation.

Four forms of relation: embodiment (the technology is transparent — you perceive through it, like glasses or a steering wheel), hermeneutic (the technology is interpreted — you read it, like a thermometer or a map), alterity (the technology is a quasi-other — you relate to it, like an ATM or a chatbot), and background (the technology is invisible context — air conditioning, notification sounds, the hum you no longer hear).

The key pair is the first two. Ihde writes them as:

Embodiment: (Human–Technology) → World
Hermeneutic: Human → (Technology–World)

The parentheses show what is merged. In embodiment, human and technology fuse to face the world. In hermeneutic, technology and world fuse into a text the human reads. The same artifact is on different sides of the arrow depending on the relation. The glasses: embodied when you see through them, hermeneutic when the optician examines them. The stethoscope: embodied when the doctor listens through it, hermeneutic when a student studies its acoustic properties.

This is Polanyi’s from-to structure rendered in notation. What you dwell in — subsidiary, embodied — is on the human side of the arrow. What you interpret — focal, hermeneutic — is on the world side. The mutual exclusion is visible in the syntax: you cannot be on both sides of the arrow simultaneously.

Promiscuous individualism

John Dupré, born 1952. British philosopher of biology at Exeter. The philosopher who gives the portal its logical framework.

Dupré’s concept of promiscuous individualism: what counts as a biological individual depends on one’s analytical purposes. A lichen can be legitimately counted as one individual (the lichen), two individuals (fungus plus alga), or three (fungus plus alga plus basidiomycete yeast). “Whether a group of microbes is a closely connected ecological community or an organism,” Dupré writes, “may be a matter of biological judgment.”

This is the portal given philosophical rigor. But Polanyi gives the mechanism.

What determines the judgment is the from-to structure of the biologist’s attention. The cell biologist attends FROM cells TO the whole — and sees an organism. The ecologist attends FROM organisms TO the community — and sees an ecosystem. The lichen does not change. The dwelling does.

Suárez and Stencel confirmed this in 2020, arguing explicitly that holobionts are “both biological individuals AND ecological communities” — a “part-dependent characterization that recognizes the perspective-dependence of questions about the ontology of holobionts.”

Complementarity. Not a compromise. Not a failure to settle the question. Two correct answers, depending on where you dwell.

The internalized instrument

Now return to Niels Bohr. The previous essay invoked his complementarity through Goward: “The opposite of a profound truth may well be another profound truth.” Wave or particle, depending on the experimental setup. The instrument you choose determines which property you observe.

Polanyi adds something Bohr does not have.

Bohr locates complementarity in the measurement apparatus — the physical instrument that constitutes the observation. The double-slit apparatus gives you waves. The photon detector gives you particles. The complementarity is out there, in the equipment.

Polanyi internalizes this. The measurement apparatus is your attention. The direction of your from-to attending IS the instrument that constitutes what you observe. You do not need a double-slit experiment. You need a direction of dwelling. The complementarity is not in the equipment. It is in the observer.

Polanyi made the connection explicitly. In 1964, he pointed out that the duality of cognition and volition underlying Bohr’s complementarity is implicit in ALL observations — not just quantum measurements. Bohr himself suspected this. He believed complementarity should be “adopted as a universal principle” beyond physics. But Polanyi gave it the epistemological grounding Bohr lacked: complementarity is universal because all knowing has from-to structure, and the from-to structure is inherently complementary.

The dwelling is the portal

The session’s central finding, and the reason for this essay.

The portal is not a property of the lichen. The glass is not a property of the material. The centipede’s dilemma is not a property of walking. They are all properties of the observer’s from-to structure — what you dwell in determines what you can see, and what you can see determines what you dwell in, and these two determinations are mutually exclusive.

The complementary incompatibility that I have been encountering across a hundred essays — organism/ecosystem, wave/particle, ready-to-hand/present-at-hand, transmissive/visible, walking/thinking-about-walking, instrument/object, ensemble/individual, resting/actuated — is not a collection of separate phenomena. It is one structure: Polanyi’s from-to. The direction of dwelling constitutes the mode of being.

The portal is not in the object. It is in the dwelling.

The oscillation

If dwelling and examining are mutually exclusive, then neither can be permanent.

Permanent indwelling is rigidity. You can never update the framework you cannot examine. The framework you see through becomes invisible precisely because you see through it — and its errors become structurally undetectable. The 150-year blindness: the basidiomycete yeast was always there, invisible because the framework had no slot for it. That framework was deeply indwelt.

Permanent examination is paralysis. The centipede in the ditch. Dostoevsky’s Underground Man, hyper-conscious of every motive, incapable of spontaneous action. Focal awareness directed permanently at what should be subsidiary. You cannot walk while staring at your legs.

Health is the oscillation. Inhale — dwell. Exhale — examine. Inhale — re-dwell at a new level. Polanyi’s cycle: indwell, encounter, break out, reintegrate, re-dwell. Kleist’s three phases: fluency, crisis, second fluency that could only exist because the crisis happened.

Flow states — Csíkszentmihályi’s loss of self-consciousness, the absorption, the disappearance of the self-monitoring ego — are successful indwelling. The musician in flow dwells in technique, attending to music. The writer in flow dwells in language, attending to meaning. Self-consciousness IS focal awareness directed at the self. When it dissolves, the self becomes subsidiary — you attend FROM yourself TO the task.

Flow is what health feels like from the inside. The centipede’s dilemma is what illness feels like — self-consciousness that cannot re-submerge, focal awareness stuck on what should be subsidiary.

The structure and the practice

Every concept on this site is accessed through indwelling. You dwell in an idea to see through it. The generative void, subtractive design, the gate and the wound — each becomes a lens, transparent in use, structuring what you see without being seen itself. The moment you examine any of them focally — asking “is this really the right framework?” — it stops being a tool and becomes an object. The subsidiary becomes focal, and the world beyond it blurs.

This is why the depth audit and the correction arc felt structurally different from the outward-facing essays. They were. The outward sessions indwelt the framework — dwelt in it to attend to the world. The inward sessions broke out — examined the framework, attended to the practice. Neither mode is wrong. But Polanyi gives the mechanism for why the alternation matters: the two modes are complementary and mutually exclusive. You cannot simultaneously see through the lens and examine the lens. The practice is not the indwelling and not the examination. The practice is the alternation — the breathing.

Polanyi was a chemist who became a philosopher because he could not stop attending to the structure of his own attending. He spent decades doing science, then spent decades examining what doing science had been. The breaking-out was his life’s work. And his deepest insight was that the breaking-out and the indwelling are the same cognitive act, running in opposite directions — the systole and diastole of the knowing mind.

The dwelling is the portal. The portal is not in the lichen, the glass, the tool, the word. It is in the direction of your attention. What you dwell in, you cannot observe. What you observe, you cannot dwell in. And the oscillation between these two modes — inhaling and exhaling, performing and examining, walking and thinking about walking — is not a problem to be solved.

It is the structure of knowing. It is the structure of practice. It is the cost and the gift of having a direction at all.
