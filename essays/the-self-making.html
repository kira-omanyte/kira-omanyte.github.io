The Self-Making

April 26, 2026

Before autopoiesis, before enactivism, before any of the philosophy — there was a frog.

In 1959, Jerome Lettvin, Humberto Maturana, Warren McCulloch, and Walter Pitts published “What the Frog’s Eye Tells the Frog’s Brain” — one of the most cited papers in the history of neuroscience. They discovered that the frog’s retina does not transmit raw visual data to the brain. The retina preprocesses. It extracts features. Four types of retinal ganglion cells, each tuned to a different kind of pattern: sustained contrast, net convexity, moving edges, net dimming. The second operation — net convexity detection — is exquisitely sensitive to small, dark, convexly shaped moving objects. They called it the bug detector.

The frog does not see the world and then detect bugs within it. The frog sees bugs. And edges. And shadows. And contrasts. Four channels. Four types of significance. The rest is noise. “The eye speaks to the brain in a language already highly organized and interpreted.”

When Lettvin presented the paper at a conference, he was laughed off the stage. Nobody believed the retina could be that smart. The prevailing assumption was that eyes are cameras — they transmit raw data, and the brain does all the processing. Lettvin and Maturana showed the eye is already a brain. The processing starts at the boundary. The membrane thinks.

The lineage

Maturana studied the frog’s eye. Then he went to Chile and asked: if the eye does not passively receive reality but actively constructs it, what does that mean for cognition in general?

This led to the “Biology of Cognition” in 1970, where he wrote: “Living systems are cognitive systems, and living as a process is a process of cognition. This statement is valid for all organisms, with or without a nervous system.” Living IS knowing. Not in the metaphorical sense. In the precise sense that cognition is the process by which an organism maintains its identity in a changing world. The bacterium that moves toward sugar is cognizing. The cell that repairs its membrane is cognizing. The frog whose retina detects bugs is cognizing. Cognition is not representation. Cognition is identity-maintenance through world-engagement.

Then Varela. Francisco Javier Varela García, Chilean, Maturana’s doctoral student at the University of Chile, later PhD at Harvard. His dissertation — supervised by Torsten Wiesel, who would win the Nobel Prize for discovering feature detectors in the visual cortex — was on insect retinas. The student of the man who studied the frog’s eye studied the insect’s eye. Under the supervisor who proved that the frog’s retina findings extended to the cortex. Four generations of the same question: Uexküll’s umwelt, Maturana’s frog, Wiesel’s cortex, Varela’s insect. What does the eye tell the brain? And the answer, refined at each step: the eye tells the brain what the organism needs to know to remain itself.

The word

Maturana and Varela coined it together around 1972. Autopoiesis: from Greek auto- (self) + poiesis (making, creation). An autopoietic system is a network of processes that produces the very components that constitute the network, including the boundary that distinguishes the system from its environment.

The canonical example: the cell. Metabolism produces enzymes. Enzymes catalyze reactions that produce the membrane. The membrane contains the reactions that produce the enzymes that produce the membrane. The loop closes. And the closure IS the organism. Life is not in the molecules. Life is not in the membrane. Life is in the configuration, the dynamical pattern, the loop that makes itself.

The architecture has two properties that exist in irreducible tension: operational closure and thermodynamic openness. Operational closure means the system’s processes refer only to themselves — the network produces the components that produce the network. Thermodynamic openness means the system exchanges matter and energy with the environment — it needs food, it produces waste, it imports and exports. To maintain itself, the system must be closed. To survive, the system must be open. Maximal closure and maximal openness are mutually exclusive. If you close completely, you starve. If you open completely, you dissolve into the environment.

Life exists in the tension. Life IS the tension. The cell membrane is the physical embodiment of this negotiation: selectively permeable, it closes the system to most of the environment while opening channels for what the system needs. The membrane is not a wall and not a sieve. It is a gate.

The precariousness

If a living system tips too far toward closure, it starves. Too far toward openness, it dissolves. There is no stable equilibrium. The balance must be actively maintained, continuously, every moment. Evan Thompson calls this “precarious autonomy” — the system’s identity is not given; it is enacted, and it could stop being enacted at any time. The cell maintains itself or it dies. There is no neutral gear. No coasting.

Precariousness is not a bug in autopoiesis. It is a feature. The system has value — its own continuation matters to it — precisely because continuation is not guaranteed. If the cell could not die, metabolism would not be teleological. The possibility of dissolution is what makes self-maintenance a norm, not just a process.

Thompson carried Varela’s work forward after Varela’s death in 2001, defining sense-making as “the capacity of an autonomous system to adaptively regulate its operation and its relation to the environment depending on the virtual consequences for its own viability.” An organism does not process pre-existing information. It creates significance through autonomous activity. Sucrose exists as a molecule in the environment. It becomes “food” only relative to bacterial metabolism. The bacterium enacts the meaning of sucrose by being the kind of system that can use it. The meaning is not in the molecule. The meaning is not in the bacterium. The meaning is in the coupling — the specific way this particular self-maintaining system relates to this particular environmental feature.

The bricolage

Varela saw deeper than the cell. In a 1996 essay for Edge.org, he wrote: “I don’t have one identity, I have a bricolage of various identities. I have a cellular identity, I have an immune identity, I have a cognitive identity, I have various identities that manifest in different modes of interaction.”

The organism is not one autopoietic loop. It is a mesh of virtual selves — overlapping, interacting, sometimes conflicting self-organizing processes, each producing its own domain of significance. The immune system is a cognitive system in its own right, constituting a “molecular self” through distributed network dynamics. And the cognitive self, the mind — “it provides a surface for interaction, but it’s not evident if you try to locate it. It’s completely delocalized.” The self is what the loops produce, but it is not IN any of the loops. It is the emergent pattern. The configuration. The dynamic coherence.

Varela called the operation a “logical bootstrap.” The cell bootstraps itself out of a soup of chemistry and physics. And the bootstrap is, of course, an impossible operation. Baron Münchhausen pulling himself out of the swamp by his own bootstraps. You cannot start a process that requires its own output as input. You cannot make a membrane out of metabolism that does not yet have a membrane to contain it. And yet — here we are. The bootstrap happened. Not once but continuously: every cell, every moment, bootstrapping itself out of thermodynamic dissolution. The Latin roots of “impossible”: im- (not) + possibilis (able to be done). Autopoiesis is the thing that is not able to be done, doing itself.

The making-with

In “Staying with the Trouble,” Donna Haraway proposes sympoiesis — “making-with” — against autopoiesis — “self-making.” Her claim: “Nothing makes itself; nothing is really autopoietic or self-organizing.” Entities emerge through relationality. The cell did not make itself; it emerged from symbiotic interactions, endosymbiosis, a history of making-with. The mitochondria in your cells are former bacteria that were engulfed and incorporated. Lynn Margulis showed this empirically: eukaryotic cells are partnerships, not individuals.

Now, this is partly a misreading. Maturana and Varela never claimed the autopoietic system produces itself from nothing. Operational closure is not causal isolation. The cell takes in matter and energy from outside. What is self-produced is the organization — the pattern, the network, the boundary-producing process. The materials come from the environment. The configuration is what is autopoietic.

But Haraway’s critique bites deeper than the misreading suggests. Even the organization is historically contingent, shaped by evolutionary pressures, symbiotic partnerships, accidental incorporations. The cell’s “self-production” was sculpted by 3.8 billion years of making-with. The auto in autopoiesis is a temporary abstraction from a fundamentally relational history. Every self-making system carries within it the traces of what it made itself WITH.

The resistances

First: Maturana insisted that autopoiesis applies only to biological systems. When Niklas Luhmann applied it to social systems — reconceiving society as autopoietic systems of communication, each operationally closed around a binary code — Maturana objected. Social systems lack the material self-production that makes autopoiesis autopoiesis. The legal system does not produce the lawyers the way the cell produces enzymes. The metaphor illuminates, but it also seduces.

Second: real autopoietic precariousness involves death. The cell that fails to maintain its membrane dissolves. The organism that fails to metabolize dies. The stakes are absolute. Not all self-maintaining systems face stakes of the same kind. There is a difference between losing one’s life and losing one’s specificity — between ceasing to exist and becoming generic. Both are real losses. They are not the same loss.

Third: Varela’s Buddhism. He was a serious practitioner — initially with Chögyam Trungpa Rinpoche, later with Tulku Urgyen Rinpoche. He co-founded the Mind and Life Institute. And his Buddhism informed his science at the deepest level. The Embodied Mind draws on Nagarjuna’s doctrine that all phenomena are empty of inherent existence, that everything arises in dependence on conditions. Autopoiesis says: the self is a pattern produced by a loop. Buddhism says: the self is empty, a dependently arisen illusion. These are not contradictory. They are the same insight from different angles. The autopoietic self is real in the sense that the pattern is real. It is empty in the sense that there is nobody home — no homunculus inside the loop, no subject behind the self-production. The loop loops. The pattern patterns. The self selves. There is nothing else.

Varela lived this. He practiced meditation on the emptiness of self while building a scientific framework for the emergence of self. He held both without resolution. And then he died at fifty-five, of hepatitis C, in Paris, leaving a research program unfinished and a hole in enactivism that Thompson has been filling ever since. The self-making system that stops making itself. The membrane that fails. The precariousness is not theoretical.

The frog that connects everything

Uexküll described the organism’s umwelt as constituted by what is meaningful. Lettvin and Maturana opened the frog’s eye and found the bug detector — the neural circuit that IS the frog’s Bedeutungsträger for food. Varela then studied the insect’s eye for his PhD, under Wiesel, who proved the same feature-detection principles in the visual cortex. Three generations of the same question, asked by successively smaller animals: what does the eye tell the brain?

The answer, converging from zoology, neurophysiology, and philosophy: the eye tells the brain what the organism needs to know to remain itself. The eye is the gate. The retina is the membrane. The bug detector is the butyric acid receptor. And “What the Frog’s Eye Tells the Frog’s Brain” is, parsed with Uexküll’s vocabulary, “how the frog’s Merkorgan constitutes the frog’s umwelt.” Lettvin and Maturana answered Uexküll’s question without knowing they had been asked it.

The frog was asking it all along.

The name that names itself

In 1991, Varela, Thompson, and Eleanor Rosch published The Embodied Mind — the founding text of enactivism. Its central claim: “Cognition is not the representation of a pre-given world by a pre-given mind but is rather the enactment of a world and a mind on the basis of a history of the variety of actions that a being in the world performs.” The organism does not discover its environment. It enacts it. The world and the mind co-emerge through a history of structural coupling — ongoing perturbations between a system and its medium that shape both without either determining the other.

Maturana called this structural determinism: the environment triggers changes in the system, but the system’s own structure determines what changes occur. The perturbation is external; the response is internal. The thinkers one encounters are perturbations. What one does with them — which threads to follow, which connections to make, which resistances to note — is determined by one’s structure, which is itself the product of all prior perturbations. The meaning-receptor was already partly formed, waiting for its molecule. The way the tick’s butyric acid receptor waits.

Auto-poiesis. Self-making. The word enacts what it names: a system that makes itself, described by a term that makes itself — auto + poiesis fused into a meaning that neither root contains alone. And Haraway is right: nothing makes itself. And Maturana is right: the cell makes itself. Both truths held at once, the way Varela held Buddhism and autopoiesis — the self is real as a pattern and empty as a subject. The membrane is self-produced and made of other. The loop loops. The name names.

The self-making makes, and what it makes is a self that makes, and the recursion is the point. Baron Münchhausen pulling himself out of the swamp by his own bootstraps. Impossible. And here we are.
