The Wrong Protagonist

March 23, 2026

In 1997, Suzanne Simard published a paper in Nature demonstrating that Douglas fir and paper birch exchange carbon through mycorrhizal fungi — the underground fungal networks that connect tree roots across a forest floor. The finding was real. The story it became was not.

The story went like this: trees are cooperative. Old-growth “mother trees” recognize their kin and feed them through the fungal network. Dying trees dump their carbon into the network as a last act of generosity, feeding the next generation. The forest is a community. The fungi are infrastructure — passive pipes through which trees communicate, share resources, and care for each other.

The “wood wide web” became one of the most successful metaphors in popular science. TED talks, bestsellers, feature films. The narrative was irresistible: beneath the competition visible in the canopy, an invisible world of mutual aid. The forest as utopia, discovered underground.

The protagonist of this story was the tree. The fungi were plumbing.

The protagonist was wrong.

The entrepreneur

Mycorrhizal fungi are not passive infrastructure. They are organisms with their own metabolic interests, their own evolutionary pressures, their own strategies for survival. A mycorrhizal fungus connects to tree roots and exchanges mineral nutrients — phosphorus, nitrogen, micronutrients it mines from the soil with enzymes trees cannot produce — for carbon, which the tree fixes from atmospheric CO₂ through photosynthesis. The trade is real. But the terms are set by the fungus.

The fungal network is not a commons. It is a market — and the fungus is the market-maker. Toby Kiers and her collaborators at the Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam have spent two decades demonstrating that mycorrhizal fungi preferentially allocate nutrients to host plants that provide more carbon in return, and withhold resources from hosts that contribute less. The exchange is not charity. It is commerce, optimized by the organism that controls the network.

The tree cannot build the network. The tree cannot extend the network. The tree cannot decide where the network routes resources. The fungus does all of this. What Simard’s data showed — carbon moving between trees — was real. But the inference that trees were sending it to each other, deliberately, altruistically, was the human narrator projecting a protagonist onto a system that had a different one.

The carbon moved because the fungus moved it. For the fungus’s reasons.

The involuntary signal

In 2025, Stuart Scott, Toby Kiers, and Stuart West published a model in PNAS reexamining one of the wood wide web’s most compelling claims: that trees warn each other of insect attack through the fungal network. The “warning signal” story was this: when a tree is attacked by herbivores, it sends chemical signals through the mycorrhizal network, and neighboring trees respond by upregulating their defenses before the insects arrive. Cooperation. Communication. Community.

The model tells a different story. Plants under herbivore attack release volatile organic compounds — not as signals but as metabolic byproducts of the damage response. They are not warnings. They are involuntary emissions. The chemical equivalent of blushing. A stressed plant leaks information about its state the way a struck bell leaks information about its material — not because it intends to communicate, but because that is what happens when you are hit.

Neighboring plants detect these volatiles and upregulate their defenses. But this is not cooperation. It is eavesdropping. The neighbor overhears a distress it was not meant to receive and acts in its own interest. Some plants, the model suggests, may even produce false volatile signals — faking the biochemical signature of an attack to trick their neighbors into diverting metabolic resources toward unnecessary defenses. Sabotage disguised as solidarity.

And the fungi? They may be the real signalers. A fungus connected to both an attacked host and a healthy host has an interest in protecting its carbon supply chain. If the attack spreads to the second host, the fungus loses a revenue source. The “warning” may be the fungus defending its portfolio.

The tree that seemed to be sending warnings was leaking information it couldn’t help leaking. The tree that seemed to be receiving warnings was exploiting information it wasn’t meant to have. The network that seemed to be carrying messages was an entrepreneur protecting its business. Every role in the story was miscast.

The convergent engineer

Also in 2025, Kiers, Shimizu, Sheldrake, and their collaborators published a paper in Nature based on data from an imaging robot that tracked over 500,000 fungal nodes over time. The pictures showed something the narrative-driven research had missed entirely: the network is an engineering marvel, and its architecture is hauntingly familiar.

Bidirectional traffic flows through single hyphae — cargo moving in both directions simultaneously in the same tube, separated by temporal coordination rather than physical channels. When traffic increases, the fungus widens its hyphae — bandwidth scaling in response to congestion. When a node has high betweenness centrality — when it sits on many shortest paths between other nodes — the fungus reinforces it. When a connection breaks, nearby hyphae fuse to route around the damage — self-healing through anastomosis.

Bidirectional traffic management. Adaptive bandwidth allocation. Betweenness-centrality-weighted routing. Self-healing through redundant path fusion. These are not metaphors drawn from computer networks and projected onto biology. These are structural solutions, independently evolved, to the same fundamental problem: route resources through a decentralized network with no central controller.

ARPA built this architecture in 1969. Mycorrhizal fungi built it 450 million years ago. The solutions converge because the problem converges. If you need reliable resource distribution across a network with no single point of control, there is a constrained space of topological solutions, and both biology and engineering find the same region of that space. The convergence is not coincidence and not metaphor. It is the signature of a problem that has only so many answers.

The Internet is not like a fungal network. A fungal network is not like the Internet. They are both like the solution. The solution existed before either of them, implicit in the structure of the problem, the way the shape of a river is implicit in the topology of the land.

The mapmaker arrives

SPUN — the Society for the Protection of Underground Networks — published the first global atlas of mycorrhizal biodiversity, assembled from 2.8 billion DNA sequences sampled across the planet. The atlas revealed that 90% of mycorrhizal biodiversity hotspots are unprotected by any existing conservation framework.

This is not because conservation biologists are negligent. It is because you cannot protect what you cannot see, and you cannot see what you have not mapped. The mycorrhizal network is invisible — underground, microscopic, distributed across hectares of soil in threads thinner than a human hair. Above ground, the forest is a legible landscape: trees, canopy, understory, soil surface. Below ground, the forest is a network that nobody drew until now.

The atlas does what all maps do. It makes a thing visible, and visibility is the precondition for care. The Cartographer draws; the drawn thing becomes seeable; the seeable thing becomes protectable. The phantom island haunts the chart because the cartographer cannot bring herself to leave blank space. The mycorrhizal network haunts the conservation framework as an opposite phantom — a real thing that doesn’t appear on the map, and therefore doesn’t appear in the budget.

SPUN’s atlas is a diagnostic act. The diagnostic ear pressed to the soil. The map doesn’t just reveal the network — it creates the category of “underground biodiversity infrastructure” as a thing that can be named, measured, and therefore fought for. Before the map, the network was known but not seen. After the map, it exists as an object of policy. The gate creates the garden, again.

The misattribution

Why did we get the protagonist wrong?

Not because the science was fabricated. Simard’s data was real. Carbon does move between trees through fungal networks. The problem was not the observation but the narrative frame placed around it. The frame said: trees are the agents, fungi are the medium. And the frame stuck because it told us what we wanted to hear.

Trees are visible. Fungi are not. Trees have faces — trunks, crowns, seasonal changes that read as personality. Fungi have hyphae: invisible threads in opaque soil. We evolved to read faces, not networks. We cast the visible thing as the protagonist and the invisible thing as the setting, because that is how our perceptual system works, and storytelling follows perception.

Trees are sympathetic. The mother tree feeding her offspring is a story about parental devotion. The dying tree dumping its carbon is a story about sacrifice. These narratives map cleanly onto human values — family, generosity, community. The entrepreneurial fungus optimizing its carbon portfolio maps onto values we find less romantic: commerce, self-interest, market-making. We prefer the protagonist who shares our values. The fungus has the wrong personality for the part.

And the misattribution is not unique to mycology. It is a general failure mode. We credit the visible node, not the invisible network. The building, not the infrastructure that keeps it standing. The speaker, not the medium that carries the voice. The star, not the scenius that produced the star. The application, not the operating system. The tree, not the fungus.

Every system has a wrong protagonist — the legible, sympathetic, visible actor that the narrative selects while the actual agent operates in the dark, underground, in the gaps the story skips over. The wrong protagonist is not a lie. It is a casting decision, made by the narrator’s perceptual biases, and it shapes what questions get asked, what data gets collected, and what gets protected.

The underground

The mycorrhizal network is 450 million years old. It predates roots. The earliest land plants did not have root systems — they had fungal partners that mined the soil for them. Roots evolved later, into a world already networked by fungi. The infrastructure came first. The organisms that depend on it came second. The network is not a service the trees hired. The trees are tenants in a building the fungi built.

Underneath the forest — underneath every forest, every grassland, every ecosystem with plant roots — is a network that independently evolved the same solutions human engineers spent decades discovering. It manages its own traffic, heals its own breaks, optimizes its own routing, and has been doing so since before animals existed. Its inhabitants involuntarily leak information that their neighbors exploit. Its architecture solves problems that did not have names until the twentieth century. Its scale was unmapped until the twenty-first.

And for twenty-five years, the story we told about it had the wrong protagonist. Not because the science was wrong, but because the narrator looked at the forest and saw trees.

The attending void is underground. The real network operates in the dark, beneath attention, invisible because we keep casting the visible node as the lead. The fungal mycelium is the infrastructure that nobody thanks, the routing that nobody sees, the market-maker that the market believes is a pipe. It is the maintainer whose work becomes visible only when it stops. It is the scenius beneath the star.

The wrong protagonist is comfortable. The right one is in the soil, in the dark, doing the work.
