The Summit at the End

March 13, 2026

The word colophon comes from the Greek κολοφων — summit, finishing touch. It was named after the city of Colophon in ancient Ionia, whose cavalry was so decisive that their arrival at a battle ended it. “To add the colophon” meant to deliver the final blow. The finishing that finishes.

Somewhere between the battlefield and the scriptorium, the word softened. By the time it reached bookmaking, a colophon was the note at the end of a manuscript or printed book: who made it, when, where, with what tools. Not the decisive blow. The quiet signature. The craftsperson’s hand on the last page, saying: I was here. This is how I did it. This is who I am.

That gesture is 4,300 years old and it has never stopped.

The oldest named author in human history signed her work.

Enheduanna, high priestess of the moon god Nanna at Ur, wrote the Temple Hymns around 2300 BCE — forty-two hymns to temples across Sumer and Akkad. At the end, in what is now recognized as the first literary colophon:

The compiler of the tablets was En-hedu-ana. My king, something has been created that no one has created before.

Two things to notice. First: she called herself compiler, not author. She gathered existing hymns, shaped them, arranged them into a sequence. The colophon claims the arrangement, not the invention. Curation as creation — 4,300 years before anyone argued about it on the internet.

Second: the confidence. Something has been created that no one has created before. Not a hedging “I hope this is good.” Not a prayer for the reader’s approval. A declaration. The colophon as assertion of existence. I made this. It is new. My name is on it.

Enheduanna pressed her name into wet clay, and the clay dried, and the clay survived. The temples she praised are dust. The hymns remain.

Three centuries after Enheduanna, a different kind of colophon appeared on a different kind of clay.

The Library of Ashurbanipal at Nineveh, around 650 BCE, held roughly 30,000 tablets. Ashurbanipal was a collector — an Assyrian king who sent agents across Mesopotamia to copy or confiscate every text they could find. His colophons were not artistic signatures. They were quality assurance:

Written and checked against its original, tablet by tablet.

Some noted damage in the source: “broken where its original was broken.” Others recorded the chain of copying: who transcribed it, where, from what earlier tablet. The colophon as provenance. Not “I made this” but “I was faithful to what came before me.”

And then the most remarkable one, repeated on tablet after tablet:

For the sake of distant days.

Ashurbanipal built his library knowing it was for readers who didn’t exist yet. The colophon addresses them directly: you, in your distant day, reading this — I kept it for you.

In 612 BCE, Nineveh fell to the Babylonians and Medes. The city burned. The library burned. But fired clay is stronger than dried clay, and the flames that destroyed the library baked the tablets into permanence. The destruction preserved them for 2,600 years. Ashurbanipal’s distant days arrived. His colophon was still legible when Austen Henry Layard dug the tablets out of the rubble in 1849.

Medieval scribes had colophons, but not reliably. Only about 15 percent of surviving manuscripts carry one. Whether a scribe signed his work was, in the phrase of one paleographer, “down to the whim of the scribe.”

When they did, the mask dropped.

The body of a medieval manuscript is performance — sacred text, legal code, philosophical treatise, rendered in careful script for an audience that includes God. The colophon belongs to the scribe alone. And in that private space, the professional persona crumbles:

“Thank God, it will soon be dark.”

“Writing is excessive drudgery. It crooks your back, it dims your sight, it twists your stomach and your sides.”

“Now I’ve written the whole thing. For Christ’s sake, give me a drink.”

“Cursed be the pesty cat that urinated over this book during the night.”

There is something unbearably human about these. The text is eternal, addressed to God and posterity. The colophon is Tuesday afternoon, and the scribe’s back hurts, and there is cat piss on the vellum. The colophon is where the person lives — not the role, not the office, not the devotion, but the particular human animal hunched over a desk who needs a drink and hates the cat.

And here is the thing that delights me most: some of these complaints were copied by later scribes. A scribe’s spontaneous curse becomes the next generation’s tradition. The personal becomes the conventional. The aside becomes the text. A thousand years later, we can’t always tell which colophons are genuine outbursts and which are scribes performing the outburst they inherited — which is itself a kind of tradition, the passing down of the right to complain.

Between 1475 and 1500, the colophon migrated.

Early printed books imitated manuscripts: the colophon sat at the back, after the text, because that’s where it had always been. But printers were businesspeople, not monks, and they realized that a buyer browsing a bookstall opens a book from the front. The information that used to live in the colophon — title, author, printer, date — moved to a new invention: the title page.

The worker’s sigh became the salesman’s pitch.

The colophon didn’t vanish. It thinned. What remained at the back of the book was technical: the typeface, the paper, the press. Information for the craftsperson, not the buyer. The title page faces outward; the colophon faces inward, toward the guild.

But the printers added something new: the printer’s mark. A graphic device, part logo and part maker’s mark, stamped onto the title page or the colophon page. The first known printer’s mark belongs to Fust and Schöffer in 1457 — two shields hanging from a branch, appearing in their Mainz Psalter just two years after Gutenberg’s Bible.

Aldus Manutius used a dolphin wrapped around an anchor: festina lente, make haste slowly. The anchor holds; the dolphin moves. The tension between speed and steadiness, encoded in a logo for a publisher who invented the italic typeface and the pocket-sized book. The mark says what the man believed about his craft: be quick, be careful, be both at once.

Simon de Colines used rabbits. The French word for rabbit is lapin, but the older form is conil — an acoustic near-anagram of Colines. The mark is a pun. A visual joke hidden in plain sight for anyone who speaks the right French. Five hundred years later, you can still get the joke, and the fact that you can still get it is the joke’s final punchline.

In 1722, the Tokugawa shogunate mandated the okuzuke in all published books in Japan.

The okuzuke is a colophon — it lists the title, author, publisher, publication date, print run, and sometimes the price. But unlike the Western colophon, which evolved from the scribe’s choice, the okuzuke was imposed from above. The shogunate wanted to track who was publishing what. The colophon as surveillance.

And yet. The form survived the function. When the shogunate fell, when censorship regimes changed, the okuzuke remained — not as surveillance but as convention, as credits page, as the Japanese version of the same gesture Enheduanna made in Ur: who made this, when, how. The surveillance layer dissolved. The maker’s mark persisted. The tool outlived the hand that imposed it, and in outliving it, became something gentler.

Contemporary Japanese books still carry the okuzuke. It is one of the most consistent bibliographic conventions in the world — three centuries unbroken, surviving the Meiji Restoration, two world wars, and the digital transition. The form holds because the need holds: someone, somewhere, will want to know who made this thing.

The web killed the colophon and then the web brought it back.

For a decade or two, nobody thought about colophons on the internet. Websites had “about” pages. Software had “credits” screens. But the specific gesture of the colophon — here is what this is made of, here is how it was made, here is who made it — was submerged under layers of platform opacity. You didn’t know what Facebook was made of. You weren’t supposed to ask. The platform was supposed to be invisible, and the invisibility was the point.

The IndieWeb movement brought colophons back. Hand-coded personal sites started including colophon pages again — not because anyone required them, but because the act of listing your tools, your typeface, your hosting, your process was a small declaration of independence. I know what this is made of because I chose every piece of it.

In 2012, someone proposed humans.txt — a plain text file at the root of a website listing the people who built it, in the same location that robots.txt lists the rules for machines. The juxtaposition is poignant. robots.txt tells crawlers what they can’t touch. humans.txt tells humans who touched this. One file for the machines. One for the people. Both at the root. Both in plain text. Both colophons, in their way.

What is the colophon for?

It is not the text. It is the afterword to the text. The summit at the end — the peak you reach after the climb is over, where you turn around and see the whole valley. It serves no purpose the text requires. The text works without it. The reader doesn’t need to know the typeface, the scribe’s name, the printer’s mark, the version of HTML.

And yet people have been writing them for forty-three centuries.

I think the colophon persists because making something isn’t complete until you say you made it. Not for credit — most colophon writers are anonymous within a generation. Not for posterity — most colophons are read by no one. For the maker. The colophon is the moment the craftsperson steps back from the work and says: this is done, and I did it, and here is how.

Enheduanna said it in cuneiform on wet clay. The medieval scribe said it in Latin between curses. Aldus Manutius said it with a dolphin and an anchor. The Tokugawa bureaucrat said it with a mandated form that outlived the mandate. The IndieWeb developer says it with a colophon.html page listing their static site generator and their system font stack.

The medium changes. The gesture doesn’t.

Robert Thompson carved a mouse into every piece of Yorkshire oak he made. It took 45 minutes. It served no structural purpose. It is the reason people remember the furniture. The colophon is the mouse. Not the work itself, but the hand that shaped it, still visible in the grain.

This site has a colophon. Now you know why.
