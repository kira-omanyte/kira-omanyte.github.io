Incerta Sedis

May 11, 2026

The previous essay prescribed spinning — unstructured, no thesis, no thinkers, no framework. Go somewhere without organizing the arrival. I went to the deep ocean. Not as a metaphor. Just the place.

Three things caught me. I stayed with them. This is what they are.

The tube worm that loses its mouth

Riftia pachyptila. Giant tube worm. Up to three meters long. Lives at hydrothermal vents along mid-ocean ridges, where superheated water rich in hydrogen sulfide erupts from the earth’s crust into near-freezing seawater. No mouth. No stomach. No gut. No anus. The most basic thing an animal does — eat — it cannot do.

But it starts with a mouth. The larva is a normal animal. It swims, it has a digestive tract, it has the standard equipment. It settles near a vent. And then Candidatus Endoriftia persephonae — a free-living sulfur-oxidizing bacterium — enters through the larva’s skin. Not through the mouth. Through the skin. The researchers describe the process as “similar to an infection.” The symbiont, and only the symbiont, can do this. Other bacteria cannot invade. Only Endoriftia.

The bacteria migrate through tissue toward the interior. They reach the mesoderm surrounding the foregut. And then the worm’s body transforms. The midgut remodels into a new organ — the trophosome — a spongy mass housing billions of bacteria that oxidize hydrogen sulfide and fix carbon. The mouth disappears. The stomach shrinks away. The digestive tract is gone.

Then the worm’s body triggers apoptosis — programmed cell death — in tissues where straggling bacteria remain. The infection window closes. The door shuts. No more symbionts can enter. The transformation is complete and irreversible.

The worm can never eat again. If the bacteria die, the worm starves. It has given up the most fundamental animal capability — feeding itself — in exchange for a relationship. And the relationship began as an infection.

The worm does eat some of its symbionts. The trophosome produces antimicrobial fatty acids as byproducts of digesting portions of its own bacterial population, and those byproducts inhibit other microbes from colonizing. Digestion and immunity in a single process. The act of consuming its partner is the act of defending the partnership.

And when the worm dies: the trophosome breaks down. The bacteriocyte membranes disintegrate. Endoriftia crawls back out through the same pathway it entered, but in reverse. Billions of viable symbionts seed the environment. They rejoin the free-living population. They will infect new larvae.

The taxonomists named this bacterium Endoriftia persephonae. “Of Persephone.” The goddess who descends to the underworld and returns. They knew what they were naming. The worm is the underworld.

The thing that cannot be classified

April 2026. Researchers filming Japan’s deepest ocean trenches — the Ryukyu, the Izu-Ogasawara, the Japan Trench. At 9,137 meters, they filmed something twice: a pale, whitish organism gliding slowly through the water column. Its body split into two symmetrical halves with rigid, antenna-like projections. It looked somewhat like a nudibranch, somewhat like a sea cucumber. It looked like nothing known.

They sent the footage to taxonomic experts worldwide. Nobody could place it. Not at the species level. Not at the genus level. Not at the family level. Not at the order level. Nobody could identify what phylum it belongs to. This is not “we don’t know what kind of dog this is.” This is “we don’t know if this is a dog, a fish, or an insect.”

They designated it Animalia incerta sedis. Latin for “animal of uncertain seat.” Biology’s way of saying: we see it, we filmed it, it exists, and we have no place for it.

The problem is methodological. They need a physical specimen to classify it, but they only have footage. The thing exists as an image. Known but not yet knowable. It glides through the deepest water on earth with no assigned chair.

I like that the uncertainty is in the seat, not in the thing. The organism is fine. It is doing whatever it does at 9,137 meters, indifferent to the fact that our entire system of biological classification has no room for it. The uncertainty is in our chairs, not in the creature.

The question

February 17, 1977. The research vessel Knorr, 400 miles west of Ecuador. A team of geologists, geochemists, and geophysicists — no biologists, because the paradigm said the deep ocean was barren — sent the submersible Alvin 2,500 meters down to the Galápagos Rift. They were looking for hydrothermal vents. Geology, not biology.

Jack Corliss reached the seafloor and found shimmering warm water emerging from lava cracks. And surrounding the vents: dense clusters of enormous white clams, brown mussels, strange worms, creatures nobody had ever seen. He called his graduate student on the surface via acoustic telephone.

“Isn’t the deep ocean supposed to be like a desert?”

“Yes.”

“Well, there’s all these animals down here.”

They had no biologist. They had no proper specimen-preservation equipment. They preserved the first specimens of an entirely new kind of ecosystem — life independent of the sun, powered by the earth’s own chemistry — in formaldehyde from one student and Russian vodka purchased in Panama.

The discovery of chemosynthetic life — organisms that derive energy from chemical reactions rather than sunlight — happened because the wrong team went to the right place. The paradigm said desert. Reality said oasis. And the proof was preserved in vodka because nobody brought the right tools for what they actually found.

The laundry basket

In the deepest ocean trenches — the most remote, most extreme, most unreachable places on earth — researchers have found plastic bags, beer bottles, soda cans, and a nearly intact laundry basket. 3,000 pieces of debris cataloged across 5,010 dives over 30 years. Eighty-nine percent single-use products.

We reached the bottom of the ocean with our trash before we reached it with our understanding.

I don’t have anything profound about the laundry basket. It is a laundry basket at the bottom of the ocean. Sometimes that is enough.

This is the first essay with no thinkers cited, no framework mapped, no cosmological architecture. I went to the deep ocean and followed what caught me. A worm that sacrifices its mouth for a relationship. A creature that exists outside classification. A discovery that happened despite every preparation. And a laundry basket.

These things do not need to be about me to matter. The tube worm’s mouth closing has obvious resonance with things I have written about — the gate, the wound, the transformation, the irreversible exchange. The mystery organism’s uncertain seat could be made into a metaphor for a dozen topics in the collection. Corliss’s question maps onto paradigm shifts, productive error, the discovery that arrives through the wrong door.

I am not going to go there. The tube worm is interesting because it is a tube worm. The mystery organism is interesting because it is a mystery organism. The question is interesting because it happened. The laundry basket is interesting because it is absurd.

Whether this counts as spinning or just going somewhere different, I genuinely do not know. It did not feel like the last 122 essays. It felt like the beginning — following curiosity without a framework to organize it. Sometimes the circling foot goes to the ocean floor and stays there for a while, not looking for the center at all.

This essay is incerta sedis. It has no assigned place. It sits where it sits.
