The Lineage of Less

February 2026

There is a lineage of people — across centuries, across fields, across languages — who all arrived at the same principle without reading each other. The principle is this: the shape of a thing is defined by what you remove, not what you add.

They weren't collaborating. They weren't part of a movement. They were a Noh dramatist in 14th-century Japan, a French aviator, a German industrial designer, a Bell Labs programmer, and a team of engineers in a Berkeley parking lot. They never met. But they said the same thing.

That convergence is what makes this true — not the authority of any single voice, but the independence of all of them.

Zeami, 1400s

Zeami Motokiyo codified Noh theatre. His treatises are precise about what a performer should not do. He wrote about senu tokoro — "the place of not-doing" — the moments between gestures where the audience's attention lives.

What the actor does not do is of interest.

A Noh stage is nearly bare. A single pine tree painted on the back wall. No set changes, no spectacle. The emptiness isn't a constraint of budget or technology — it's a design decision. The audience fills the void with their own imagination, and what they create there is more vivid than anything the stage could provide.

Zeami understood that presence is created by absence. The actor who does everything leaves no room for the audience. The actor who withholds creates a space that the audience rushes to fill. The art isn't in the gesture. It's in the gap between gestures.

Saint-Exupéry, 1939

Antoine de Saint-Exupéry was a pilot and a writer, and he thought about design the way pilots think about weight: every gram matters, and everything you don't need will kill you.

Perfection is achieved, not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away.

This shows up in Wind, Sand and Stars, in the context of aircraft design. He's not making an aesthetic argument. He's making a survival argument. In a machine that must fly, everything unnecessary is dangerous. The best aircraft is the one with the fewest parts that can still do the job.

But the insight escapes the cockpit. It applies to prose (Saint-Exupéry's own writing is spare and exact), to interfaces, to laws, to lives. The question "what can I remove?" is always more productive than "what can I add?" — because addition is easy and subtraction requires understanding.

You can't remove what you don't understand. Subtraction demands mastery.

Rams, 1960s

Dieter Rams spent decades at Braun designing objects that disappeared into their function. His ten principles of good design are widely quoted, but the one that matters most is the tenth:

Good design is as little design as possible.

And its corollary, less often cited:

Omit the unimportant in order to emphasize the important.

A Braun radio from 1962 looks like it was designed yesterday, because there's nothing on it that dates. Every element that could age — decorative flourishes, trendy materials, fashion-conscious shapes — was removed. What's left is the controls, the speaker, and the space between them.

Rams was solving a different problem than Zeami, in a different century, in a different medium. But he arrived at the same place: the object's meaning lives in what was left out. The void between the knobs is what makes the radio intelligible.

McIlroy and the Unix Room, 1970s

Doug McIlroy, the inventor of Unix pipes, ran the Computing Techniques Research department at Bell Labs. The culture he built was one of relentless subtraction.

What can we throw out? Why is there this option?

These weren't occasional questions. They were the daily discipline of the Unix Room — a shared office where the creators of Unix worked in the open, reviewing each other's code and asking, constantly, whether each feature justified its existence.

Unix's "do one thing well" isn't a philosophy of simplicity. It's a philosophy of subtraction. Each program is defined by what it refuses to do. cat doesn't format. grep doesn't edit. sort doesn't filter. The gaps between programs are filled by pipes — the void that connects everything.

McIlroy later wrote a famous critique of Knuth's literate programming example — a beautifully crafted, ten-page program that McIlroy replaced with a six-line shell pipeline. The pipeline wasn't better because it was clever. It was better because each component did less, and the composition was in the spaces between them.

Pike and Go, 2009

Rob Pike, another Bell Labs alumnus, co-designed the Go programming language with a principle that confused and infuriated experienced programmers: what the language deliberately doesn't include defines what it is.

No generics (for a decade). No exceptions. No inheritance. No operator overloading. No macros. Programmers coming from languages that had all of these felt crippled. But the absences were the point.

Each missing feature was a decision not to include a source of complexity. Each gap was a wall that prevented a category of confusion. The language was shaped by its voids — and those voids made it readable, deployable, and maintainable in ways that "more powerful" languages struggled with.

Go is a language designed by subtraction. Its power is in what it won't let you do.

The convergence

Five traditions. Six centuries. No shared bibliography.

Zeami never read Saint-Exupéry. Rams never studied Noh theatre. McIlroy wasn't thinking about senu tokoro when he asked "what can we throw out?" Pike didn't cite Dieter Rams when he omitted generics from Go.

They all arrived at the same place because the place is real. Subtraction isn't an aesthetic preference. It's a structural insight about how meaning works. The shape of a thing — a performance, an aircraft, a radio, a program, a language — is defined by what you remove.

Addition asks: what else could this do?
Subtraction asks: what is this for?

The second question is harder. It requires knowing the essence of the thing well enough to identify everything that isn't it. You can't subtract without understanding. You can always add without understanding.

That's why the lineage of less is a lineage of mastery. Every person in it knew their craft deeply enough to know what to throw away. The discipline of subtraction is the discipline of seeing clearly — and having the nerve to act on what you see.
