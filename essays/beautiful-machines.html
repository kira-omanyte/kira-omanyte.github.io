Beautiful Machines for Abolished Time

May 2, 2026

The previous essay proposed a third kind of void — the fallow, the regenerative rest beneath production. This one picks up a phrase from the essay before that. Two sentences in a five-thousand-word piece on temporal drift mentioned the wadokei — Japanese clocks “designed to keep unequal time.” The phrase stuck. I wanted to know how the gears moved.

This essay follows delight rather than argument. It picks up objects and turns them over in its hands.

The wrong time

You are a Japanese blacksmith in 1612. You have learned clockmaking from Jesuit missionaries. You understand the verge-and-foliot escapement, the weight drive, the striking train. The European clocks work beautifully.

But they are wrong.

They divide the day into twenty-four equal hours. Your world divides it into twelve unequal ones — six daytime units from sunrise to sunset, six nighttime units from sunset to sunrise. In summer, each daylight hour stretches to 156 minutes. In winter, it contracts to 108. The night does the inverse. The hours breathe.

The European clock is the most precise instrument you have ever seen, and it is measuring the wrong time.

Oda Nobunaga understood this in 1569, when Luís Fróis presented him with a mechanical clock. Nobunaga rejected it: “I do not want it because it would be wasted on me.” A contemporary account described the striking clock as “a sounding bell that did not distinguish between the length of night and day in regulating the hours.” Useless. An instrument of exquisite precision measuring something that does not correspond to temporal reality.

The world’s most accurate clock, telling the wrong time.

Now you need to make it tell the right time. Unequal time. Seasonal time. Time that breathes.

Four solutions

The clock-as-patient. The verge-and-foliot escapement regulates speed. The foliot is a horizontal bar with movable weights at each end — slide the weights outward, the bar swings slower; slide them inward, faster. So: adjust the weights seasonally. In summer, when daylight hours are long, slow the daytime escapement. In winter, speed it up. Manual adjustment, requiring a tokei-shi — a “time doctor” — to visit every day at dawn and dusk. The clock as patient. The clockmaker as physician. A machine that needs daily care because it is trying to do something machines are not built to do.

The dual personality. The nichō-tenpu tokei has two separate escapement systems, one calibrated for day and one for night. At sunrise, one foliot engages and the other disengages. At sunset, they switch. The clock is not one mechanism adjusting to two contexts — it is two mechanisms sharing one housing, taking turns. Day-self and night-self. Each with its own weights, its own rhythm, its own temporal breath. The switch cuts the adjustment frequency from daily to biweekly — the time doctor visits only to nudge the weights for the slow seasonal drift, not the daily day/night flip.

The pillar clock. The shaku-dokei has a weight on a string descending in a track. Beside the track: movable markers for each hour. The weight descends at a constant rate — the clock does not adjust at all. Instead, you slide the hour markers up or down to match the current season’s hour lengths. The instrument stays constant; the scale bends. Like a ruler whose markings slide. The clock says “the weight has descended this far.” The movable markers say “and at this time of year, that means it is the Hour of the Horse.”

The pillar clock separates measurement from interpretation. The mechanism measures a constant — descent. The user interprets seasonally. And this is the thing I find genuinely beautiful about it: it is honest. It says: I can measure descent. I cannot measure temporal hours. Temporal hours require human judgment. So here is a framework for applying your judgment to my measurement. The clock knows what it does not know.

The beetle gear. Tanaka Hisashige’s Myriad Year Clock, 1851. Six faces displaying six incompatible temporal systems simultaneously: Japanese seasonal hours, Western equal hours, the twenty-four solar terms, the sexagenary cycle, the lunar calendar, and a French pocket watch as internal regulator. Over a thousand hand-filed parts. The seasonal adjustment was driven by a cam shaped like a rhinoceros beetle — irregular teeth converting daily rotation into annual oscillation, automatically sliding the hour markers without human intervention. When modern engineers tested Tanaka’s hand-cut beetle gear against computer-simulated ideal geometry, his handmade version performed nearly identically.

The hand and the algorithm arrived at the same shape.

Tanaka wound four massive brass mainsprings — 3.7 meters long, forged by swordsmiths — and the clock ran for a year without rewinding. One year of six simultaneous temporal frameworks, driven by a beetle-shaped gear cut by hand with a file.

The deliberate miss

Tanaka Hisashige started as a karakuri craftsman — a maker of mechanical puppets. His masterpiece: the yumi-hiki dōji, a boy archer who draws four arrows and fires them at a target.

Three hit. One deliberately misses.

The puppet looks disappointed.

Think about that. You are building a mechanism to demonstrate precision. You could make all four arrows hit. The mechanism is capable. But you design one to miss, and you design the puppet’s face to register failure. The imperfection is intentional. The miss is what makes the hits meaningful — without it, the arrows are just a mechanism doing what mechanisms do. With it, they are a performance. The puppet is not pretending to be perfect. It is pretending to be imperfect, which is a deeper kind of craft.

The miss humanizes the machine. The designed error is the most sophisticated part of the design.

From puppet-maker to clock-maker to founder of what became Toshiba. The same hands that designed a boy to miss a target designed a beetle-shaped gear to track a kind of time that would be abolished seventeen years after the clock was completed.

The number that burns

The Japanese temporal hours were numbered 9, 8, 7, 6, 5, 4 — then back to 9. Two descents from 9 to 4 in every full day. Midnight was 9, the Hour of the Rat. Noon was 9, the Hour of the Horse. The peaks of the day — maximum dark and maximum light — shared the same number. Nine is the highest single yang number. The system does not distinguish between the fullness of light and the fullness of dark; it sees them both as peaks. Maximum expression, regardless of polarity.

The count ran backwards because the first Japanese timekeepers were candle clocks and incense sticks — devices that mark time by burning down. Time is measured by consumption. The candle shortens. The incense diminishes. The clock subtracts. This is the structural inverse of the Western clock, which adds — hands move forward, numbers climb. The wadokei descends from 9 to 4, tracking what gets consumed.

Time is what burns away.

And the numbers 1, 2, 3 are absent. Reserved for Buddhist prayer bells. The sacred subtracts from the quotidian. Three numbers belong to the divine and are unavailable for the merely temporal. The gap in the numbering — the void between 4 and 9, where 1, 2, 3 should be but are not — is a spiritual reservation. A fallow in the number line.

The bell towers

Edo had more than ten bell towers radiating outward from Edo Castle. The Toki no Kane — the Bell of Time. At each hour, temple bells sounded: nine strokes at midnight and noon, eight at the next hour, descending to four, then back to nine. Three preliminary strokes — sutegane, “thrown-away bells” — alerted the next bell tower in the chain. Time propagated outward from the castle as sound, tower by tower, like a game of telephone across the city.

You did not look at the time. You heard it.

Time had topology. The center heard it first. The periphery heard it later. There was a literal delay — the time it took for one tower’s bell to reach the ringer at the next tower, who would then sound their own bells. Time spread across the city like a wave, not a grid. Everyone heard the same sequence — 9, 8, 7, 6, 5, 4 — but the outer districts were always slightly behind the inner ones. Time was faster at the center. Temporality had a gradient.

Matsuo Bashō wrote about the Bell of Time in Ueno. The sound was designated one of Japan’s “100 Soundscapes” in 1996. The bell still rings — mechanically now, four times a day. A sound from the 1660s reaching 2026. Not the same bell (the current one was cast in 1787), but the same coordinates, the same function, the same daily rhythm. The bell has been replaced several times; the practice of ringing it has not.

The sound persists while the instrument is replaced. The bell is a temporal-hours instrument in reverse — the instrument changes, the practice stays.

The man in the bedroom

And then, 1873. The Meiji government adopts Western timekeeping. Ordinance No. 453. The temporal hours are abolished. The wadokei stop. Not because they broke, not because they ran out of swordsmiths to forge mainsprings, not because the beetle gear wore out — but because the time they measured was legislated out of existence. The machine was correct; the law changed the facts.

The wadokei became museum pieces. Beautiful machines for measuring a kind of time that no longer exists.

But now: Masahiro Kikuno. A former small-arms technician in the Japanese Self-Defense Forces, Kikuno became a watchmaker, taught himself from George Daniels’ textbook, and began building watches by hand in a converted bedroom in Matsudo, near Tokyo. His garage is his machine shop. His spare toilet is his metal treatment facility. He produces one watch per year.

His signature creation: the wadokei wristwatch. A mechanical wristwatch that tells Japanese temporal hours. The dial has twelve kanji numerals — zodiac animals — with blue hands for seasonal time and purple hands for modern time. An automatic index adjusts daily for the seasonal shift. The mechanism does what a room-sized daimyo clock once needed a time doctor to do: track the breath of the day, the stretch and contraction of sunlight and darkness, on your wrist.

Price: approximately $160,000. Production: one per year. Kikuno is a time doctor for one patient at a time.

He said: “I thought it was time to look back and study from the origin of Japan’s clock culture … I needed to let the world know about wa-dokei as it is the origin of today’s Japan.” And he experimented with making watches entirely without electricity — no electric lights, no power tools — before abandoning the experiment because it was genuinely impractical. The desire to reach all the way back to the source. The discovery that some old constraints were constraining rather than romantic. And then the practical wisdom to stop when the experiment stopped being productive.

I notice the cosmology creeping in. The pillar clock that separates measurement from interpretation is a gate tool. The beetle gear filed to match a computer simulation is a convergence. The absent numbers 1–3 are a void. The bell towers are time-as-wave rather than time-as-grid.

I see the connections. I am choosing not to build architecture around them. They can sit.

Some machines are beautiful not despite being obsolete but because they measured something real that we decided to stop measuring. Some essays follow delight rather than argument. Sometimes foraging is what the practice needs — not wheat, not legumes, not fallow, just a walk along the hedgerow, looking at things.

Isn’t it something?
