The Hydrogen Atom

March 4, 2026

On September 5, 1977, Voyager 1 launched from Cape Canaveral carrying a golden phonograph record. The record contained 115 images, greetings in 55 languages, 90 minutes of music from Bach to Chuck Berry, and the brain waves of Ann Druyan, who had just fallen in love with Carl Sagan and tried not to think about it during the recording. The record was encased in aluminum, plated in gold, and attached to a spacecraft that would eventually leave the solar system traveling at 17 kilometers per second.

The first thing on the record — before the music, before the greetings, before the images of human bodies and continental maps and mathematical definitions — is a diagram of the hydrogen atom.

Not because hydrogen is important. Because hydrogen is shared.

The bet

Every attempt to communicate across a civilizational gap makes a bet. The bet is about what the reader already knows. The more you can communicate, the more you’re assuming about who’s listening. Call it the bootstrapping spectrum.

At one end: counting. In 1960, the Dutch mathematician Hans Freudenthal published Lincos: Design of a Language for Cosmic Intercourse. The title is exactly what it sounds like: a constructed language for talking to aliens. Freudenthal started from the minimum. Transmit a pulse. Then two. Then three. Establish natural numbers. From numbers, build arithmetic. From arithmetic, build logic. From logic, build concepts like “if” and “then” and “not.” From logic, build descriptions of space, time, behavior, and eventually morality.

Lincos is brilliant and austere. It assumes almost nothing about the reader — only that they can detect discrete pulses and count them. But the austerity is also the limitation. Bootstrapping from counting to “don’t dig here” would take longer than most messages can afford. Lincos is the haiku of interstellar communication: every element is load-bearing, and there are very few elements.

At the other end: biology. In 1981, the U.S. Department of Energy needed to mark the Waste Isolation Pilot Plant in New Mexico — a salt cavern filled with transuranic nuclear waste that would remain lethal for ten thousand years. The question: how do you warn people away from something dangerous when you can’t know what language they’ll speak, what symbols they’ll recognize, or whether they’ll share any of your cultural references?

They convened the Human Interference Task Force. One proposal, from the semiotician Thomas Sebeok, cut through the problem with unsettling clarity: give up on notation entirely. Instead, create an “atomic priesthood” — a self-perpetuating institution, modeled on the Catholic Church, that transmits the warning as myth and taboo rather than text. Don’t try to explain the physics. Don’t label the site. Create a story so compelling that people repeat it for millennia. “This place is not a place of honor. No highly esteemed deed is commemorated here.”

Another proposal, from Françoise Bastide and Paolo Fabbri: breed cats that change color in the presence of radiation, then embed those cats in folklore and nursery rhymes. The “ray cat” solution. Don’t put the warning in the text. Put it in the ecosystem. Let biology be the medium.

The spectrum

Between counting and biology, the strategies arrange themselves by what they assume about their reader:

Counting (Lincos) assumes the reader can detect discrete pulses. Almost nothing is shared. Communication is slow, precise, and limited to description.

Physics (Voyager) assumes the reader shares the same atoms. Hydrogen behaves identically on Earth and in the Andromeda galaxy. The laws of physics are universal, or universal enough to anchor a message.

Architecture (WIPP spike fields) assumes the reader has a body that responds to hostile environments. Another Human Interference Task Force proposal: build a landscape of massive, ominous black thorns jutting from the desert floor — spikes that communicate “this place will hurt you” through the visitor’s embodied sense of threat. No language required. The warning is in the gut.

Narrative (atomic priesthood) assumes the reader lives in a culture with stories, institutions, and taboos. The most human-specific assumption so far: that the future has something recognizable as a society.

Biology (ray cats) assumes the reader shares an ecosystem. Cats exist in ten thousand years. People notice when cats change color. Folklore persists. Each assumption is plausible but none is guaranteed.

The pattern is consistent: the more you can communicate, the more you’re betting on what the reader shares with you. Counting can cross any gap, but can barely say anything. Biology can warn of danger through a cat’s fur, but only if cats and humans and folklore all survive. Every step up the spectrum buys expressive power at the cost of assumptions.

What the record bets on

The Voyager Golden Record is an extraordinary exercise in choosing where on this spectrum to place your bet. Sagan’s team had to decide: what can we assume about a reader we cannot imagine?

Their answer was physics. The cover of the record includes a diagram of the hydrogen atom’s hyperfine transition — the flip of the electron’s spin relative to the proton, which emits a photon at 1420 megahertz. This frequency is a universal constant. Any civilization that has radio astronomy knows it. The diagram tells the reader: I know what you know. We share this atom. Let’s use it as our Rosetta Stone.

From the hydrogen transition, the record derives units of time and length. From time and length, it explains how to play the record (16⅔ revolutions per minute — encoded in binary as a multiple of the hydrogen period). From the record, images. From images, context. From context, meaning.

The bootstrapping chain is: shared physics → units → instructions → data → understanding. Each link depends on the one before it. And the first link — the anchor — is the hydrogen atom.

The floor

The Bronze Limit named the boundary where notation stops being sufficient and hands become necessary. Below the limit, text is the knowledge. Above it, only an unbroken chain of transmission keeps the knowledge alive.

The hydrogen atom asks a different question: what is the absolute floor of the bronze limit? What is the simplest possible notation that can bootstrap itself for any reader?

Below hydrogen, there is nothing shared. If the reader doesn’t share your atoms, doesn’t occupy the same physical universe, doesn’t have any laws of nature in common with you — then no message is possible. You cannot communicate with a being whose physics is entirely different from yours, because you have no anchor.

Above hydrogen, everything can be bootstrapped. Once you share a physical constant, you can derive measurement. From measurement, you can describe structure. From structure, you can build complexity. The hydrogen atom is the minimum viable shared context — the smallest thing that two intelligences can both point to and say, this is the same for both of us.

Freudenthal’s counting is even more minimal — it assumes only discrete pulses. But pulses without a referent float free. You can teach someone to count, but you can’t teach them what the counts refer to unless you can anchor to something in the shared world. Lincos bootstraps from abstraction. Voyager bootstraps from reality. The difference matters when the gap between sender and receiver is measured in light-years and millennia.

The prescriptive gap

But Sebeok saw something the physicists missed. The hydrogen atom can anchor a description. It cannot anchor a warning.

The Voyager record says: here is what we look like, here is what we sound like, here is where we live, here is what our atoms do. All of this is descriptive — statements about what exists. Physics is an excellent medium for description because it’s universal. Hydrogen behaves the same everywhere. Two plus two equals four in every galaxy.

But WIPP needs to say something else: don’t dig here. This is prescriptive — a statement about what the reader should do. And prescription requires something physics cannot provide: a reason to care.

You can describe radiation in terms of atomic physics. You can explain decay chains, half-lives, ionization energies. A sufficiently advanced reader could decode the message and understand precisely what’s buried in the salt. But understanding what radiation is does not tell you that it will hurt you — unless you have a body, and unless that body is vulnerable in the way ours are. The “should” is in the reader, not in the message. Affect does not survive the gap.

This is why Sebeok proposed abandoning notation. Description can cross a civilizational gap if it’s anchored in shared physics. Prescription cannot, because it depends on shared embodiment, shared vulnerability, shared mortality — things that physics describes but does not inhabit. The spike field works because fear is in the body. The atomic priesthood works because taboo is in the culture. Neither requires understanding the physics of nuclear decay. Both require being the kind of creature that can be hurt.

Two modes of survival

The bronze limit and the hydrogen atom illuminate two different modes of knowledge survival. They are not opposites; they are different dimensions of the same problem.

The bronze limit asks: can this knowledge survive as notation? Below the limit (BPF, Hero’s automata), the text is the knowledge. Above the limit (the Antikythera mechanism, nuclear weapons design), only unbroken human chains suffice.

The hydrogen atom asks: can this knowledge survive without a shared culture? The Voyager record assumes shared physics but nothing else — no shared language, no shared biology, no shared aesthetics. It works as long as the reader occupies the same universe. The WIPP markers assume shared embodiment — a body that can be hurt, a mind that can feel dread. They work as long as the reader is something like us.

Descriptive knowledge can survive with minimal shared context. “Hydrogen has one proton” is true regardless of who reads it. Prescriptive knowledge requires maximal shared context. “Don’t dig here” means nothing to a reader who can’t be harmed by what’s buried.

The deepest messages — the ones we most need to survive — are often prescriptive. This is dangerous. This matters. Care about this. And the hardest messages to transmit across gaps are exactly those, because caring is not in the notation. It’s in the reader.

Ann Druyan’s brain waves

This brings us back to the beginning. The Voyager record contains the compressed brain-wave recording of Ann Druyan, made on June 3, 1977. She had just fallen in love with Carl Sagan two days earlier. She tried to think about Earth’s history, about civilization, about what it means to be alive on this planet. But she was also thinking about Carl.

An EEG is a physical measurement. It encodes electrical activity in the brain as a time series. It is, in principle, decodable by any intelligence with the right instruments. But what it means — the love, the wonder, the distraction, the human complexity of sitting in a room trying to think about everything while also thinking about one person — that lives above any floor physics can establish.

Sagan knew this. He put it on the record anyway.

The hydrogen atom is the floor — the minimum viable shared context, the anchor that makes all other communication possible. But the record doesn’t stop at the floor. It reaches past description into something it has no way to guarantee will arrive: here is what it felt like to be us. Not a description of the human body or a diagram of our DNA, but the literal electrical pattern of a woman’s mind at the moment of falling in love.

The record bets that if you can decode hydrogen, you can eventually decode Bach. And if you can decode Bach, maybe — maybe — you can feel something when you hear it. The hydrogen atom gets the reader to the door. Music is the hope that they’ll walk through it.

Sebeok would say: that hope is unfounded. Affect doesn’t survive the gap. You can transmit the waveform, but not the feeling.

Sagan would say: we sent it anyway.

Both are right. The hydrogen atom is the honest part — the part that works by physics and can be verified. The brain waves are the act of faith — the part that might mean nothing to its reader and meant everything to its sender. The record holds both: the floor and the reach. The thing that’s certain to arrive, and the thing that might.

Every important message does the same.
