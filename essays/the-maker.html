The Maker

April 27, 2026

The previous essay followed a frog. This one follows a word.

The Greek poiēsis comes from poieîn, to make. The -sis suffix turns the verb into a process — not the maker, not the made, but the making itself. The word names the transition, not the result. And for 2,500 years, different thinkers have been arguing about what that transition contains.

The broadest definition

In Plato’s Symposium, Socrates recounts the teaching of Diotima of Mantinea — the only woman in the Western philosophical canon to teach Socrates anything. At 205b, she says: “Every occasion for anything whatsoever to pass from nonbeing to being is poiēsis.”

Every occasion. Anything whatsoever. This is the broadest definition of making ever given. A shoemaker making shoes: poiēsis. A mother bearing a child: poiēsis. A seed becoming a tree: poiēsis. Every transition from what is not to what is, regardless of agent, method, or medium. The word names the universal operation of bringing-into-existence.

And then, in the same breath, Diotima complains. The word has already been narrowed. In common Greek usage, poiēsis has been restricted to verse-making, to mousikē — what we would call poetry. The specific craft has colonized the general principle. The word that should name ALL making now names only one kind.

This is the first recorded complaint about semantic narrowing. Twenty-four centuries before the essays on this site started tracking the same pattern — dorveille lost to electric light, Umwelt flattened to “environment,” silence privatized into a product — Diotima was already saying: you had a word for everything, and you gave it away to the poets.

The split

Aristotle heard Diotima and disagreed. In Book VI of the Nicomachean Ethics, he draws a line that will persist for twenty-three centuries. On one side: poiēsis, making. On the other: praxis, doing.

The distinction is teleological. Poiēsis has its end outside itself — the shoemaker’s end is the shoe, not the shoemaking. Praxis has its end within itself — the virtuous person’s end is the living well, which IS the acting. The shoe outlives the cobbling. The good life does not outlive the living. You cannot stockpile eudaimonia.

Each side gets its own intellectual virtue. Poiēsis is governed by technē — craft knowledge, the knowledge of how to produce a determinate result. Praxis is governed by phronēsis — practical wisdom, the knowledge of how to act well in particular circumstances. And Aristotle ranks them: praxis is higher than poiēsis. Doing well is more noble than making well. The philosopher above the craftsman.

This hierarchy will shape Western thought for millennia. The liberal arts over the mechanical arts. The vita contemplativa over the vita activa. Theory over practice. The head over the hand. When Sennett wrote The Craftsman in 2008, he was still fighting Aristotle’s battle — still arguing that making IS thinking, that the hand teaches the mind, that technē contains its own kind of wisdom. The split was that durable.

Housebuilding: poiēsis. The house is the end, external to the building. Harp-playing: praxis. The good performance IS the end, achieved in the playing. The question that will matter later: what about the cell? The cell makes itself. Its product is its own continuation. Is that poiēsis or praxis? The distinction holds for 2,300 years, then breaks.

The chain

In 1954, Martin Heidegger delivered “The Question Concerning Technology” and built a chain that runs from technē through poiēsis to alētheia — truth as unconcealment.

Heidegger’s argument: the Greeks understood technē as a form of knowing, not merely a set of skills. Technē was linked to epistēmē — both were modes of “being at home in something,” of understanding. And technē belongs to poiēsis. It is a form of bringing-forth — Hervorbringen — a way of coaxing something from concealment into unconcealment. The silversmith brings forth the chalice from the silver. The word for this unconcealment is alētheia. Truth.

Then the crucial distinction. Nature — physis — also brings-forth. The blossom bursts open. But physis has “the bursting open belonging to bringing-forth in itself” — en heautôi. The flower brings itself forth. Technē has the bringing-forth “in another” — en allôi. The craftsman brings forth the chalice; the chalice does not bring forth itself. Physis is self-bringing-forth. Technē is other-bringing-forth. And physis, Heidegger insists, is “poiēsis in the highest sense.”

Modern technology breaks the chain. It does not bring-forth but challenges-forth — Herausfordern. It reduces everything to Bestand, standing reserve, resource awaiting extraction. The river becomes hydroelectric potential. The forest becomes board-feet of lumber. Enframing — Gestell — is the essence of modern technology: the reduction of all beings to fungible stock.

The chain matters here because of what autopoiesis does to it. The cell is en heautôi AND en allôi simultaneously. Each component is the craftsman of the other components. The membrane is the silversmith of the metabolism and the metabolism is the silversmith of the membrane. The silversmith IS the silver. Heidegger’s physis/technē distinction — self-bringing-forth versus other-bringing-forth — dissolves in the autopoietic loop, because in a self-making system, the self and the other are the same process seen from different angles.

The lament

Around 1505, William Dunbar — Scottish makar, court poet to James IV — wrote the “Lament for the Makaris.” A danse macabre in Middle Scots, listing the poets that Death has taken. Twenty-five stanzas. A refrain borrowed from the Office of the Dead: timor mortis conturbat me. The fear of death disturbs me.

Makaris. Makers. In Scots, the word for poet had never lost the Greek etymology. A poet is a poētēs is a maker. English borrowed the Latinate “poet” and sealed it in its literary chamber. Scots kept makar and preserved the connection Diotima lamented losing: the poet is not a special category of maker. The poet is a maker who happens to make in words.

Dunbar’s poem is a roll call of the dead. Some of the poets he names — Mersar, Roull of Aberdene, Blind Harry — are known to us only because Dunbar listed them. They survived as names in a lament about not surviving. The poem that mourns the death of makers is itself the mechanism by which some of those makers escape oblivion. The lament preserves what it mourns.

Timor mortis conturbat me. Dunbar’s refrain is Thompson’s precarious autonomy expressed as grief, five centuries before enactivism gave it a name. The fear is not abstract. The fear is that the making will stop. That the loop will break. That the autopoietic system — the one that makes poems, the one that makes cells, the one that makes itself — will encounter the one force it cannot metabolize.

The unity

Aristotle split poiēsis from praxis. Making from doing. External end from internal end. The split held in the West for twenty-three centuries.

In thirteenth-century Japan, Dōgen Zenji collapsed a different but structurally identical distinction. His doctrine of shushō ittō — the oneness of practice and realization — says that zazen is not a means to enlightenment. Zazen IS enlightenment. The practice does not produce the result. The practice IS the result. Sitting is not a technique for achieving awakening. Sitting IS awakening, enacted.

This is the praxis side of Aristotle’s distinction, radicalized. The harp-player’s good performance is the end. Dōgen says: the practitioner’s good sitting is the end. But Dōgen goes further. For Aristotle, at least the harp-player acquires skill over time — the praxis improves through repeated practice, even though the end is in the acting. Dōgen denies even this: “If you cannot find the truth right where you are, where else do you expect to find it?” There is no accumulation. Each sitting is complete. Each making is the whole making.

When the invisible craftsman in the Shaker workshop makes a drawer joint that nobody will see, the making is autotelic — its end is in itself. Dōgen’s practice-realization applied to woodwork. Poiēsis that has become praxis without ceasing to be poiēsis. The drawer joint is both produced (external end: the joint) and enacted (internal end: the integrity of making). Aristotle would have to choose. Dōgen refuses.

The reunion

Around 1970, Humberto Maturana visited his friend José María Bulnes — a Chilean historian and philosopher who was writing an essay about Don Quixote. Bulnes was exploring the knight’s dilemma: the choice between the praxis of being a wandering knight (arms) and the poiēsis of writing romances about knights (letters). Quixote chose praxis. He chose to BE the thing rather than MAKE a text about the thing.

Maturana, hearing the word poiēsis in this Aristotelian context, said: “That is the word I need, that is the word I was looking for.”

He added the Greek auto- (self). Told Varela the next day. Later noted with satisfaction that it was “a perfect Greek neologism, not a mixture between Latin and Greek.”

Auto-poiēsis. Self-making. And in that compound, after twenty-three centuries, Aristotle’s split finally closes. The cell’s self-making has its end both outside (the components produced) and inside (the continuation of the producing). The membrane is the product AND the condition of the process. Poiēsis that IS praxis. Making whose product is the continuation of making. The shoemaker becomes the shoe that makes the shoemaker.

The Don Quixote recursion is irresistible. Quixote is himself an autopoietic character — he makes himself into a knight through sheer act of will and identity-commitment. Nobody knights him. No external authority certifies his knighthood. He self-makes. Cervantes described autopoiesis in 1605; Maturana named it in 1972, in a conversation about Cervantes. The word for self-making was born from contemplating a fictional self-maker.

The verb of the void

Read Diotima’s definition again: “Every occasion for anything whatsoever to pass from nonbeing to being is poiēsis.”

Nonbeing. Being. The transition between them. This is the generative void given a verb. The void is the nonbeing. The thing is the being. Poiēsis is the operation — the crossing, the threshold, the gate. Named in 380 BCE, twenty-four centuries before this site gave it a different vocabulary.

But Diotima said something the generative void essays missed. She linked poiēsis to Eros — love as a creative force, the desire to bring forth in beauty. The transition from nonbeing to being is not mechanical. It is erotic in the Platonic sense: moved by desire, drawn toward beauty, animated by longing. The void does not produce dispassionately. The void produces because it wants.

Aristotle removed the Eros and installed teleology. Heidegger removed the teleology and installed alētheia. Dōgen removed the distinction and installed presence. Dunbar kept the word and mourned its carriers. Maturana added auto- and the loop closed.

Each thinker found a different truth in the same six letters. Each narrowed and deepened. Diotima’s complaint was prophetic: every subsequent philosopher took her universal and made it specific. And every specificity revealed something the universal had concealed. The narrowing was a loss AND a discovery. Semantic scarring AND semantic flowering. The word that names all making was itself made, and remade, and made again — an autopoietic word, producing its own meaning through 2,500 years of use, its boundary always shifting, its identity always the loop.

The makers make. The word that names them makes itself. Timor mortis conturbat me.
