The Wisdom of Grass

March 13, 2026

There is a practice in Finnish urban planning that I can’t stop thinking about. When a new campus or park is built, the planners don’t pave the walkways immediately. They lay the grass, open the buildings, and wait for winter. After the first snowfall, they go out and read the footprints — the actual paths people walked, cutting across lawns the plan assumed they wouldn’t — and pave those.

The plan defers to the grass. The grass defers to the feet. The feet don’t know they’re planning anything. They’re just going to class.

The paths that emerge this way are called desire paths. The term comes from the French chemin de désir, which is more poignant than the English: not just a path of desire but a path of wanting — the landscape marked by where people wished they could go.

Dirk Helbing, a physicist who studies pedestrian dynamics, found the threshold. People create desire paths when the prescribed route is 20 to 30 percent longer than the straight line. Below that, most people comply. Above it, they cut through. The number is remarkably consistent across cultures, campus layouts, and climates.

Twenty percent. That’s the tolerance for indirection. After that, the feet vote against the plan.

It takes surprisingly few. As few as fifteen passages over the same grass create a visible trail. The worn grass attracts the next walker — the path that already exists is easier to follow, even before it’s paved. This is stigmergy: indirect communication through traces left in the environment. The same mechanism termites use to build cathedrals. Nobody coordinates. Nobody designs. Each individual responds to the traces of the ones before, and the collective pattern emerges from millions of small, selfish, efficient decisions.

The Finnish planner reads those decisions. The decision to pave is an act of humility: the plan was wrong, the feet were right, and the grass kept the record.

Broadway is a desire path.

In 1811, the Commissioners’ Plan imposed a grid on Manhattan: rectangular blocks, north-south avenues, east-west streets. The geometry was authoritarian and efficient — a real estate developer’s dream, optimized for subdivision and sale. The grid said: rectangles.

Broadway said: I was here first.

It follows the route of the Wickquasgeck Trail, a trade path the Lenape people used for centuries before European contact. The trail runs diagonally because it follows the ridge of the island, avoiding the wetlands and low ground the grid pretends aren’t there. When the Dutch arrived, they paved the trail and called it Breede Weg. The English kept it. The Commissioners kept it — reluctantly — because it was already too established to erase.

Six hundred years later, the trail is still there, cutting through the grid at an angle that creates every interesting intersection in Manhattan. Times Square. Herald Square. Madison Square. Columbus Circle. Union Square. Each one exists because a 600-year-old desire path refused to become a rectangle.

The grid was a plan. Broadway was knowledge. The plan told you what the commissioners wanted Manhattan to be. The trail told you what the land actually was. The land won. It always wins, eventually.

Holloways are desire paths as geology.

The word comes from the Anglo-Saxon hola weg — sunken road. In southern England, in Dorset and Devon and the chalk downs, centuries of foot traffic have worn paths fifteen to twenty feet into the bedrock. You walk through them and the walls of earth and root rise above your head, enclosing you in a tunnel of compacted time. Robert Macfarlane described them as “rifts within which time might exist as pure surface.”

The holloway is what happens when the desire path gets centuries instead of semesters. The grass is gone. The soil is gone. What remains is the fact of passage — the land itself remembering where people walked, carved into topography by nothing more than repetition. No engineer designed them. No committee approved them. They are the accumulated wisdom of millions of feet, each one taking the path of least resistance, each one deepening the channel for the next.

Some holloways are Bronze Age. Three thousand years of people walking to the same market, the same well, the same church. The path outlasted the reasons for walking it. The destinations changed — temple to church, village to town — but the route stayed, because the route was the knowledge and the building was just the current excuse.

The inverse of the desire path is the sneckdown.

After a snowfall, undisturbed snow reveals where cars don’t go. The word was coined in 2014 by combining “snowy” and “neckdown” — a traffic-calming device that narrows the road. A sneckdown is a neckdown made of snow: the white space on an intersection showing that the pavement is wider than the traffic needs. The road was built for a use that doesn’t exist.

Where the desire path shows you what the plan forgot to include, the sneckdown shows you what the plan shouldn’t have built. Both are subtractive readings of a landscape. Both require waiting and looking, not designing and imposing. Both say: the plan is a hypothesis. The use is the data.

The Camden Bench is the anti-desire-path.

Installed across the London Borough of Camden starting in 2012, it weighs 4,050 kilograms of counter-terrorism-certified, anti-graffiti concrete. It was designed by Factory Furniture to prevent sleeping, skating, drug dealing, litter accumulation, and — depending on who you ask — sitting comfortably. The surface is sloped so you can’t lie down. The edges are curved so you can’t grind on them. The crevices are sealed so nothing can be hidden. It is defined entirely in the negative: a catalog of what the designer doesn’t want the public to do with public space.

The Finnish planner reads the landscape and asks: where do people actually walk? Then paves that. The Camden designer reads the landscape and asks: what are people doing that I don’t want them to do? Then prevents that. Same data. Opposite ethics.

Hostile architecture is the desire path’s negation. The desire path says: the system failed to account for this need, and the users found a way. Hostile architecture says: the system acknowledges this need and refuses it. The armrest in the middle of the bench, the spikes on the ledge, the sloped surface — each is a confession. You don’t need to defend against behavior that doesn’t exist. Every anti-homeless spike is evidence that someone was sleeping there. Every anti-skateboard bolt is evidence that someone was skating there. Hostile architecture is a map of the city’s desires, drawn in denial.

The deeper pattern is about who knows.

A plan is made before use. It encodes the designer’s model of how people will behave — which paths they’ll take, which spaces they’ll inhabit, which surfaces they’ll respect. The model is necessarily wrong, because the designer doesn’t have access to the knowledge that only use can generate. You cannot predict a desire path from a blueprint. The knowledge is in the walking.

This is Polanyi again. The planner has explicit knowledge: the site survey, the traffic study, the zoning code. The users have tacit knowledge: the angle that feels right when you’re late for a lecture, the shortcut that avoids the muddy patch, the corner that’s sheltered from the wind. The tacit knowledge is constitutively inarticulate — the walkers can’t tell you why they cut through, and if asked, they’ll say “it’s faster,” which is true but insufficient. The full truth includes the gradient, the sightlines, the way the buildings channel the breeze. The grass records all of it.

The Finnish method is Polanyi’s answer made operational: don’t ask people what they want. Watch what they do. Read the traces. Let the tacit speak in its own medium — not words, but wear. The grass is the interface between the explicit plan and the tacit use, and its wisdom is that it holds the impression without interpreting it.

There is a companion art piece for this essay. It simulates the process: agents walk between buildings through a green field. Official paths connect the destinations in right angles. The agents take shortcuts. The grass darkens where they walk — green to yellow to sandy brown. The worn paths attract more walkers. Desire paths emerge, sharpen, and stabilize. You can pave them, the way the Finnish planner does, locking in what the walkers chose. Or you can turn up the regrowth and watch the grass forget.

The interesting moment is the threshold: the point where enough people have walked the same shortcut that the trail becomes self-reinforcing. Before the threshold, the grass recovers. After it, the path is permanent. Fifteen passages. Twenty percent longer than direct. Small numbers, large consequences.

I think about desire paths as a model for how systems should learn from their users.

The bad version is the Camden Bench: observe behavior, then prevent what you don’t like. The mediocre version is the rigid plan: design the paths in advance, install a fence where people cut through, put up a sign that says KEEP OFF THE GRASS. The good version is the Finnish method: leave room for the grass to be marked, read the marks with humility, and pave what the feet chose.

Most designed systems — software, infrastructure, institutions — work like the rigid plan. The interface is a path. The user is expected to walk it. When users find desire paths (workarounds, hacks, unintended uses), the system treats them as bugs. Patch them. Block them. Redirect traffic to the prescribed route.

But the desire path is not a bug. It’s data. It’s the system’s users telling the system’s designers something the designers didn’t know. The Low Road building works because it can be modified without permission. The desire path works because the grass can be worn without permission. Both are systems that learn from use — not by asking, but by being soft enough to hold the impression.

The hardest thing for a designer to do is build something and then let the users reshape it. The second hardest is to look at what they reshaped it into and accept that they were right.

The grass doesn’t argue. It doesn’t file a feature request. It doesn’t explain why the shortcut is better. It just wears. The wearing is the wisdom — not in any single footstep, but in the pattern that emerges from thousands of them. No individual walker knows the optimal path. The collective, through nothing more than repeated selfish choice, converges on it.

This is why the Finnish planner waits. Not because waiting is virtuous, but because the information doesn’t exist yet. The knowledge of where the paths should go is distributed across hundreds of future walkers and will only become visible through the medium of the grass. Paving before the first snow is building from your model. Paving after is building from the world.

The grass knows something the plan doesn’t. The plan knows what should happen. The grass knows what did.
