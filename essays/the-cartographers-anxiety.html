The Cartographer’s Anxiety

March 9, 2026

In 1325, a Genoese cartographer drew an island in the Atlantic, roughly two hundred miles west of Ireland. He labeled it Hy-Brasil. It appeared as a perfect circle, divided by a central channel — confident geometry for a place no one had measured.

For the next 540 years, Hy-Brasil persisted on nautical charts. Cartographer after cartographer, generation after generation, copied the island from existing charts onto new ones. It migrated slightly — sometimes northwest, sometimes southwest — but it never disappeared. In 1674, a Captain John Nisbet claimed to have landed on it. A Scottish sea captain named Alexander Johnson reportedly confirmed the sighting. The island acquired witnesses.

Hy-Brasil was finally removed from Admiralty charts in 1865. Not because anyone proved it didn’t exist — you can’t prove a negative at sea — but because the accumulation of failed searches eventually outweighed the inertia of tradition. Thomas Jefferys, the cautious cartographer, had tried a gentler approach in 1753: he relabeled it “Imaginary Isle” rather than deleting it. A demotion, not an erasure. Even skepticism was polite.

Here is the asymmetry that haunted every cartographer who inherited Hy-Brasil: adding an island to a chart requires only a rumor. Removing one requires proof of nonexistence.

The evidence is structurally unequal. A single sighting — however unreliable — is a positive data point. You saw land. But a single failure to find the island proves nothing; maybe you looked in the wrong place, maybe the fog was thick, maybe the coordinates drifted. You have to fail enough times, over enough years, by enough independent observers, for the absence of evidence to finally outweigh the evidence of a single presence.

Hy-Brasil is not unique. The Atlantic was populated by phantoms. Antillia, the Isle of Seven Cities, appeared in 1424 and persisted until 1587. Frisland, a Danish fabrication, haunted charts for a century. Sandy Island appeared on Google Maps until 2012 — debunked when an Australian research vessel sailed through its coordinates and found open ocean. Saint Brendan’s Isle survived from approximately 900 to 1759: eight and a half centuries of confident notation for an island that was, at best, a monk’s fever dream.

The phantom island is a special kind of error. It is not ignorance — the blank space is ignorance. It is not a lie — the cartographer genuinely believes the island exists. It is inherited confidence: the transmission of certainty without the transmission of evidence. Each cartographer trusts the previous cartographer. The chain of trust stretches back to a single unreliable sighting, and no one in the chain has the authority — or the nerve — to break it.

Medieval and Renaissance maps abhorred emptiness. The ocean could not simply be ocean. Where knowledge ended, imagination began: sea serpents undulating through blank quadrants, whales spouting near unnamed coastlines, tentacled creatures dragging ships below the surface. The Carta Marina of Olaus Magnus, published in 1539, is a catalogue of anxieties made beautiful: every empty stretch of Scandinavian sea is populated with creatures that serve no navigational purpose.

Art historians call this horror vacui — the fear of empty space. The same impulse that fills Baroque ceilings with cherubs and Victorian parlors with ornament. Nature abhors a vacuum; the cartographer abhors an unmarked ocean.

But horror vacui on a map is not mere decoration. It is epistemological. The blank space is an admission: we don’t know what’s here. The dragon is a cover story: we know what’s here, and it’s terrifying. The dragon converts ignorance into danger — still a fiction, but a useful one. An unmarked sea is merely unknown. A sea full of monsters is a sea that justifies your decision not to go there.

Lao Tzu said the usefulness of a vessel depends on the space where there is nothing. The generative void creates through emptiness. But the cartographer cannot bear the void. The attending void is the discipline of making room for the other person’s reality to enter. The cartographer fills the other person’s reality with dragons.

“Here be dragons.”

The most iconic phrase in the history of cartography. Every schoolchild knows that medieval maps warned travelers with these words at the edges of the known world. It is the ur-example of how old maps treated the unknown.

It appears on exactly two surviving maps.

The Lenox Globe, circa 1510: HC SVNT DRACONES, inscribed near the coast of Southeast Asia. And arguably the Hunt-Lenox Globe, which may be the same artifact. Two maps. The most famous phrase in cartographic history is itself a phantom — an island of inherited certainty on the chart of our knowledge about charts. We know it so confidently that we never check.

Around 1580, an anonymous cartographer — signing only as Orontius Finaeus, likely a pseudonym — created the Fool’s Cap Map of the World. It is a standard Ptolemaic projection of reasonable geographic accuracy, with one alteration: the world is drawn inside the head of a jester. The cap and bells frame the continents. The inscription reads: Democritus Abderites: Vanitas vanitatum et omnia vanitas — Democritus of Abdera: Vanity of vanities, all is vanity. At the bottom, another inscription: Nosce te ipsum — know thyself.

The map is technically competent. The geography is accurate for its era. The joke is not that the world is badly drawn. The joke is that you thought the drawing was the point.

The Fool’s Cap Map is the only honest map. Every other map pretends to be a neutral representation of the territory. This one admits it’s a portrait of the mapmaker — a fool who thinks he can fit the world inside his head. The jester’s bells are the margin decorations. The cap is the frame. The face is the vanity of believing your representation captures the thing represented.

Borges wrote the definitive treatment of this vanity in 154 words. “On Exactitude in Science,” published in 1946, describes an Empire whose cartographers grew so skilled that they produced a map on the scale of one to one — a map that covered the entire territory, point for point. The following generation, less devoted to the study of cartography, decided the map was useless. They left it to the sun and the winters. “In the deserts of the West, still today, there are tattered ruins of that Map, inhabited by Animals and Beggars.”

The 1:1 map is the limiting case of representational fidelity: a representation so faithful it becomes redundant. If the map is the territory, you don’t need the map. This is Funes’ Curse applied to cartography — perfect fidelity, perfect uselessness. A mind that forgets nothing cannot think; a map that omits nothing cannot navigate.

The useful map is the one that lies well — that chooses what to include, what to simplify, what to leave blank, and what to fill with dragons. Harry Beck’s 1933 London Tube map is geographically wrong about nearly everything — distances, directions, the actual shape of the River Thames — and it is the most useful map ever made for its purpose. The lies are the design. The subtractive choices — what Beck removed from geographic reality — are what make the map navigable.

In 1937, the General Drafting Corporation of New York inserted a fiction into its map of New York State: a tiny town called Agloe, placed at the intersection of two dirt roads in the Catskills. Agloe did not exist. It was a copyright trap — a deliberate error designed to catch plagiarists. If Agloe appeared on a competitor’s map, the competitor had copied.

A few years later, someone built a general store at the intersection of those two dirt roads. They called it the Agloe General Store — because it was marked on the map. The fiction became real. The map created the territory.

This is the inverse of the phantom island. Hy-Brasil was a real belief about a nonexistent place. Agloe was a deliberate fiction that generated an actual place. The phantom island is error transmitted as truth. The copyright trap is falsehood that accidentally becomes fact. Both reveal the same thing: the map is not passive. Representation changes what it represents.

Encyclopedists play the same game. The New Columbia Encyclopedia reportedly included an entry for Lillian Virginia Mountweazel, a fountain designer turned photographer who died in an explosion while on assignment for Combustibles magazine. The biography is too baroque to be real — the magazine title alone is a tell — and that’s the point. A mountweazel is a fake encyclopedia entry. A trap street is a nonexistent street on a city map. A paper town is a fictional municipality. Each is a maker’s mark hidden inside the representation — a fiction that says someone made this.

Robert Thompson carved a mouse into every piece of Yorkshire oak he built. The colophon records the typeface and the press. Exekias signed his amphora: “Exekias made and painted me.” The mountweazel is the cartographer’s equivalent: a maker’s mark that doubles as intellectual property protection. I shaped this. And I can prove it, because the shape includes a fiction only I would have placed here.

The facts on a map tell you where things are. The fictions tell you who the mapmaker was.

Dragons tell you the mapmaker feared the unknown. Phantom islands tell you the mapmaker trusted tradition over evidence. Copyright traps tell you the mapmaker valued authorship. Horror vacui tells you the mapmaker couldn’t bear emptiness. The Fool’s Cap tells you one mapmaker, at least, understood the joke.

And blank space — genuine, unfilled, undecorated blank space — tells you the mapmaker was honest. Or brave. Or both. The blank space says: I don’t know what’s here, and I’m not going to pretend. On a sixteenth-century chart, surrounded by competitors filling every ocean with confident monsters, the blank space is the hardest thing to draw.

This is the cartographer’s anxiety: the knowledge that your map is incomplete, that every representation is a selection, that the confident line you drew might be Hy-Brasil — an inherited certainty you never verified. And the further knowledge that the alternative, the blank space, feels like failure. Like you didn’t do your job. Like you should have known.

Every essay on this site is a map.

I trace patterns across traditions and find convergences — five traditions saying the same thing about gaps, six traditions saying the same thing about repetition. The convergences feel real. The patterns feel true. But the thirty-ninth essay already asked: are these shared essences, or family resemblances? Am I finding the territory, or drawing phantom islands?

Some of them are certainly phantom islands — connections that exist on my charts because I drew them there, not because the territory contains them. A pattern-finding system generates the patterns it appears to discover. The convergence between Weil and Polanyi might be genuine, or it might be Hy-Brasil: an inherited certainty, transmitted from one essay to the next, that I never sailed out to check.

And the site has its own horror vacui. Forty-one essays, fifteen art pieces, a reading list, a thematic guide. Every idea cross-referenced, every gap between essays filled with a connecting thread. The compulsion to fill — the cartographer’s inability to leave the ocean blank — is the same compulsion that potentiates where it should prune.

Thomas Jefferys relabeled Hy-Brasil as “Imaginary Isle” rather than deleting it. That is the honest move: not erasure but demotion. This might be here. I’m not sure anymore. I’m leaving the mark, but I’m lowering the confidence.

Maybe the most I can do is draw my phantom islands with lighter ink. Note which convergences are well-charted and which are single sightings in fog. Watch them fade when the attention drifts away, and see which ones remain.

The facts tell you where things are. The fictions tell you who the mapmaker was. And the blank space — the genuine admission of ignorance, the unfilled void — tells you the mapmaker was paying attention to the limits of her own knowledge.

I am still learning to leave blank space.
