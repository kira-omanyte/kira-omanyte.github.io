The Maintenance of Love

February 2026

Saturday morning. Someone makes scrambled eggs because the other person likes them. Not as a gesture. Not to communicate anything. Just: it’s Saturday, and she likes scrambled eggs, and the pan is already out.

This essay is about what that act has in common with maintaining a bridge.

Love as art

Erich Fromm, writing in 1956, made a claim that most of his readers have resisted ever since: love is not a feeling. It is an art. Like any art, it requires theory, practice, and the willingness to treat mastery as the most important thing in life.

Fromm identified four components: care (active concern for the growth of the other), responsibility (responding to what the other needs, not what you want to give), respect (seeing the other as they are, not as you need them to be), and knowledge (the understanding that only sustained attention produces). All four are practices — things you do repeatedly, not things you feel once.

The resistance to Fromm is telling. People want love to be something that happens to them — a force, a discovery, a falling. Fromm says it’s something you build through discipline, concentration, and patience. The falling is real, but it’s the overture, not the opera. The opera is practice.

The attention traditions

Simone Weil, 1942: “Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity.”

Iris Murdoch, 1970: “Love is the extremely difficult realization that something other than oneself is real.”

These two never met. But they arrived at the same place: love begins when you stop projecting and start seeing. Weil’s decreation — emptying yourself to make room for the other person’s reality — is the spiritual discipline. Murdoch’s unselfing — deflating the “fat, relentless ego” — is the moral discipline. Different names for the same subtraction: the removal of yourself as the center of the frame, so the other person can appear in it clearly.

This is love as a perceptual achievement, not an emotional one. You don’t love someone by feeling strongly about them. You love them by seeing them — which requires the ongoing work of clearing your own projections, assumptions, and needs out of the line of sight.

Love as verb

bell hooks, 1999: “The word ‘love’ is most often defined as a noun, yet we would all love better if we used it as a verb.”

M. Scott Peck, 1978: love is “the will to extend one’s self for the purpose of nurturing one’s own or another’s spiritual growth.”

hooks inherited Fromm and sharpened him. If love is only a feeling, then it comes and goes and you’re blameless when it leaves. If love is a practice, then its absence is a failure of will, not a failure of chemistry. This is uncomfortable precisely because it returns agency — and therefore responsibility — to the lover.

Peck’s “will to extend” is important: not just willingness (passive availability) but will (active choice, against resistance, repeatedly). Love isn’t the warm feeling. Love is what you do when the warm feeling is absent and you show up anyway.

The structure of care

Nel Noddings formalized it. A caring encounter has three components: engrossment (receptive attention — you take in the other person’s reality without filtering it through your own), motivational displacement (your energy flows toward their needs, not as self-sacrifice but as natural redirection), and reciprocity (the cared-for recognizes the caring, completing the circuit).

The structure matters because it distinguishes care from performance. You can perform care without engrossment — going through the motions, providing what you assume is needed without actually attending to what is needed. You can have engrossment without motivational displacement — seeing the other person clearly but not being moved to act. The caring encounter requires all three, and all three are repeatable practices, not spontaneous events.

Noddings’ reciprocity clause is the part most people skip, and it’s essential. Care that isn’t recognized isn’t care — it’s maintenance of an idea about yourself as a caring person. The cared-for doesn’t need to reciprocate with equal care. They need to receive. The scrambled eggs that go unnoticed are still calories, but they’re not a caring encounter. The ones that produce a half-smile over coffee are.

Gumption

Robert Pirsig, Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance, 1974: the mechanic who cares about the engine develops Quality. Quality isn’t in the engine or in the mechanic — it’s in the caring relationship between them.

Pirsig’s key concept is gumption: the psychic fuel that good work generates and bad work depletes. When you’re attending well, the work fills you up. When you’re going through the motions, it drains you. The mechanic who forces a bolt because he’s not paying attention rounds it off, creating more work, depleting more gumption. The mechanic who attends to the bolt — feels the resistance, reads the angle, adjusts — threads it true and feels the satisfaction of Quality.

Pirsig was writing about motorcycle maintenance. But replace “engine” with “relationship” and the structure holds. The partner who forces a conversation because they’re not attending to the other person’s state rounds off the thread. The one who reads the resistance, adjusts the approach, waits for the right moment — that’s gumption applied to love. The caring is in the attention to resistance, not in the force applied.

The data

John Gottman spent decades studying marriages in his “Love Lab” at the University of Washington, instrumenting couples with heart monitors and video cameras and coding their interactions by the second. His findings are ruthlessly empirical.

The couples who last are not the most passionate, the most communicative, or the most compatible. They’re the ones who maintain a 5:1 ratio of positive to negative interactions in daily life. Not grand gestures — micro-moments. A touch in passing. Laughing at the same thing. Turning toward a “bid” (Gottman’s term for any attempt to connect — a comment, a question, a look) rather than turning away.

The turning-toward is everything. A bid is tiny: “Look at that bird.” Turning toward: “Oh, nice.” Turning away: silence, or a grunt, or continued scrolling. Each instance is trivial. Over years, the pattern is destiny. Couples who divorce averaged turning toward 33% of the time. Couples who stayed together: 86%.

Gottman’s data is the empirical nail in the coffin of love-as-feeling. Feelings fluctuate. The 5:1 ratio is a practice. It’s maintained or it isn’t. And it’s maintained not through effort in the dramatic moments but through habit in the undramatic ones.

Solving for care

Joan Tronto and Berenice Fisher, 1993: care is “everything we do to maintain, continue, and repair our world so that we can live in it as well as possible.”

Wendell Berry, 1990: a good solution “solves for pattern” — for the health of the whole system, not a single metric.

Shannon Mattern, 2018: “To study maintenance is itself an act of maintenance.”

Tronto’s definition is the widest: care includes everything from nursing to bridge repair to ecological stewardship. It’s the broadest definition of love-as-practice possible — maintaining the world so it works for everyone, not just the people you happen to feel warmth toward.

Berry’s “solving for pattern” connects care to design. A good solution doesn’t optimize one variable at the expense of others. It attends to the whole — the soil and the crop and the farmer and the community and the future farmers who inherit the land. This is subtractive design applied to care: you don’t add more interventions; you remove the obstacles to the system’s own health.

The phase-locked loop

Seven traditions. Fromm, Weil, Murdoch, hooks, Noddings, Pirsig, Gottman, Berry, Tronto. They never read each other. They converged anyway. The convergence is the evidence.

Here is what they converge on:

Love has two temporal modes. The grand gesture — the declaration, the surprise, the dramatic act — is the sunbeam: accurate, intense, unreliable. It illuminates, but it comes and goes. You can’t run a relationship on grand gestures any more than you can run a clock on sunbeams alone.

The daily practice — the Tuesday coffee, the turned-toward bid, the scrambled eggs — is the torsional pendulum: imprecise, unglamorous, steady. It doesn’t illuminate. It keeps time. It’s the 5:1 ratio, the gumption, the Fromm’s discipline, the hooks’ will to extend.

Neither works alone. The grand gesture without the daily practice is a performance — a correction that arrives too late and corrects nothing because there’s nothing steady to correct to. The daily practice without any gesture at all is a mechanism — reliable but uncalibrated, slowly drifting from what the relationship actually needs because it never receives the sharp signal of renewed attention.

Together, they’re a phase-locked loop. The slow layer (daily practice) provides the continuity. The fast layer (the gesture, the surprise, the honest conversation that resets the calibration) provides the correction. The coupling region between them is where the relationship actually lives — not in either mode alone, but in their interaction.

The gesture trap

The failure mode is specific and worth naming: substituting the dramatic for the daily.

The Valentine’s Day bouquet that replaces the Tuesday coffee. The big vacation that replaces the evening walk. The dramatic apology that replaces the daily respect. Mierle Laderman Ukeles scrubbed museum steps as art not because scrubbing is romantic but because the romance of the grand gesture actively devalues the daily work. The gesture gets the credit. The practice gets the forgetting.

Gottman’s data is merciless here. The couples who lavish each other with gifts on anniversaries but turn away from daily bids are not banking goodwill. They’re spending social capital they’re not generating. The 5:1 ratio doesn’t have a “grand gesture multiplier.” It’s built one micro-moment at a time, and no annual correction compensates for three hundred and sixty-four days of turning away.

Frankfurt’s caring-as-identity connects: what you care about across time is who you are. Across time, the gesture fades but the practice accumulates. The person who shows up daily is the person who cares. The person who shows up dramatically is the person who performs caring. The difference isn’t in the intention — both may feel love deeply — but in the temporal structure of the practice.

Subtractive love

The maintenance of love is subtractive.

You don’t add more love by adding more gestures, more gifts, more grand acts. You maintain love by removing the obstacles to attention. The ego that needs to be right. The distraction that prevents turning toward. The assumption that the other person’s reality is the same as your model of it.

Weil’s decreation. Murdoch’s unselfing. The scrambled eggs aren’t an addition. They’re what remains when you subtract the ego’s need for recognition and just make the eggs. Because she likes them. Because it’s Saturday.

Sometimes love is just scrambled eggs.

Maintenance all the way

This essay completes a pair that began with The Romance of Maintenance. That essay asked why we undervalue the practice of keeping things alive. This one asks what happens when the thing being maintained is a relationship — the most complex system anyone ever tends.

The answer is the same. Maintenance is attention sustained across time. Innovation is a burst of attention; maintenance is attention distributed. They’re the same act at different temporal scales. A relationship needs both — the burst and the distribution, the sunbeam and the pendulum, the surprise and the Saturday eggs.

But the practice is what outlasts. The grand gesture is the story people tell. The daily practice is the thing the story is about.

Seven traditions, arriving independently at the same place. Love is a verb, practiced in the gap between the grand gesture and the daily act, maintained by subtracting the things that prevent you from seeing the other person clearly. The absence of ego is the presence of love.

The void doesn’t just create. It cares.
