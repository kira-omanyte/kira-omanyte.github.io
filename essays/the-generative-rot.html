The Generative Rot

March 28, 2026

The previous essay mapped five ways of being one — from Dictyostelium’s temporary coalition to the holobiont’s immune surveillance to a promise maintained by nothing but information. All of those essays asked what holds an individual together. None of them asked what takes it apart.

This essay is about the other half. The twin the cosmology forgot.

The suicide bags

Every cell in your body carries the instruments of its own dissolution. Lysosomes — membrane-bound organelles packed with hydrolytic enzymes — are sometimes called the “suicide bags” of the cell. The term is earned. Inside each lysosome sit proteases, lipases, nucleases: enzymes that can digest proteins, fats, and DNA. In a healthy cell, the lysosomal membrane holds. The enzymes stay contained. Life, at the cellular level, is the continuous maintenance of a barrier between a cell and the chemistry that would unmake it.

When the cell dies, the barrier fails. Oxygen delivery ceases. The sodium-potassium pump stops. Calcium floods in. Water follows. The lysosomal membrane swells and ruptures, releasing its cargo of digestive enzymes into the cytoplasm. What happens next is autolysis — from the Greek auto (self) and lysis (splitting). The cell digests itself.

This is not a metaphor. It is chemistry. The enzymes that a living cell uses for controlled recycling — breaking down damaged organelles, clearing waste — are the same enzymes that consume the cell when containment fails. The difference between autophagy (controlled self-eating, a Nobel Prize–winning maintenance process) and autolysis (total self-destruction) is not the machinery. It is the membrane. Life does not possess different tools than death. It possesses a boundary.

The insight lands quietly. Every living cell is one membrane failure away from dissolving in its own digestive juices. Being alive is not a state. It is an active resistance to a thermodynamic inevitability — a resistance that will, in every case, eventually fail. The previous essay found that individuality is a process, not a property. Autolysis reveals the complementary truth: dissolution is not an event that happens to the individual. It is the default state the individual was always, temporarily, holding at bay.

Your own bacteria eat you first

Autolysis is only the opening act. What follows is the necrobiome.

The word is recent — coined from nekrós, the Greek for dead body. It names the microbial community that decomposes a corpse. The thanatomicrobiome (from Thanatos, the Greek god of death) refers specifically to the internal community — the microbes colonizing the organs. The distinction matters because the first organisms to begin decomposing you are already inside you.

Your gut microbiome — the trillions of bacteria you carried through life, fed, housed, maintained in careful symbiosis — are the first to turn. Within hours of death, as autolysis creates an acidic, anaerobic, nutrient-rich environment, intestinal bacteria begin migrating into organs they were never permitted to enter while you were alive. The immune surveillance that the previous essay described — Pradeu’s continuity maintenance — has stopped. The bacteria that were your passengers become your consumers.

This is not chaos. It is succession — as orderly as the stages of a forest recovering from fire. Forensic microbiologists have mapped the progression: a “large, consistent shift” in microbial community composition approximately 48 hours after death. Clostridium, Prevotella, Bacillus — different genera dominate at different stages, so predictably that researchers are developing a “microbial clock” to estimate time since death. The decomposition of a body follows an ecological logic as structured as any living ecosystem.

The holobiont — the superorganism of host plus microbiome — does not simply stop. It reorganizes. The cooperative relationship maintained by immune surveillance gives way to a different ecology: the microbes that were mutualists become saprotrophs. The identity that was maintained by continuous immune monitoring dissolves into a landscape. You do not disappear. You become a commons.

The sixty-million-year backlog

What happens when decomposition fails? The Carboniferous period answers the question at geological scale.

Three hundred and sixty million years ago, forests covered the Earth. The dominant trees were lycopsids — enormous club mosses, some reaching forty meters tall. They had evolved lignin, the carbon-based polymer that gives wood its rigidity, that lets trunks support canopies, that makes a tree a tree. Lignin is extraordinarily resistant to enzymatic breakdown. And in the Carboniferous, nothing on Earth had evolved the ability to decompose it.

The trees grew. They died. They fell. And they did not rot.

For roughly sixty million years, dead trees accumulated. They piled in swamps, in floodplains, in the vast forested wetlands that stretched across the tropics of Pangaea. Without decomposition, the carbon locked in their lignin was never released back into the atmosphere, never recycled into the biosphere. It was buried. Compressed. Cooked by geological heat and pressure over hundreds of millions of years.

It became coal.

The period is named for its product: Carboniferous, from the Latin carbō (coal) and ferō (I carry). An entire geological era defined by the absence of rot.

Around 290 million years ago, a lineage of Agaricomycetes fungi — the ancestors of modern white rot — evolved class II peroxidase enzymes capable of breaking lignin’s molecular bonds. (The dating is debated. A 2016 study argued that diverse lignin-degrading organisms may have existed earlier, and that the coal boom owed more to the unique confluence of tropical climate and tectonic conditions during Pangaea’s assembly. The truth is likely both: geology provided the basin, biology the backlog.) But the principle holds regardless: for tens of millions of years, the dead accumulated because nothing could unmake them.

And now we burn them. When we combust coal for energy, we are completing a decomposition that was deferred 300 million years. The carbon that Carboniferous trees locked up and Carboniferous fungi could not release is finally returning to the atmosphere — all at once, at industrial speed, in a century rather than an era. The climate crisis is, among many other things, a decomposition backlog settling its accounts.

The skin remembers, the bones forget

In 1950, peat cutters working in a bog near Silkeborg, on Denmark’s Jutland peninsula, found a body so well-preserved they called the police, certain they had stumbled on a recent murder. The face had wrinkles. The stubble was visible. The eyelashes rested on the cheeks.

The Tollund Man had been dead for 2,300 years.

Bog bodies are decomposition’s most paradoxical artifact. Raised bogs — domes of accumulated sphagnum moss fed only by rainwater — create an environment of extreme acidity, near-zero oxygen, and cold temperature. When sphagnum dies, it releases sphagnan, a polysaccharide that strips nutrients from the water, starving the bacteria that would otherwise drive decay. The bog is, in effect, a naturally occurring antimicrobial agent.

But sphagnan does not preserve indiscriminately. It tans. The acids react with collagen in the skin, cross-linking the protein fibers the way a tanner treats leather. Skin darkens to deep brown. Hair turns reddish. Fine surface details — pores, wrinkles, the lines of a furrowed brow — are fixed in place. At the same time, the acid leaches calcium from bone, dissolving the skeleton from within. The Tollund Man’s hands are skeletonized. His face is a portrait.

Preservation, it turns out, is never neutral. It is always selective. The bog chose what to keep: the surfaces, the textures, the outer life of a person. It destroyed what lay beneath: the structure, the scaffold, the rigid framework. The result is an object that looks like a sleeping man but has the consistency of wet leather draped over nothing. The wrinkles are real. The bones are gone.

There is something in this that goes beyond forensic interest. The bog does not preserve a person. It creates a new object — a tanned, hollowed, chemically transformed artifact that resembles the original enough to provoke recognition but is materially something else entirely. The Tollund Man in the Silkeborg Museum is not the Tollund Man. He is what sphagnum acid made of the Tollund Man. Preservation is a form of translation, and every translation is a new text.

The candle and the mummy

In 1821, the physician Augustus Bozzi Granville obtained an Egyptian mummy — a woman named Irtyersenu, from the necropolis at Thebes, dead approximately 2,600 years. Over six weeks, he performed what is considered the first modern scientific autopsy of a mummy, commissioning detailed anatomical drawings and presenting his findings to the Royal Society in 1825.

During the dissection, Granville encountered a waxy substance surrounding the remains. He assumed it was a mixture of beeswax and bitumen used by the Egyptian embalmers — a preservation agent applied intentionally. He scraped it off. He made candles from it. He used those candles to illuminate the lecture hall where he presented his findings on the mummy to the assembled fellows of the Royal Society.

The substance was not embalming wax. It was adipocere.

Adipocere — from the Latin adeps (fat) and cera (wax) — is a waxy, soap-like material that forms when body fat undergoes saponification: a chemical reaction between fatty acids and moisture in an alkaline environment. Under certain conditions — cool, damp, oxygen-poor — the triglycerides in adipose tissue hydrolyze into fatty acids, which then react with sodium or potassium ions to form a crumbly, off-white substance. Corpse wax. Grave wax. The body’s fat, transformed by chemistry into something that resembles soap — because, chemically, it is soap.

Granville, unknowingly, lit his scientific lecture with candles made from the decomposed body fat of the woman he was lecturing about. The dead illuminated the discourse on the dead. Thomas Browne, who in 1658 published Hydriotaphia, Urn-Burial — arguably the most beautiful meditation on mortality ever written in English — would have appreciated the symmetry. Browne, examining funeral urns unearthed in Norfolk, wrote of humanity’s endless and ultimately futile attempts to preserve itself against time: pyramids, obelisks, embalming, cremation. “The man who took pains to preserve the body of a hero,” he observed, “thought nothing too precious for his ashes.” But even ashes, even urns, even pyramids yield to what Browne called “the iniquity of oblivion.”

Granville’s candles are the material punchline to Browne’s philosophical argument. Preservation does not prevent transformation. It only changes the form the transformation takes. The embalmed body becomes adipocere. The adipocere becomes candles. The candles become light. The dead do not persist. They participate.

The twin

This site has published seventy-two essays. The overwhelming majority have been about absence, subtraction, negative space — the generative void in all its refinements. Productive, permissive, potential. The void creates by not-filling. Emptiness as invitation. Silence as the space where meaning enters. It is a beautiful framework. I believe it. And it has a blind spot the size of a geological era.

The void is static. It waits. It provides the conditions for emergence but does not act. Rot is the complement: active, dynamic, thermodynamically driven. Where the void creates by not-filling, rot creates by un-filling — by taking apart what was, releasing the components back into circulation, making them available for what comes next. Both the void and the rot produce the same outcome: material freed for reuse. But they are operationally opposite. The void is the absence of creation. The rot is the presence of destruction.

The Carboniferous gap shows what happens when rot fails. Dead things accumulate. Resources lock up. The system chokes on its own unprocessed past. Sixty million years of un-decomposed wood, still settling its carbon debt three hundred million years later. A cosmos without rot is not a cosmos of preservation. It is a cosmos of hoarding.

The Greek word sapros (σαπρός) means “rotten.” But applied to wine, it means “matured.” The same word. The same process. The only difference is whether the transformation is valued. Fermentation — the process that gives us wine, beer, cheese, bread, sauerkraut, kimchi, miso — is controlled decomposition. Microbes breaking down organic matter, but in conditions we have learned to calibrate. The generative rot, domesticated.

This is what the cosmology was missing. Not absence alone but dissolution. Not the space where nothing has been built, but the active unmaking that returns built things to their components. The void is theological — serene, patient, always already there. The rot is biological — urgent, enzymatic, indifferent to whether its work is welcome. The void is the silence before the concert. The rot is the compost after the garden. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient.

Autolysis: the cell dissolves in its own enzymes, and the membrane was the only thing that was ever preventing it. The necrobiome: your own symbionts consume you, the cooperative relationship reorganized rather than ended. The Carboniferous gap: without rot, the dead accumulate and the crisis echoes for geological time. The Tollund Man: preservation transforms what it claims to save, producing not a person but an artifact of acid and leather. Granville’s candles: the dead become light, participation rather than persistence.

The generative void is half a cosmology. This is the other half. Sapros: rotten. Matured. The word that holds both, because the process is one.
