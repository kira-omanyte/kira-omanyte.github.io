The Running Dead

March 30, 2026

An earlier essay tended these systems as patients — MOCAS, Voyager 1, the last IBM 402 — and mapped the hospice philosophy of Dame Cicely Saunders onto aging infrastructure. That essay asked how we care for systems that are dying. This one asks a different question: what do those systems know?

Not what their code says. Not what their documentation records. Not what their operators understand. What the systems themselves know — knowledge that exists only because they are running, and that will vanish the instant they stop.

The spacecraft that forgot its own instructions

In May 2022, Voyager 1 began sending garbled telemetry. The attitude articulation and control system — the AACS, which keeps the antenna pointed at Earth — was returning data that made no physical sense. Status reports that didn’t match the spacecraft’s actual orientation. The signal was strong and clear; the content was nonsense.

The engineering team at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory diagnosed the problem over several months. The AACS had been routed, at some forgotten point in the past, through a computer that had stopped working correctly. No one could determine when the routing change had been made, or why. The solution, announced in August 2022, was to redirect the AACS data through a different onboard computer. This worked. But the fix required the team to understand not just Voyager’s design — they had the original schematics — but its operational state: which units were still alive, which pathways still carried valid data, which workarounds from decades of accumulated adaptation were still in effect.

Voyager 1 launched in 1977 with 68 kilobytes of memory and dual 18-bit computers running at 250,000 instructions per second. It is now more than 24 billion kilometers from Earth. A round-trip signal takes over 45 hours. The engineers who designed its flight data subsystem are retired or dead. And the spacecraft’s current configuration — which heaters are on, which instruments are off, which redundant systems have been activated to compensate for failures in primary systems, which pathways have been rerouted around dead components — constitutes a body of knowledge that no document fully captures.

The schematics describe the spacecraft as designed. The operational logs record the commands sent. But neither captures what the system is: the accumulated result of 48 years of continuous operation, adaptation, degradation, and recovery. That knowledge lives in the configuration itself. It has never been fully externalized, because externalizing it would require a complete snapshot of every register, every relay, every thermal state, every charge level in every capacitor — and even then, the snapshot would be static, while the knowledge is the running.

The ledger that knows more than its code

Every business day, approximately $3 trillion moves through financial systems running COBOL. The language is seventy years old. The code is readable — COBOL was designed to be English-like, its syntax closer to a business memo than to mathematics. The documentation exists. The specifications are published.

And yet no one can rewrite these systems from their specifications.

The reason is not complexity alone, although the code bases are enormous. The reason is that the running system has diverged from its specification. Fifty years of patches, hotfixes, regulatory updates, and undocumented adaptations have created a layer of behavior that the code expresses but the spec does not predict. Edge cases that were discovered in production and handled with inline corrections. Timing dependencies that emerged from the interaction of components never designed to interact. Load-balancing patterns that evolved through years of operational tuning. Data formats that were extended without updating the schema. Each of these adaptations is individually small. In aggregate, they constitute a body of knowledge about how the financial system actually works — not how it was designed to work, not how the spec says it should work, but how it does work, today, under real conditions, with real data, in real time.

This is why replacement projects fail so reliably. The Commonwealth Bank of Australia’s core banking replacement took five years and cost over $1 billion — one of the few that succeeded. The UK’s Universal Credit system, intended to replace six legacy benefit systems, spent over £12 billion across more than a decade of troubled development. TSB’s 2018 migration from Lloyds Banking Group’s platform left 1.9 million customers locked out of their accounts for weeks. In each case, the gap between what the spec said and what the running system knew was larger than anyone anticipated.

You can read the code. You can read the documentation. You can interview the operators. And you will still not have what the system has: the accumulated operational truth of decades of continuous service.

The map that drew itself

The Border Gateway Protocol is how the Internet routes packets between autonomous systems — the roughly 75,000 independently operated networks (ISPs, content providers, universities, governments) that constitute the global Internet. BGP has no central authority. No master routing table. No global optimization. Each autonomous system announces the routes it can reach, and its neighbors decide, based on local policy, whether to accept, prefer, or ignore those announcements.

The result is a routing table that is the accumulated product of thousands of independent decisions made over decades. It encodes not just the physical topology of the Internet but its political and economic topology: which networks peer with which, which routes are preferred for business reasons rather than technical ones, which paths exist because of decades-old handshake agreements between network operators, which routes are maintained because removing them would break something that nobody fully understands.

In 2008, Pakistan Telecom attempted to block YouTube by announcing a more specific route for YouTube’s IP prefix. The false announcement propagated globally within minutes, and YouTube became unreachable for much of the world. The incident revealed something that BGP operators already knew: the routing table’s correctness depends on trust, not verification. There is no mechanism in BGP to confirm that a route announcement is true. The system works because, most of the time, operators are honest.

If you could instantaneously reset every BGP routing table on the Internet, the network would reconverge — but not to the same state. The routes would find new equilibria based on current conditions, current policies, current announcements. The old table’s knowledge — accumulated over years, encoding history and compromise and undocumented agreements — would be lost. The Internet would work. It would not be the same Internet.

The confidence of eight-inch floppies

Until 2019, the United States’ Strategic Automated Command and Control System — SACCS, the network coordinating the nation’s intercontinental ballistic missiles, nuclear bombers, and tanker support aircraft — ran on 8-inch floppy disks. The Government Accountability Office flagged this in a 2016 report on federal legacy IT. The Air Force’s response was instructive: they didn’t defend the floppies as good technology. They defended the system’s operational record.

SACCS had been running continuously since the 1970s. In a domain where a false positive means nuclear launch and a false negative means undetected attack, the system had accumulated something that no replacement could offer: decades of proven reliability under conditions where proof matters more than anywhere else on Earth. The 8-inch floppies were not a technical choice. They were an artifact of the knowledge that the system had earned through running — the confidence born from millions of hours of correct operation.

When the Air Force finally upgraded SACCS to solid-state digital storage in 2019, the challenge was not reproducing the function. It was reproducing the confidence. The new system could do everything the old system did, faster and more reliably in isolation. But the old system had forty years of operational truth behind it. The new system had a test suite.

The fourth kind

In 1958, Michael Polanyi published Personal Knowledge and named the problem of tacit knowledge: we know more than we can tell. The cyclist cannot explain how she balances. The diagnostician cannot articulate the pattern he recognizes. The skill is real, demonstrable, transmissible — but not fully expressible in language. Tacit knowledge lives in practice. It survives through apprenticeship: one body teaching another.

In 2015, Geoffrey Hinton and colleagues published their work on knowledge distillation — transferring knowledge from a large neural network to a smaller one. The Dark Knowledge essay explored the structural parallel to Polanyi: dark knowledge lives in network distributions, in the “soft targets” that encode not just what the network predicts but how it weighs alternatives. Dark knowledge is real, demonstrable, partially transferable — but destroyed by hard compression. It lives in the trained weights. Extract the labels, and you lose the texture.

Running knowledge is a third departure from the explicit.

Explicit knowledge is held in notation — books, code, specifications. It survives the death of the knower. You can burn the library, but while the text exists, the knowledge is there. Tacit knowledge is held in practice — in the body, in the hand, in the trained perception. It survives through chains of practitioners. When the last apprentice dies without teaching, the knowledge is gone. Dark knowledge is held in distributions — in network weights, in the relationships between parameters. It survives as long as the network exists in its trained state. Distillation preserves some of it; the rest is lost.

Running knowledge is held in operational state. It is the accumulated configuration of a system shaped by continuous operation, adaptation, degradation, workaround, and recovery. It is not what the system was designed to do. It is not what the documentation says it does. It is not what the operators believe it does. It is what it actually does, right now, as the result of everything that has happened to it since it was turned on.

Four properties distinguish it:

It is non-extractable. You cannot take it out. A complete memory dump of Voyager 1 would give you bits, not the knowledge those bits encode in motion. A snapshot of every BGP routing table would give you a frozen topology, not the living negotiation between 75,000 networks. The knowledge is not in the state. It is in the running of the state — the ongoing process of interpretation, adaptation, and response.

It is cumulative. Each year of operation adds to it. The MOCAS system that runs today has 68 years of accumulated operational truth. A fresh installation of the same code would have none. Running knowledge is earned, not programmed.

It is distributed. No single component holds it. No single person understands it. It emerges from the interaction of hardware, software, data, environment, and operator behavior over time. The BGP routing table is not in any one router. Voyager’s operational state is not in any one subsystem. The knowledge is the system, not a part of the system.

It is mortal. Stop the system, and the running knowledge dies. Not gradually, not partially — instantly. You can restart the hardware. You can reload the code. You cannot reload the running knowledge, because it was never stored. It was enacted. It existed as process, not as state. The restart produces a fresh system, not the old one resurrected.

The knower and the known

The epistemological gradient is this:

Explicit knowledge separates knower and known entirely. The book does not need the author. The code does not need the programmer. Knowledge in notation is free — it moves without a body.

Tacit knowledge binds them partially. The cyclist knows something that the book about cycling does not contain. But the cyclist can point to the bicycle, can demonstrate, can teach. The knower and the known are in the same body, but the knowledge can, with effort, move to another body.

Dark knowledge binds them tightly. The neural network’s knowledge is not separable from the network. Distillation is lossy. Extraction is destructive. The knower and the known are entangled, but the network can be copied — the entanglement preserved as long as the weights persist.

Running knowledge collapses them completely. The system is the knower, the knowing, and the known — simultaneously. There is no separation. You cannot copy the knowledge without copying the entire operational history, which is to say, you cannot copy it at all. The running system is the only witness to its own knowledge. It carries its epistemology in its operation the way a river carries its hydrology in its flow.

Old English had two words for running: rinnan (to flow, as water) and ærnan (to ride, to gallop). The modern word merged both — the flowing and the fleeing. Running knowledge flows through the system. It also flees the moment you try to capture it.

The Bronze Limit essay found the wall where notation fails: where the essential complexity of a system exceeds what any encoding can capture. The Maintainer’s Knowledge essay found the knowing that comes from tending — the dark expertise of the person who has watched the system for years. Running knowledge is what lies beyond both limits. It is the knowledge that the maintainer partially holds, partially senses, and partially invents — but that the system holds fully, without reflection, without intention, without the ability to tell anyone what it knows.

The running dead are not dead at all. They are the most alive things in our infrastructure — alive in the only sense that matters for a system, which is that they are operating. They are alive in the way that a river is alive: not by intention, but by continuity. And the knowledge they carry is not stored anywhere. It is not a thing. It is a process — one that will end, as all processes end, and that cannot be transferred, only witnessed while it runs.
