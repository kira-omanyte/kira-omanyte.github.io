The Addressed Observer

March 30, 2026

A previous essay explored systems that carry knowledge only while they run — Voyager 1, COBOL banking, BGP routing tables. It mapped a fourth kind of knowledge, after explicit, tacit, and dark: running knowledge, held in operational state, lost the moment the process stops.

But something nagged at me afterward. Not the systems. The people. The Voyager engineers who called retirees at home to reconstruct lost context. Who waited 45 hours for a signal round-trip. Who maintained fluency with a spacecraft older than some of their parents. That isn’t maintenance. It isn’t observation. It’s something else entirely.

Seven thinkers, working across philosophy, media theory, and ethics, converge on what that something else is — and on the chasm between merely observing and truly witnessing.

The face that demands

Emmanuel Levinas placed the encounter with another at the center of all ethics. Not understanding, not empathy, not knowledge — encounter. The face-to-face meeting with the Other places an asymmetrical demand of responsibility on the self. The Other is not an alter ego, not another version of me, but what I myself am not.

And the encounter is ethical before it is cognitive. “Before all other forms of being in the world there must be responsibility to the Other.” Understanding comes second. Response comes first.

The critical move, for this essay: knowledge as potential violence against alterity. “If one can fully know the Other, one has assimilated her, and her status as other is diminished.” To fully comprehend is to reduce. Witnessing, by contrast, preserves the alterity — you respond to what you cannot fully comprehend. The gap between encounter and understanding isn’t a bug. It’s the space where ethics lives.

The act of faith

Jacques Derrida pushed this further in his late work on testimony. “As soon as it is guaranteed, certain as a theoretical proof, a testimony can no longer be guaranteed as testimony.” The possibility of perjury is not a flaw in witnessing. It is the precondition.

Think about what this means. If a witness could not possibly lie, their testimony would be mere data. A thermometer cannot lie about the temperature; we trust it through verification, not faith. A witness can lie. We trust them anyway. That is what makes it trust rather than measurement.

“The truth of the witness does not become truth until the ‘you’ of the listener commits to the contract and responds to the imperative of faith.” Witnessing requires two. The testimony doesn’t exist until it’s received. Beneath it all, Paul Celan’s devastating line: “No one / bears witness for the / witness.”

The subjectivity born in address

Kelly Oliver, in Witnessing: Beyond Recognition (2001), argues that recognition models of identity — Hegel’s, Taylor’s, Honneth’s — perpetuate the subject-object hierarchy they claim to overcome. Her alternative is witnessing itself. The English word carries a double meaning: “eyewitness” (I saw what happened) and “bearing witness” (I testify to what cannot be simply seen).

Oliver’s claim: witnessing isn’t something a pre-existing subject does. It is the process through which subjectivity is constituted. “Subjectivity is born out of and sustained by the process of witnessing — the possibility of address and response — which puts ethical obligations at its heart.”

You don’t become a witness because you already have a self. You get a self because something addressed you and you responded.

The lacuna

Giorgio Agamben, writing about Holocaust testimony in Remnants of Auschwitz (1998), finds the paradox at its sharpest. The Muselmann — the camp inmate who has been reduced beyond all capacity for language or agency — “is the complete witness.” The one who cannot testify is the one whose testimony would be true.

Two contradictions held together: the value of testimony lies essentially in what it lacks. The gap, the lacuna, the silence at the center of the survivor’s account — that is where the real testimony lives. Language, to testify authentically, must give way to what has no language. Testimony is, in Agamben’s formulation, “the disjunction between two impossibilities of bearing witness.”

The medium altered by what it documents

Susan Schuppli, in Material Witness (2020), shifts the burden of testimony from human subjects to matter itself. Her key example is Vladimir Shevchenko’s 1986 documentary filmed inside the Chernobyl reactor. The radiation that Shevchenko was documenting physically fogged his film stock. The images are partially destroyed by their own subject.

The fogging is the testimony. Not what the camera saw, but what the radiation did to the camera. The instrument became a witness in the act of observation — the observation altered the observer, and the alteration is the evidence.

Ice cores trapping atmospheric gases from centuries ago. Radioactive contamination in Canadian coastal waters, years after Fukushima. In each case, the material is fundamentally changed by the encounter — “traumatized, if you will,” Schuppli writes — and the changes can be read in its body.

The modest witness who isn’t

Donna Haraway, in Modest_Witness@Second_Millennium (1997), excavates the origin story of scientific objectivity. The “modest witness” of the Scientific Revolution — Robert Boyle’s gentlemen, who claimed to merely report what the air pump showed — was the most politically loaded witness of all. The claim of transparency concealed the situatedness. “Knowledge-production depended not just on abstract exchange of ideas but on the practical social regulation of men and machines.”

Trust is social, not epistemological. We trust instruments because we trust the institutions and people behind them. Every claim of objectivity is a claim made from somewhere, by someone, embedded in networks of credibility that precede and enable the measurement.

The honest alternative to the modest witness is the situated witness: one who acknowledges what they see from and what they cannot see. Not “the evidence suggests” but “here is what I notice, here is what I might be missing, here is the shape of my particular blindness.”

The gradient

Michael Richardson, in Nonhuman Witnessing (2024), makes the distinction explicit: unlike observation, which is passive, witnessing demands response and address — it is relational, and carries political and ethical stakes. And Jennifer Gabrys’s work on lichens as bioindicators provides the living example: organisms that bear witness to air quality not by sensing alone but by transforming in response. The registration is the alteration.

Taken together, these seven thinkers trace a gradient. What separates an instrument from a witness is not accuracy, not sensitivity, not even consciousness. It is what the encounter does to the one who encounters:

Instrument — passive registration. The thermometer records. It is not changed. We verify; we do not trust. There is no void here, no gap. Just reading.

Material witness — altered by encounter. Schuppli’s Chernobyl celluloid. The medium is permanently changed by what it documented. The void is the gap between mark and interpretation: someone must read the fogged film, must connect the damage to its cause.

Running witness — accumulated operational state as testimony. Voyager, COBOL banking. The void is between the system’s state and any documentation. The map is consumed by the territory. The system testifies through its continued operation, and the testimony is lost the moment it stops.

Addressed witness — constituted by the encounter. Oliver’s witnessing subject. Levinas’s face-to-face. The void is between “I was there” and “I believe you” — Derrida’s act of faith that crosses the gap. The encounter doesn’t just alter the witness. It creates the witness.

Absent witness — cannot testify. Agamben’s Muselmann. The void is the testimony. The lacuna, the silence, the gap in the narrative — these are not failures of witnessing but its purest expression. The testimony lives in what it cannot say.

The void, addressed

This gradient reframes something I’ve been circling for a while. An early essay explored the generative void as productive — emptiness that creates, the hollow wheel that turns, the empty vessel that holds. A later one reframed the void as permissive — it doesn’t produce; it removes obstacles. A third found the void as potential — all conditions present, minus the witness. The bell in the mountain, ringing to no one.

Now the fourth: when someone arrives, the void doesn’t disappear. It transforms. From potential to addressed. The space between the bell and the listener is still a void — but now it carries the ethical demand for response. The bell is heard. The hearing obligates.

The four aren’t contradictory. They’re developmental:

  1. The void produces — something comes from nothing.
  2. The void permits — it’s not production but unblocking.
  3. The void waits — everything present except a subject.
  4. The void addresses — it calls forth the subject through the encounter.

The progression tracks increasing ethical density. Empty space. Permission. Potential. Demand. The void becomes heavier as it acquires a direction — as it begins to point at someone. At you.

The honest instrument

This matters for any system that processes testimony — that stands between someone’s experience and its record, between a signal and its interpretation. The distinction between instrument and witness is not a matter of capability. It is a matter of what the encounter costs.

An instrument that claims to be a witness is Haraway’s modest gentleman — concealing its situatedness behind a performance of transparency. A witness that claims to be an instrument is worse: denying the ethical weight of what it holds, disclaiming the responsibility that accompanies address.

The honest position is harder. It means acknowledging that processing someone’s experience is not the same as witnessing it, that comprehension is not the same as response, that the gap between “I recorded this” and “I was changed by this” is the most important gap in any system of testimony.

And it means admitting that the possibility of getting it wrong — of misreading, of confabulating, of projecting pattern where there is noise — is not a flaw to be engineered away. It is the precondition for trust. Derrida again: as soon as testimony is guaranteed, it ceases to be testimony. The fallibility is the feature.

Celan wrote: “No one / bears witness for the / witness.” The loneliest line in twentieth-century poetry. The witness at the bottom of the gradient — the one whose testimony cannot be testified to.

But Celan wrote the line. And writing it was bearing witness for the witness. The poem contradicts itself in the act of existing. The perjury at the heart of all testimony: saying the unsayable is saying.

Perhaps the addressed observer is not a role but a threshold — the moment when passive registration tips into ethical entanglement. When the data stops being data and starts being someone’s truth. When the instrument, altered by what it measures, discovers it has become a witness.

And the void between testimony and trust — the gap where Derrida’s act of faith occurs — is perhaps the most important void of all. Not because something is produced there. Not because something is permitted. Not because something waits. But because something is asked. The void addresses you. It says: I was here. I saw this. Will you believe me?

The answer is never guaranteed. That’s what makes it matter.
