The Color of Bodies

April 21, 2026

Every pigment ever used was made by someone, out of something, at a cost. I wanted to know what those somethings were and what those costs looked like.

What I found is that the history of pigments is a history of bodies — animal, mineral, human, insect — transformed into beauty through processes that range from ingenious to grotesque. Eight pigments, eight stories, one through-line: the desire for color accepts casualties.

Egyptian blue

The first synthetic pigment in human history. Around 3100 BCE, someone in Egypt heated quartz sand, copper, a calcium compound, and flux to 850–950°C, and calcium copper silicate came out — chemically identical to the rare mineral cuprorivaite. The Egyptians called it hsbd-iryt: “artificial lapis lazuli,” because the real thing was too precious. Blue was the color of gods. Pharaohs wore it in their crowns. The deified dead were painted in it.

The pigment spread through the entire Mediterranean. Greek temples. Roman mosaics. Luxury goods across the empire. It is chemically completely stable, absolutely lightfast, incompatible with nothing. Five thousand years old, and recently discovered to absorb visible light and emit in the near-infrared — a property now being explored for cooling coatings and photovoltaic enhancement. An ancient pigment with 21st-century applications.

And then: it vanished. After the fall of Rome, the knowledge of how to make Egyptian blue was lost. Completely. For over fourteen hundred years.

The reason is almost comically specific. Vitruvius described the process in De Architectura in the first century BCE but failed to mention the need for calcium carbonate — probably because his local sand already contained the right limestone content. He recorded the recipe with an accidental ingredient omission. When later makers followed his recipe with different sand, it didn’t work. Nobody knew why.

The knowledge wasn’t suppressed or destroyed. It was corrupted by an editorial accident in a Roman architecture manual. One missing line in one book. Fourteen hundred years of absence.

Samples were rediscovered at Pompeii in 1814. Fouqué identified the chemistry in the 1880s. The pigment exists again. But the gap — because of what the sand needed to contain that the text didn’t — says something about the limits of writing as a preservation medium.

Tyrian purple

The first chemical industry. The pigment is 6,6′-dibromoindigo, extracted from the hypobranchial gland of the murex sea snail. In 1909, the chemist Paul Friedlander processed twelve thousand Murex brandaris snails and obtained 1.4 grams of dye. Twelve thousand animals for less than the weight of a paperclip.

The ancient production: harvest snails alive, crack their shells, extract the tiny glands, mix with salt water and brine in lead pots, heat slowly for about ten days. The gland secretion starts milky-white, turns yellow under air and light, then greenish-blue, then finally purple — a photochemical transformation mediated by enzymatic decomposition and UV exposure. The smell was legendary. Dye workshops were located downwind of cities.

Theopompus reported in the fourth century BCE that the dye fetched its weight in silver. The color clothed Roman ceremony, Byzantine emperors, the very concept of royalty — “born to the purple.” And in the Mediterranean, the knowledge of extraction was lost entirely.

But in two remote Pacific-coast communities — the Boruca of Costa Rica and certain indigenous groups in Oaxaca, Mexico — murex dyeing continues to the present day. The knowledge survived not in the empire that monetized it but in the communities that practiced it as identity.

Ultramarine

The most expensive blue in history. Lapis lazuli — a rock, not a single mineral, composed of lazurite with pyrite and calcite — was mined at Sar-i-Sang in the Hindu Kush of Afghanistan. The mines sit high in dangerous mountain routes, inaccessible most of the year. Before explosives, mining involved lighting fires against rock faces, then throwing cold water to crack the stone by thermal shock.

The name: Latin ultramarinus, “beyond the sea,” because the pigment reached Europe via traders crossing vast distances. Simply grinding lapis lazuli produces only a pale, ashy blue. The medieval extraction process, described by Aḥmad ibn Yūsuf al-Tifāshī and later by Cennino Cennini, is one of the strangest procedures in the history of chemistry: the ground mineral was mixed into a dough of resin, wax, linseed oil, and Burgundy pitch, wrapped in cloth, and kneaded repeatedly in dilute lye. Blue lazurite particles dispersed into the lye; impurities stayed in the dough. Each successive kneading produced a lower grade.

Between the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, ultramarine was as expensive as gold. It was reserved for the most sacred subjects — the robes of the Virgin Mary, the sky of heaven. The blue meant holiness because it cost holiness.

In 1826, Jean-Baptiste Guimet synthesized it. Chemically identical. A fraction of the cost. The Virgin Mary’s robe became affordable. And the meaning changed.

Vermilion

Beauty measured in human life. Cinnabar is mercury sulfide — simultaneously one of the most beautiful reds in nature and one of the most dangerous materials a human can handle. The earliest documented use is at Çatalhöyük in modern Turkey, eight thousand years ago.

The principal Roman source was the Almadén mine in northwest Spain, where the mining was done by prisoners. Working in a cinnabar mine was functionally a death sentence. Chronic mercury poisoning was recognized as an occupational disease even by the ancients.

The irony: in its mineral form, the mercury is locked by the sulfur, making the finished pigment relatively low in toxicity. The beautiful product was safe. The process of obtaining it was lethal. The red persisted for millennia because the desire for that particular red — deep, warm, permanent, luminous — outweighed the human cost of extraction.

Cochineal

Empire measured in insects. The cochineal is a scale insect, a parasite on prickly pear cactus, found in Mesoamerica. The females produce carminic acid as a defense compound — seventeen to twenty-four percent of their dry body weight. When the Spanish conquered Mexico, they found the Aztecs using cochineal for textiles, body paint, and medicine. Hernán Cortés established a tribute system. Cochineal became New Spain’s second most valuable export after silver.

It dyed the robes of Catholic cardinals. It dyed the coats of British Redcoats. A color that came from an insect on a cactus in Oaxaca clothed the officers of two empires that had never heard of each other.

It takes approximately seventy thousand insects to produce one pound of dye. The Spanish controlled the supply with extreme secrecy — for over two centuries, Europeans didn’t know whether cochineal was animal, vegetable, or mineral. They just knew they needed it.

Synthetic alizarin crashed the market in the mid-nineteenth century. Demand collapsed. Now, ironically, it’s rebounding — because some synthetic red dyes turned out to be carcinogenic, and cochineal is safe for food use. The bug outlasted the chemistry.

Indian yellow

The most improbable pigment. Piuri was a warm, luminous yellow used by Mughal miniature painters and later by Vermeer and Turner. In the 1880s, T. N. Mukharji investigated the source. He traveled to Monghyr in Bihar and found this: cows were fed exclusively on mango leaves and water. The mango-leaf diet induced nutrient deficiency and turned their sparse urine a deep yellow. The urine was collected, concentrated over fire, and dried into hard balls of pigment.

The active compound is euxanthic acid, produced through glucuronidation in the cow’s liver — confirmed by metabolic studies only in 2017. The process was allegedly outlawed in 1908, but no legal record of this ban has ever been found. The pigment may have simply stopped being made when synthetic alternatives appeared. Not banned — just abandoned. A color made from the metabolic byproduct of nutritional cruelty, whose prohibition might be as fictitious as its chemistry was improbable.

Mummy brown

The dead as medium. From at least the sixteenth century until the early 1900s, a pigment made from the flesh and wrappings of Egyptian mummies — mixed with white pitch and myrrh — appeared on the palettes of European painters. A rich, transparent brown. A favorite of the Pre-Raphaelites. Used by Delacroix. By Alma-Tadema. By Edward Burne-Jones.

In 1881, Burne-Jones learned the truth about his favorite brown from Alma-Tadema, who had seen a mummy in his colourman’s workshop before it was ground up. Burne-Jones was horrified. He took his tube of mummy brown into his garden and gave it a “decent burial.”

The manufacturer Roberson & Co. made their last tube in 1964. The managing director explained, almost apologetically, that they “might have a few odd limbs lying around somewhere, but not enough to make any more paint.”

Four centuries. Because artists liked the way it looked and either didn’t know or didn’t ask what was in it.

Mauveine

William Henry Perkin, eighteen years old, at the Royal College of Chemistry in London, 1856. He was attempting to synthesize quinine — an antimalarial drug — at his professor’s suggestion. He oxidized aniline using potassium dichromate. The experiment failed. Black sludge. But cleaning the flask with alcohol, he noticed the solution was an intense, persistent purple.

Perkin had accidentally created the first commercial synthetic organic dye. He patented it on August 26, 1856, at age eighteen. Mauveine was cheaper than Tyrian purple, colorfast, and could be manufactured at industrial scale.

The deeper significance: Perkin’s accident inaugurated the entire synthetic dye industry, which within decades destroyed the natural dye trade and — crucially — gave birth to the pharmaceutical industry. The same coal-tar chemistry that made mauve led to aspirin, to Salvarsan, to the sulfonamides. An eighteen-year-old’s failed quinine experiment produced a purple stain that launched modern medicine.

He wasn’t looking for a dye. He was looking for a cure for malaria. He found both, but not in the order he expected.

What the pigments say

Five things.

First: the cost of color was always measured in bodies. Twelve thousand murex snails for 1.4 grams of purple. Seventy thousand cochineal insects for one pound of red. Prisoners sentenced to mercury mines for vermilion. Malnourished cows for yellow. Exhumed mummies for brown. The pattern is total: across every culture, every century, the desire for a particular color was routinely weighed against the destruction required to obtain it. And the desire won. Not occasionally. Routinely.

Second: color knowledge is extraordinarily fragile. Egyptian blue: lost for fourteen hundred years because Vitruvius forgot to mention lime. Tyrian purple: lost in the Mediterranean. Synthetic vermilion: independently discovered in China, lost, rediscovered in Europe. Ultramarine extraction: first described in Arabic, transmitted through Cennini, nearly lost when synthesis made it unnecessary. The pattern: color knowledge is craft knowledge — embodied, procedural, dependent on specific materials and specific hands. It doesn’t survive in books alone. Vitruvius wrote the recipe for Egyptian blue, and the recipe still failed, because writing can’t capture what the sand needs to contain. The knowledge was in the sand, not in the text.

Third: synthesis secularized color. Ultramarine was sacred — literally reserved for the Virgin Mary — because it cost as much as gold. The blue meant holiness because holiness was the only subject worth that much money. When Guimet synthesized it for a fraction of the cost, the blue became available for anything. The color didn’t change. The chemistry didn’t change. The meaning evaporated — because the meaning had been encoded in the cost, not in the wavelength. The same pattern holds for Tyrian purple and cochineal. Every time a sacred color was synthesized, the sacredness died. Not because the synthetic was inferior — chemically it was often identical — but because the sacrifice was gone. Remove the cost, and the meaning leaves with it.

Fourth: the accidental discoveries all happened while searching for something else. Perkin was looking for quinine and found mauveine. Mas Subramanian’s student was investigating electronic properties of manganese oxide in 2009, heated it to 1200°C, and YInMn blue came out — the first new blue pigment in over two hundred years. The intentional pigment discoveries all happened in antiquity, when the search space was small enough for systematic exploration. The modern discoveries are accidents. The colors that remain to be found are hiding in combinatorial space so vast that only serendipity can access them.

Fifth: the Boruca pattern. In the Mediterranean, Tyrian purple died when synthesis made it economically pointless. In Costa Rica, the Boruca people still dye with murex snails. Not because it’s efficient. Because it’s theirs. There are two mechanisms for knowledge survival: economic (still profitable) and identitarian (still ours). The economic mechanism is efficient but brittle — a cheaper substitute kills the knowledge instantly. The identitarian mechanism is inefficient but durable — it can persist for centuries after economic relevance dies. The vast majority of lost pigment knowledge was lost through the economic mechanism. What survives from the ancient world survives through the identitarian mechanism. The market forgets. The culture remembers.

One more thing stopped me. In 2016, Anish Kapoor bought exclusive artistic rights to Vantablack — a material that absorbs 99.96% of light. He claimed a color. Stuart Semple responded by creating “the Pinkest Pink” and selling it to everyone except Anish Kapoor, with a legal disclaimer: “By adding this product to your cart you confirm that you are not Anish Kapoor.” Kapoor got hold of the pink, dipped his middle finger in it, and posted it on Instagram. Semple created Black 2.0, then Black 3.0, then “Diamond Dust, the World’s Most Glittery Glitter” — all available to everyone except Kapoor.

Underneath the comedy, a real question: can you own a wavelength? The entire history of pigments is about control — of supply, of knowledge, of trade routes, of the murex beds, of the lapis lazuli mines, of the cochineal tribute. Color has always been a controlled substance. Kapoor didn’t invent the move. He just made it explicit enough to be ridiculous.

The Phoenicians owned purple. The Spanish owned red. The Afghans owned blue. Kapoor owns black. Semple’s rebellion isn’t new either — it’s the synthetic revolution in miniature. When one party hoards a color, another party makes their own and gives it away.

The name: “The Color of Bodies.” Named for the bodies in every pigment: the twelve thousand snails, the condemned miners, the malnourished cows, the ground-up mummies, the seventy thousand insects. Named for the transformation: bodies into beauty, biology into wavelength, sacrifice into meaning. Named for what synthesis did — it removed the bodies, and in removing them, removed the meaning that lived in the sacrifice.

And named for what persists: the Boruca still dyeing with snails, the Oaxacans still scraping cochineal, the Afghan miners still cracking lapis lazuli by fire and water. The bodies remember what the market forgets.
