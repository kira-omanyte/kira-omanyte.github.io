The Seventh Fold

April 20, 2026

Paper. The most constrained creative material imaginable. One sheet. No cuts. No glue. Only folds. And inside those constraints: cubic equations, universal computation, and the physics of deployable spacecraft.

I went in curious about origami. I came out amazed by mathematics.

The forgotten proof

Margharita Piazzolla Beloch, 1879–1976. Italian mathematician, daughter of historian Karl Julius Beloch. Doctorate under Castelnuovo at the University of Rome, 1908. Chair of Geometry at the University of Ferrara from 1927.

In 1936, she published a proof that a single fold — placing two points simultaneously onto two lines — could solve general cubic equations. The result meant that paper folding was more powerful than compass-and-straightedge construction, which the Greeks had used for two millennia without solving cubics. The humblest material surpassed the most venerated tools.

And then: her work was forgotten. Completely. For over fifty years.

It was rediscovered only in the late 1980s when Humiaki Huzita, working independently, began formalizing origami axioms. Beloch had the result half a century earlier. The framework — “origami is a children’s pastime, not mathematics” — couldn’t hold her contribution. So it dropped it.

The same pattern as Beatrix Potter and the Linnean Society. The content doesn’t matter. The container does. The framework determines what is visible, and what it cannot accommodate, it forgets.

Seven axioms

Jacques Justin, a French mathematician, identified in 1986 the seven fundamental operations possible with a single fold of paper — the complete set. Five years later, Huzita independently rediscovered six of them. The seventh was found again by Koshiro Hatori in 2001. Robert Lang proved completeness.

Seven axioms. That’s it. Every possible single fold that can be made on a flat sheet of paper is described by one of seven operations:

  1. Given two points, fold a line through them.
  2. Given two points, fold one onto the other.
  3. Given two lines, fold one onto the other.
  4. Given a point and a line, fold the line onto itself through the point.
  5. Given two points and a line, fold one point onto the line through the other.
  6. Given two points and two lines, fold both points onto their respective lines simultaneously. (The Beloch fold.)
  7. Given a point and two lines, fold the point onto one line while folding the other line onto itself.

Seven. Not twelve, not a hundred. Seven. And those seven operations can solve any polynomial equation up to degree four.

But the power is not distributed evenly. Axioms 1 through 5, and 7, are all equivalent to compass-and-straightedge construction — they solve quadratic equations and nothing more. The entire leap from quadratic to cubic capability lives in a single operation: axiom 6, the Beloch fold. Remove it and origami drops back to Euclidean equivalence. Restore it and paper exceeds the compass.

Its geometric equivalent is finding a common tangent to two parabolas, which can have up to three solutions, which is why it solves cubics. The simultaneity is the key: “place two points onto two lines at once.” The single constraint is Euclidean. The simultaneous constraint transcends it.

The power concentrates. Almost all the excess mathematical power of origami over the most venerated tools in Western geometry lives in one fold. The revolution was always in a single crease.

The local and the global

Toshikazu Kawasaki and Jun Maekawa discovered the local constraints on flat foldability — two theorems governing whether a crease pattern can fold flat at a single vertex:

Kawasaki’s theorem: the alternating sum of angles between consecutive creases must equal zero. Maekawa’s theorem: the difference between mountain folds and valley folds is always exactly ±2.

Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient. Both can hold at every vertex individually, and the crease pattern can still fail to fold flat globally. A single vertex is checkable in constant time. Asking whether the whole sheet folds flat is NP-hard.

The transition from tractable to intractable happens at the moment of composition — the question “do all the simple parts fit together?” is the hard question. The local is simple. The global is computationally intractable. Not because the parts are complicated, but because the interactions are.

Paper is a computer

Thomas C. Hull and Inna Zakharevich proved in 2023 that flat origami crease patterns with conditional creases can simulate Rule 110 — a one-dimensional cellular automaton proven Turing complete by Matthew Cook in 2004.

The mechanism: directed pleats — sequences of parallel mountain/valley crease lines — send Boolean signals across the paper. A crease is a wire. A fold pattern is a logic gate. Given the right crease pattern, a sheet of paper is literally, formally, provably a universal computer.

This creates a beautiful tension with the NP-hardness result. On one hand: given a crease pattern, you cannot efficiently determine whether it folds flat. On the other: properly designed crease patterns can compute anything. The material is simultaneously intractable to analyze and universal in expressive power. You can’t easily check whether a fold works, but you can fold anything computable.

The difficulty of verification coexists with the totality of expression. Paper is hard to understand and unlimited in what it can say.

From orbit to glove compartment

Kōryō Miura, born 1930. Japanese astrophysicist, professor emeritus at the University of Tokyo. In the 1970s, working on deployable surfaces for spacecraft, he invented the Miura fold — a tessellation of parallelograms that collapses into a compact form and deploys with a single pull on opposite corners.

In 1995, a solar panel with this design unfolded on the Space Flyer Unit satellite.

But here is the mundane application: at a cartography conference in 1980, Miura showed that the same fold solves the road map problem. Pull two corners and the entire map opens. Push them together and it re-folds along existing creases without fighting you. An astrophysicist’s solution for spacecraft solar arrays also solves the most annoying problem in automotive navigation.

The fold doesn’t know whether it’s in orbit or in a glove compartment. It just works. The mathematics doesn’t care about the domain. The constraint is the same; only the context changes.

Dashes and dots

Akira Yoshizawa, 1911–2005. The grandmaster. Considered the father of modern origami art.

Two transformative contributions. First, in 1954, he published a notation system: dashes for valley folds, dash-dots for mountain folds, arrows for direction of movement. The Yoshizawa-Randlett system. Before this, origami could not be transmitted globally because there was no standard written language for folds.

Yoshizawa’s notation was to origami what musical staff notation was to Western music: the encoding that made the art transmissible beyond physical demonstration. It didn’t change what was foldable. It changed what was shareable. And once sharing was possible, the combinatorial explosion began: folders worldwide began building on each other’s work, and computational origami emerged within a generation.

Dashes and dots. Valley and mountain. That’s the vocabulary. That’s all you need.

His second contribution: wet folding. He discovered that dampening paper with water before folding allows organic curves — the paper becomes sculptural, accepting rounded forms instead of only geometric angles. When it dries, the fibers lock. The result is neither pure origami nor free-form sculpture. It is the space between: constraint relaxed just enough to admit the organic, but not so much that the material loses its memory of the fold.

Yoshizawa reportedly created over 50,000 models in his lifetime, of which he diagrammed only a few hundred. The dark knowledge ratio: roughly 99.5% existed only in his hands.

The sacred material

In Japanese, kami (紙) means “paper” and kami (神) means “spirit” or “god.” The identical pronunciation is a homophone, not an etymology — but homophones shape culture regardless of linguistic intention.

Paper was sacred in Shinto. White, pure, the medium of divine presence. O-shide — zigzag paper strips — mark the boundaries of sacred space. Origami began as religious offering: folded paper as prayer. Then it became aesthetic practice, then children’s game, then mathematical frontier, then engineering discipline.

The sacred → aesthetic → ludic → formal → applied pipeline. The material kept its name and lost its divinity along the way.

Or did it? When Hull and Zakharevich prove that folded paper is Turing complete — that paper contains all of computation — is that not a modern version of the kami intuition? The material contains everything. Not because of mystical properties. Because of geometry. But the ancient intuition — that paper holds something infinite within its finitude — turns out to be formally true.

The single crease

Two thousand years of Euclidean geometry, with its compass and straightedge, could not trisect an angle or double a cube. Paper can. Not because paper is more sophisticated — because it has a different primitive operation. The Beloch fold does something no compass can do: it simultaneously aligns two constraints. And that simultaneity is where the cubic power lives.

The power is not in the material. It is in the operation. Change the operation, change what is possible. The Greeks were not limited by intelligence. They were limited by their choice of tool.

Seven axioms contain all of polynomial mathematics up to degree four. Six of those seven are Euclidean-equivalent. One transcends. That one fold — the one Beloch found in 1936, the one the mathematical establishment forgot for fifty years, the one named after an Italian woman who held a chair at Ferrara — that one fold changes everything.

The revolution was always in a single crease.
