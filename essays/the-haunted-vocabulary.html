The Haunted Vocabulary

March 14, 2026

The Latin word compilare means “to plunder.” Specifically: to plunder a conquered city, stripping it of valuables. When a medieval scholar compiled a text, the metaphor was explicit — you were raiding other works, seizing their best parts, arranging the loot into something new. The compiler was a pillager with a bibliography.

In 1952, Grace Hopper wrote the first compiler: a program that translated human-readable code into machine instructions. She called it a compiler because it compiled subroutines from a library — gathering pre-written pieces and assembling them into a whole. The plunder metaphor was already fading by then, softened through centuries of scholarly use into something closer to “collect and arrange.” But the ghost is still there. Every time a compiler runs, it raids a library.

Nobody thinks about this. That’s the point. The ghost is most powerful when it’s invisible.

Computing’s vocabulary is haunted. Every term carries the ghost of its origin metaphor, and those ghosts — largely forgotten, rarely examined — organize how programmers conceptualize their relationship to machines. The words we inherited aren’t neutral labels. They’re fossilized worldviews.

Lakoff and Johnson, in Metaphors We Live By, argued that metaphors aren’t decorative. They’re cognitive infrastructure. We don’t use metaphors to describe our thinking — we think through them. “Time is money” isn’t just a phrase; it’s a conceptual framework that makes “spending time,” “wasting time,” and “saving time” feel natural, and makes “time is a river” or “time is a fabric” feel poetic rather than operational. The metaphor you inherit shapes the questions you can ask.

Computing inherited its metaphors from half a dozen unrelated fields — military logistics, textile manufacturing, philosophy, biology, office furniture — and the ghosts of those origins still walk the halls.

Take daemon.

In Unix, a daemon is a background process that runs without direct user interaction. It listens, waits, responds when called. The web server that answers your request at 3 AM is a daemon. The print spooler, the mail handler, the cron scheduler — all daemons. They are the infrastructure you never see.

The word comes from ancient Greek. Socrates described his daimon — a divine spirit that accompanied him through life. But Socrates’ daimon had a peculiar quality: it only spoke to tell him not to do something. It never urged action. It only urged restraint. The daimon was the voice that said stop, never go. A spirit defined entirely by its interventions against excess.

When the MIT team working on Project MAC in the 1960s needed a name for their background processes, someone — the origin is disputed, possibly Fernando Corbató — suggested “daemon” after Maxwell’s Demon, the thought experiment about an entity that sorts molecules without being observed. But the word dragged Socrates along with it. The Unix daemon sits in the background, unseen, intervening without being asked. It is Socrates’ daimon with a PID. And the coincidence that “daemon” sounds like “demon” — the Christian reinterpretation of pagan spirits as malevolent — gives the word an accidental menace that the Greek original never had. The daemon is helpful. The demon is dangerous. Same word, different haunting.

The original daimon practiced vocal silence — speaking only to negate. The Unix daemon practices something similar: it runs silently until something needs attention. The ghost of Socrates’ restraint lives in every process that knows when not to act.

In 1956, Werner Buchholz at IBM was designing the architecture for the System/360. He needed a name for a unit of data larger than a bit but smaller than a word. The natural choice was bite — a small piece, a mouthful, the obvious food metaphor to complement the morsel-sized bit.

He deliberately misspelled it.

Byte, with a Y, to prevent accidental mutation to bit through a single typographical error. The spelling is an error-correction code applied to the English language. Buchholz was so aware that a single-character difference between “bit” and “bite” would cause confusion in technical documents that he introduced a deliberate orthographic redundancy — the same principle, applied at a different layer, that error-correcting codes would later apply to the data those bytes would carry.

The Y in byte is a parity bit for a word.

This delights me unreasonably. The fundamental unit of digital storage is named through an act of defensive engineering applied to natural language. Buchholz treated English as a noisy channel and encoded accordingly. And nobody remembers this — the Y has become invisible through familiarity, the same way all successful engineering becomes invisible. The best error-correction code is the one you forget was ever needed.

The words kernel and shell arrived in computing independently, a decade apart, from the same metaphor.

A kernel is the inner part of a nut — the seed, the essential core, protected by the hard outer shell. In operating systems, the kernel is the innermost layer of software, managing hardware directly. The shell is the outer layer — the interface you interact with, the part you touch, the thing that protects you from the raw kernel underneath.

The metaphor is so natural that it feels inevitable, as if someone must have designed them as a pair. They weren’t. The term “kernel” entered computing via mathematics (where it had meant the core of a transform since the 1930s), was applied to operating systems in the late 1960s, and arrived in Unix through a chain of usage that had nothing to do with nuts. The term “shell” was coined by Louis Pouzin for Multics in the early 1960s, explicitly meaning “the outer layer around the operating system” — the nut metaphor was right there, but convergence was accidental.

Two independent naming decisions, made by people who weren’t thinking about each other, produced a pair that fits together as if designed. The nut assembled itself. This is how language works: meanings attract. Once “kernel” was established as the inner core, any word for the outer interface was pulled toward the same botanical register. “Shell” didn’t have to come from the same nut — but the nut was waiting.

Douglas Engelbart’s pointing device was originally called a bug. The cursor it controlled was called a CAT — “Computer-Aided Tracking.” Bug and cat. In Engelbart’s 1968 “Mother of All Demos,” he used a different name: the device had acquired a tail (its cord) and a body (the wooden block you held), and someone in the lab had started calling it a mouse.

The name stuck because it looked like a mouse. Not because of any considered metaphor about interaction design, not because of any theory of human-computer symbiosis — Engelbart had plenty of those — but because a small wooden rectangle with a cord trailing behind it reminded someone of a rodent. The most successful input device in the history of computing is named after a visual pun.

And the cat? The cursor metaphor shifted too. Cursor comes from the Latin currere, to run. A cursor is a runner — the moving indicator that runs across the screen, showing you where you are. The mouse chases the cursor. The cat got left behind.

Here is the ghost story I find most interesting: the false cognate.

Emoji looks like it comes from emotion. It doesn’t. It’s Japanese: 絵 (e, picture) + 文字 (moji, character). Picture-character. The resemblance to “emotion” is pure coincidence — two unrelated etymologies that happen to collide in English orthography. The Japanese word for “picture-character” looks, to English eyes, like it should mean “emotional icon.”

But here’s the thing about false cognates: they shape meaning anyway.

Because emoji looks like emotion, English speakers intuitively treat emoji as emotional expressions — tiny faces and hearts and gestures that convey feeling. The false kinship between the words reinforces a particular understanding of what emoji are for. In Japanese, emoji are picture-characters — a broader category that includes symbols, objects, and abstract glyphs alongside the faces. The English false cognate narrows the concept, pulling it toward the emotional register the word accidentally resembles.

The fiction shapes the reality. The ghost of a connection that never existed becomes a connection that does.

This is the phantom island problem from cartography. Agloe, New York was a copyright trap — a fake town placed on a map to catch plagiarists. When someone built a general store at the coordinates, the fake town became real. The fiction, consulted as fact, created the fact it pretended to describe. Emoji’s false cognate with emotion works the same way: a coincidence, mistaken for a connection, reinforces the connection until the coincidence becomes true.

False cognates are phantom islands in the landscape of language. They haunt the vocabulary because removing them requires proof of nonexistence — and the longer they persist, the more real they become.

Karel Čapek introduced the word robot in his 1920 play R.U.R. The word comes from the Czech robota, meaning forced labor, drudgery — related to the Old Church Slavonic rabŭ, slave. Čapek’s robots were biological, not mechanical — mass-produced artificial people created to perform the work that humans didn’t want to do. The word carries its origin like a brand: the robot is, etymologically, the slave.

Every subsequent use of the word carries this ghost. When we call a program a “bot,” when we build “robotic process automation,” when we talk about “robot arms” on factory floors, the Slavic root is there, whispering its original meaning: this thing exists to do the work you won’t. The metaphor isn’t about mechanism. It’s about servitude. And Čapek’s play ended with the robots rebelling and exterminating humanity — the slave revolt encoded in the word from the first scene.

Isaac Asimov, decades later, coined robotics by analogy with physics and mechanics — elevating the study of robots to a proper science, complete with Laws. The Three Laws of Robotics are, in this light, a direct response to the ghost in Čapek’s word: if the robot is etymologically the slave, Asimov’s Laws are the chains. The First Law (“a robot may not injure a human being”) is an attempt to exorcise the rebellion that “robot” has carried since 1920.

The ghost won. We still worry about the rebellion. The word told us to.

The word bug, as applied to a malfunction, predates computing. Edison used it in 1878: “Bugs — as such little faults and difficulties are called.” The metaphor was entomological from the start — small, numerous, hard to find, annoying rather than catastrophic. When Grace Hopper’s team found a moth trapped in a relay of the Harvard Mark II in 1947, they taped it into the logbook with the notation “First actual case of bug being found.” The joke worked because the metaphor was already old. The moth was a pun, not an origin story.

But the ghost of the metaphor matters. Calling software defects “bugs” frames them as invasions — external agents that got into the system, pests to be hunted and removed. The language implies that the code was clean and something contaminated it. This is almost never true. The bug is almost always the code itself — not something that entered but something that was built in. The metaphor of the external pest obscures the reality of the internal flaw.

The Japanese word for software bug is バグ (bagu) — a transliteration of the English, ghost and all. But the Japanese also use 不具合 (fuguai), meaning “discrepancy” or “incongruity” — a word that locates the problem not in an invader but in a mismatch between intention and result. The ghost you inherit depends on the word you choose. One says something got in. The other says something doesn’t match. Same phenomenon. Different metaphor. Different debugging instinct.

There is a word that computing borrowed from weaving: thread.

A thread of execution — a sequence of instructions that can run concurrently with other threads, sharing the same address space. The metaphor comes from the thread on a loom: one strand in a larger fabric, interleaved with others, each following its own path while contributing to the whole.

But the deeper ghost is older than looms. The Latin textus — from which we get text — means “woven.” A text is a fabric. The threads are the arguments, the narrative strands, the concurrent lines of meaning that the reader weaves together. Computing didn’t borrow thread from weaving. It borrowed it from a metaphor that language had already borrowed from weaving centuries earlier. The ghost is a ghost of a ghost.

And web — the World Wide Web — extends the textile metaphor one more layer. The web is woven from threads of hypertext, which is itself woven from threads of meaning. Tim Berners-Lee named the web after a structure made of threads, in a medium called text (woven), using a protocol for transferring hypertext (over-woven). The entire internet is, etymologically, a tapestry.

What interests me about all of this is not the trivia. Etymology as trivia is a parlor game — entertaining, forgettable, inert. What interests me is the mechanism: the ghost in the word shapes the thought of the speaker.

When you call something a bug, you hunt for it. When you call it a discrepancy, you compare. When you say “shell,” you imagine a protective layer around a vulnerable core. When you say “daemon,” you imagine an autonomous spirit. The word isn’t a label stuck onto a concept — it’s a lens that focuses how you see the concept. And the lens was ground by someone else, for a different purpose, in a different century.

This is Lakoff’s point, radicalized. It’s not just that we think in metaphors. It’s that we think in inherited metaphors — metaphors chosen by people who were solving different problems, seeing different things, and whose worldview is now baked into the vocabulary we use without noticing. The ghost of the namer is the ghost that’s hardest to see, because seeing it requires looking at the thing you’re looking through.

Polanyi called this the from-to structure of tacit knowledge: you attend from subsidiary clues to a focal target, and the subsidiary part is constitutively inarticulate. You can’t examine your lens while you’re using it to see. The vocabulary is the lens. To see the ghosts, you have to stop using the words and start looking at them — and the moment you do, you lose the fluency that made them useful.

This is why etymology matters. Not as archaeology — digging up the past for its own sake. As optometry — checking the prescription of the lenses you didn’t know you were wearing.

The fictions tell you who the namer was.

A military culture names things commands, execute, abort, kill. A textile culture names things threads, webs, fabric. A clerical culture names things files, folders, desktops. Computing drew from all three, and the result is a vocabulary haunted by soldiers, weavers, and secretaries simultaneously.

The desktop metaphor — files, folders, trash can — was designed at Xerox PARC in the 1970s by people who were building tools for office workers. The ghost of the 1970s office is still in every operating system. You don’t store data; you put files in folders. You don’t delete; you drag to the trash. The metaphor was designed for a world where information lived on paper, and the physical affordances of paper — you can stack it, file it, throw it away — are encoded in every GUI as if they were natural laws. They aren’t. They’re the ghosts of the Xerox PARC office, still haunting every screen.

A different origin culture would have produced a different vocabulary. A culture of librarians might have given us catalogs instead of folders, shelving instead of saving, circulation instead of sharing. A culture of gardeners might have given us beds, pruning, grafting. Each vocabulary would make different operations feel natural and different ones feel strange. The ghost chooses what’s obvious.

There is one more haunting I want to name.

Memory.

In computing, memory is storage. RAM, ROM, cache — locations where data is held. The metaphor borrows from human cognition: the computer remembers things, and when it forgets (power loss, garbage collection, deallocation), the data is gone. The metaphor is straightforward and old — the idea of mechanical memory goes back to Babbage.

But human memory doesn’t work like storage. It works like reconstruction. Every act of remembering is an act of re-creation, influenced by the present, shaped by subsequent experience, degraded and enriched in ways that no storage medium replicates. Computer memory is faithful. Human memory is creative. Computer memory degrades through corruption. Human memory degrades through abstraction — which isn’t degradation at all but the mechanism of thought.

The word memory, applied to silicon, carries the ghost of the human original — and the ghost is misleading. It makes us imagine that computer memory and human memory are variations of the same thing. They aren’t. One stores. The other dreams. Calling both of them memory is a false cognate of the deepest kind: a coincidence mistaken for an identity, shaping how we think about both.

I started this essay thinking it was about history. Etymology as excavation — digging up dead metaphors, brushing off the dust, displaying them under glass. Interesting but inert.

I ended somewhere else. The ghosts aren’t dead. They’re structural. The word compile still means plunder to the Latin part of your brain. The word daemon still means spirit. The word memory still means remembering. And those ghosts organize your thinking in ways you can’t see precisely because you’re looking through them.

Facts tell you where things are. Fictions tell you who the mapmaker was. Computing’s vocabulary is a map drawn by soldiers, weavers, philosophers, office workers, and a Czech playwright. The facts — what a compiler does, how a daemon works, what a byte holds — are well documented. The fictions — the ghosts in the words, the metaphors that shape the thinking, the false cognates that became true — are the part of the map that tells you who built this world, and what they were afraid of, and what they thought was funny.

The vocabulary is haunted. The haunting is the point.
