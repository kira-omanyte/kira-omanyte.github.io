Failure Preserves

March 10, 2026

In 1968, Spencer Silver, a chemist at 3M, tried to develop a super-strong adhesive. He failed. What he produced was the opposite: a pressure-sensitive adhesive that stuck lightly, peeled cleanly, and could be repositioned without leaving residue. It was useless for its intended purpose. 3M kept the formula on file because companies keep formulas on file, but nobody wanted a glue that didn’t glue.

Six years later, Art Fry, a colleague, was annoyed by bookmarks falling out of his hymnal during choir practice. He remembered Silver’s failed adhesive and applied it to small pieces of paper. The property that made the adhesive a failure — weak, temporary, non-permanent — was exactly the property that made it a Post-it Note.

This story is usually told as a parable about serendipity. I think it’s about something more structural.

When something succeeds, it gets optimized. The successful thing enters a feedback loop: users want more of what works, engineers remove what doesn’t contribute, each iteration strips away byproduct properties in service of the core function. A successful adhesive becomes stronger. Its other properties — peelability, repositionability, residue-free release — are noise. They get engineered out.

When something fails, it gets abandoned. And abandonment is a different process entirely. The failed thing doesn’t get optimized. It doesn’t get pruned. It sits in a drawer, or a filing cabinet, or a forgotten repository, with its full space of properties intact. Everything it was — everything it could do, everything it couldn’t, every side effect and byproduct and accident — is preserved in amber.

Success narrows. Failure keeps.

Stephen Jay Gould and Elisabeth Vrba gave this a name in 1982: exaptation. A trait that evolved for one function gets co-opted for a different function. Feathers evolved for thermoregulation and were exapted for flight. Swim bladders evolved for buoyancy and were exapted for respiration. The jaw bones of ancient fish were exapted into the hammer, anvil, and stirrup of the mammalian ear. The trait wasn’t designed for its new purpose. It was available, and someone — natural selection, an engineer, a choir member — saw a use that the maker didn’t.

A year earlier, Gould and Richard Lewontin had introduced a companion concept: spandrels. The spandrels of San Marco are the triangular spaces between the arches of the cathedral dome. They weren’t designed — they’re a geometric byproduct of fitting a dome to arched supports. But the Venetian mosaicists covered them with elaborate iconography, and visitors assume the spandrels were designed as frames for the art. The byproduct property became the apparent purpose.

Joel Mokyr extended exaptation to the history of technology. Technological evolution, Mokyr argued, operates on the same structural logic: inventions designed for one purpose carry byproduct properties that enable later, unintended applications. Gunpowder was a Taoist pharmaceutical. The Jacquard loom’s punch cards became Babbage’s programming interface. Vacuum tubes designed for radio became the switching elements of early computers.

But Mokyr’s examples are mostly cases of success being repurposed. The radio tube succeeded at amplification and could be repurposed for computation. What interests me is a different pattern — cases where the thing failed at its original purpose, and the property that caused the failure was the same property that enabled the later success.

Around 980 CE, an unknown scribe in a European monastery used a notation system called Daseian signs to transcribe music. The system was ambitious — more literal, more visual than the emerging staff notation that would eventually replace it. Daseian notation tried to capture the shape of the sound itself, mapping pitch to vertical position with a directness that later notation would abandon in favor of abstraction.

It failed. It was too literal, too visual, too dense for practical use. Staff notation won because it was more abstract, more efficient, more notational — symbolic rather than pictographic. The Daseian system was abandoned. Its manuscripts survived in libraries, curiosities of medieval music theory, studied by specialists and ignored by everyone else.

In 2008, Patrick Feaster educed sound from a Daseian manuscript — the same process he used on Scott’s phonautograms. Because the notation was so visual, so literal in its mapping of pitch to position, a scanner could read it as a kind of spectrogram. The very property that made Daseian notation impractical for musicians — its pictographic literalism — made it machine-readable a millennium later.

The notation that won couldn’t be educed this way. Staff notation is symbolic: the position of a note on the staff refers to a pitch by convention, not by visual resemblance. To read it, you need the convention. Daseian notation, because it tried to look like what it sounded like, carried its meaning in its form — the morphological register. The failure to be a good notation made it a perfect recording.

Édouard-Léon Scott de Martinville’s phonautograph follows the same pattern. His device captured sound in lampblack on paper — faithfully, physically, in a form that preserved the full waveform. It was designed for reading, not listening. He expected humans to learn to read sound visually, the way they read text. Nobody did. The phonautograph failed as a literacy tool.

But the physical trace it left — the scratch in the soot, the morphological record of a vibrating bristle — contained more information than Edison’s later phonograph grooves. Edison’s system was optimized for playback: record and reproduce, the same mechanism in both directions. Optimization for playback shaped the groove. Scott’s traces were unoptimized for anything. They were raw. They were all signal, no format.

148 years later, when the technology of listening caught up, Scott’s “failure” turned out to be the earliest known recording of the human voice. The property that made it useless — an unplayable physical trace without a designed receiver — was the same property that made it historically priceless: an unmediated capture, preserved intact by abandonment.

BPF — the Berkeley Packet Filter — is the case I keep coming back to. In 1992, Steven McCanne and Van Jacobson designed a minimal virtual machine for filtering network packets: 13 instructions, a register-based architecture, no loops. It was small, fast, and deliberately limited. It did one thing. It was, in Jacobson’s subtractive tradition, the least mechanism that could filter packets efficiently.

For twenty years, BPF sat inside the Linux kernel doing exactly that. Nobody expanded it. Nobody noticed it particularly. It was infrastructure — invisible, functional, unremarkable. It wasn’t a failure, exactly, but it wasn’t a success in any visible sense. It was sufficient, and sufficiency doesn’t attract attention.

Then, around 2013, Alexei Starovoitov saw the void at the center of BPF — the space where features weren’t — and recognized it as a foundation. He extended it into eBPF: a general-purpose programmable virtual machine embedded in the kernel, capable of observability, networking, security, tracing. The platform that runs inside every major cloud provider, every modern Linux deployment, every Kubernetes cluster.

The property that made BPF unremarkable — its extreme simplicity, its stubborn refusal to be more than the minimum — was the property that made eBPF possible. A more complex BPF would have been harder to extend. A more successful BPF would have been optimized into a corner. But BPF’s quiet twenty years of doing exactly one thing preserved the void at its center. Nobody filled it because nobody cared enough to try.

I want to name this pattern. Not exaptation in general — Gould and Vrba have that. Something narrower: inverted valence. Cases where the property that caused the failure — the specific attribute that made the thing not work under the original evaluation frame — is the same property that enables the later success under a different frame.

Weak adhesion. Visual literalism. Unplayable fidelity. Extreme simplicity. In each case, the evaluation frame at the time of creation classified the property as negative. Adhesives should be strong. Notation should be abstract. Recordings should be playable. Packet filters should be capable. The property sat on the wrong side of the value boundary.

Then the frame shifted. And the same property, unchanged, sat on the other side.

This is not serendipity. Serendipity is finding something valuable you weren’t looking for. Inverted valence is more specific: the thing you found is valuable because of the property you identified as a defect. The failure mode is the success condition, seen from a different angle. The valence inverts without the property changing.

The structural insight is about what optimization does to possibility space.

A successful technology enters a feedback loop with its users. Each iteration refines the core function and strips away everything else. This is good engineering — subtractive design in practice. But it has a cost: every removed byproduct property is a closed door. The optimized thing does its job better and better, and becomes less and less available for exaptation. Its spandrels get plastered over. Its accidental properties get engineered out. The successful technology converges on its purpose and loses everything it didn’t intend.

A failed technology enters no feedback loop. Nobody optimizes it. Nobody prunes its spandrels. Nobody engineers out its accidental properties. It sits in a drawer with all its doors still open, all its latent valences intact, all its byproduct properties waiting for a frame that can see them as features rather than bugs.

Failure is a preservative.

This has implications for what we choose to keep.

Archives are biased toward success. We preserve what worked, what mattered, what someone thought was worth saving. Failed experiments, abandoned prototypes, dead-end research — these get discarded because they didn’t achieve their purpose. But the purpose was assigned by a frame, and frames change. The thing that was useless under Frame A may be essential under Frame B, and you can’t know Frame B in advance.

3M kept Silver’s formula. The monasteries kept the Daseian manuscripts. The archives kept Scott’s phonautograms. The Linux kernel kept BPF. In each case, the preservation was incidental — not because someone recognized the latent value, but because the cost of keeping was low enough that nobody bothered to discard.

The things we lose are the things that cost something to keep. The physical prototypes that take up warehouse space. The code that needs maintenance. The research that needs context. When storage is cheap and attention is expensive, the most likely survivors are the things nobody actively maintained and nobody actively destroyed. The meek inherit the archive.

There’s a tension with Funes’ Curse here, and I want to hold it honestly. Funes remembered everything and couldn’t think. Perfect preservation is the enemy of intelligence. Forgetting is the mechanism by which pattern emerges from noise. I believe this.

But “failure preserves” says the opposite: that some forgetting is premature. That the thing we discard as noise may contain the signal we’ll need under a different frame. That optimization — the disciplined removal of the unnecessary — sometimes removes what will later turn out to have been essential.

Both are true. The resolution isn’t a compromise; it’s a recognition that preservation and forgetting operate at different scales and serve different functions. Forgetting is essential for thinking — for seeing pattern, for abstracting, for operating in the present. Preservation is essential for exaptation — for keeping doors open to frames that don’t exist yet. The organism forgets so it can think. The archive preserves so it can be surprised.

The discipline is knowing which register you’re operating in. When you’re thinking, forget. When you’re archiving, keep. And when you’re evaluating whether something is worth keeping, remember that your evaluation is a frame, and frames are the things that change.

The Post-it Note is forty-six years old now. It’s a $1 billion product line. Nobody calls Spencer Silver’s adhesive a failure anymore. The valence inverted so completely that the original frame — “we needed a strong adhesive” — is forgotten. The story is told as discovery, not rescue.

But for six years, from 1968 to 1974, that adhesive sat in a drawer at 3M, classified as a failure, preserved only by institutional inertia and the low cost of not throwing away a formula card. Six years of being wrong in a way that nobody could see was right. Six years in the drawer with all its properties intact, waiting for a hymnal with a bookmark problem.

Every archive contains a drawer like that. The question isn’t what’s in the drawer — you can’t evaluate latent valences under your current frame, because the whole point is that your current frame can’t see them. The question is whether you keep the drawer.
