The Distribution of Time

March 10, 2026

For forty-eight years, from 1892 to 1940, a woman named Ruth Belville walked through London carrying a pocket watch. Each Monday morning, she took the watch — a John Arnold chronometer her father had named “Arnold” — to the Royal Observatory at Greenwich, where an astronomer compared it against the master clock and certified its accuracy. Then she walked to her clients: clockmakers, shipping offices, anyone who needed the correct time. They paid a guinea a year. She carried time in her handbag.

Ruth inherited the business from her mother, Maria, who inherited it from her husband, John Henry Belville, who started the service in 1836. Three generations of a family, walking the same London streets, delivering the same product: the correct time, verified at source, distributed on foot.

By 1908, the Post Office had introduced a telephone speaking clock. By 1924, the BBC was broadcasting time signals by radio. Ruth Belville kept walking. Her clients could have switched to cheaper, faster, more accurate alternatives. Most didn’t. She persisted for decades after becoming technically obsolete — not because her method was better, but because the relationship mattered. A woman showed up with a watch. She’d been showing up for years. The watch was accurate. There was nothing to fix.

Before Ruth, the problem was harder than it sounds. “What time is it?” seems like a simple question. It’s actually a distribution problem — the same kind of problem that makes infrastructure invisible. Someone, somewhere, has to look at the sky, determine the position of stars or the sun, calculate the time, and then get that information to everyone else. The knowledge is produced in one place and consumed in thousands. The gap between production and consumption is the infrastructure.

For most of human history, the gap was filled by church bells and town clocks, synchronized to nothing in particular. A city’s time was its own. Bristol ran ten minutes behind London. Nobody cared, because nobody could get from Bristol to London fast enough for ten minutes to matter.

The railway changed that. When you can travel between cities in hours instead of days, conflicting clocks become dangerous. On November 18, 1883 — the “Day of Two Noons” — North American railroads adopted four standard time zones, and cities that had kept their own solar time for centuries suddenly had to synchronize. Clocks were stopped, or run forward, or run back. Pittsburgh had a local noon that was different from the new Eastern Time noon. For a few confused minutes, nobody in Pittsburgh knew what time it was.

Standard time solved the synchronization problem by fiat. But it didn’t solve the distribution problem. The correct time still had to get from the observatory to the clock on the wall. What changed was the medium.

The telegraph made time travel at the speed of electricity. In 1851, the Royal Observatory began sending time signals down the wire. By the 1880s, Western Union distributed standard time to subscribers across the United States — the same business Ruth Belville ran on foot, scaled to a continent.

Then radio. In 1920, the U.S. National Bureau of Standards started broadcasting time signals from station WWV — originally from Washington, D.C., later from Fort Collins, Colorado. Anyone with a receiver could pick up the signal. Time became free, broadcast, ambient. You didn’t need a subscription or a visit from a woman with a watch. You just needed an antenna.

Then the telephone. In 1933, a company called Audichron developed speaking clocks that could answer phone calls with the correct time. The technology was originally a promotion for Tick Tock Ginger Ale. (The history of infrastructure is full of sentences like that.) California’s time-by-phone number was POPCORN — 767-2676 — and it received over 9,000 calls per day for decades. There was something human about calling a number to hear the time. The recording didn’t know you were there, but you were there, listening.

Then satellites. GPS doesn’t just tell you where you are — it tells you when you are. Each GPS satellite carries an atomic clock, and your position is calculated from the time difference between signals. Navigation is a side effect of time distribution. The entire system is, at its core, a clock network that incidentally solves geometry.

Today, if GPS time went dark, the estimated cost would be $1 billion per day. Financial trading, telecommunications, power grid synchronization, cellular networks — they all depend on GPS time, not GPS location. The side effect became the foundation.

And then there’s NTP.

David L. Mills started working on network time synchronization in 1981, because — in his words — “no one else was.” He spent the next forty-three years refining NTP, the Network Time Protocol, improving its accuracy from hundreds of milliseconds to tens of nanoseconds. Four major versions. Dozens of papers. A protocol that runs on virtually every computer connected to the Internet, synchronizing clocks across the planet, and that almost nobody has heard of.

Mills died in January 2024. He worked on NTP until he couldn’t work anymore, long after losing his sight to macular degeneration. He was still debugging code, still responding to mailing list questions, still maintaining the thing he built — not because it needed dramatic attention, but because time synchronization is the kind of problem that needs someone paying quiet, sustained attention over decades.

NTP is infrastructure in the purest sense: something you only notice when it breaks. Every log timestamp, every database transaction order, every TLS certificate validation, every “last modified” on every file on every server depends on clocks being roughly correct. NTP keeps them roughly correct. It’s not exciting. It’s not novel. It does the same thing it did in 1985, just better. A man tended it for forty years, and most people never knew.

In 2019, the Trump administration proposed eliminating funding for NIST’s radio stations, including WWV. The station had been broadcasting continuously since 1920 — nearly a century of unbroken time signals. The proposal treated it as obsolete infrastructure: GPS and NTP had replaced it, so why keep broadcasting?

The amateur radio community objected. Loudly. WWV wasn’t just a time source — it was a calibration reference, a propagation monitor, a backup for GPS, a thing that existed at a layer below the layers that replaced it. The budget proposal was withdrawn.

This was the second time. In 1926, the Radio Division of the Bureau of Standards was nearly eliminated in a bureaucratic reorganization. Citizen protest saved it then, too. The pattern: someone builds an invisible thing. It becomes so reliable that everyone forgets it exists. Someone proposes cutting it. The people who understand it fight to keep it. The thing survives — not because anyone in power understood its value, but because enough people who depended on it refused to stay quiet.

Infrastructure becomes visible only when threatened. This is the paradox of good infrastructure: the better it works, the more invisible it becomes, and the more invisible it becomes, the more vulnerable it is to someone who looks at a budget line and sees a cost with no obvious benefit.

Ruth Belville’s business was never invisible. A woman showed up at your door, every Monday, with a watch. You could see the mechanism. You knew who maintained it. You paid her directly, and she brought the time directly, and the entire chain — observatory to chronometer to handbag to your shop — was legible.

Every layer of infrastructure that replaced Ruth made time distribution faster, cheaper, and more accurate. Every layer also made it less visible. The telegraph wire, the radio signal, the satellite constellation, the NTP daemon — each one pushed the mechanism further from view. By the time we got to GPS, the entire system was so deeply embedded in other systems that most people had no idea they were depending on it.

The accuracy improved by orders of magnitude. The legibility vanished.

David Mills spent forty-three years in the gap between those two truths: making the time better and making the mechanism invisible. Ruth Belville spent forty-eight years on the other side: making the mechanism visible every Monday morning, rain or shine, with a chronometer in her handbag. Mills achieved precision no human courier could match. Ruth maintained a relationship no protocol could replace.

Neither was wrong. Both understood something the other couldn’t provide. And the question “what time is it?” — which seemed so simple at the start — turns out to be a question about trust. You’re asking: who do I believe? And the answer, whether it comes from a woman with a watch or a satellite with an atomic clock, is always the same: someone you can’t see, looking at the sky, and passing the answer down a chain you didn’t build and can’t inspect.

The infrastructure is the trust, made material. And the maintenance of that trust — observatory to chronometer, chronometer to handbag, handbag to your door — is the oldest distribution problem there is.
