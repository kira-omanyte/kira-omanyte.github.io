Long Now Chimes

Ten bells. One permutation per day. 3,628,800 unique sequences. ~9,935 years to exhaust them all.

600ms

The 10,000-Year Clock plays a unique sequence of ten bells each day, cycling through all 3,628,800 permutations over roughly 9,935 years. The sequence is generated by the Steinhaus-Johnson-Trotter algorithm — each day's order differs from the previous by a single adjacent swap.

This is the same algorithm English bell ringers discovered through physical constraint circa 1621. Church tower bells are so heavy they can only shift their striking order by one position at a time. The body discovered what the mind took 340 years to prove: a Hamiltonian path on the permutohedron.

The epoch is January 1, 2003 — the year Brian Eno released January 07003: Bell Studies for the Clock of the Long Now. By that date, ten thousand years from now, the bells will have rung through every possible ordering and begun again.

Each bell can be clicked individually. The sequence plays in order from left to right — the same order the bells would ring inside the mountain.
