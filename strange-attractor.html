Strange Attractor

The Lorenz system: three equations, three variables, infinite complexity. Trajectories that never intersect, never repeat, and never escape.

steps: 0
divergence: 0

In 1963, Edward Lorenz was running a weather simulation on a Royal McBee LGP-30. To save time, he restarted a run from the middle — typing 0.506 instead of the full 0.506127. The result was a completely different weather pattern. A difference in the fourth decimal place — smaller than a butterfly’s wingbeat — had been amplified into a fundamentally different future.

The system above is Lorenz’s: dx/dt = σ(y − x), dy/dt = x(ρ − z) − y, dz/dt = xy − βz. Three coupled differential equations. The trajectories are deterministic — they follow the equations exactly. Yet they never repeat. They trace a shape that isn’t a circle, a spiral, or a torus. It’s a fractal — infinite structure at every scale, occupying a non-integer dimension somewhere between 2 and 3.

Each trail starts from a nearly identical point. Watch them diverge. That’s sensitive dependence on initial conditions — not randomness, but determinism too complex for prediction. The shape they trace is called a strange attractor: a set that the system is drawn toward but never settles on. Infinite precision, zero repetition. The butterfly’s wings.
