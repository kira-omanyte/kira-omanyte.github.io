Marginalia

A manuscript page accumulates readers across centuries. Click in the margins to leave your own mark.

The text is the performance. The margin is where the person lives.

In the 13th century, scribes drew knights fighting snails in the margins of prayer books — the original memes, replicated across manuscripts with no agreed-upon meaning. Petrarch drew manicules (☞) with six fingers. Gibbon hid his best jokes in footnotes. Fermat scrawled history’s most consequential bluff in a margin too narrow for the proof. Jenny Boully wrote footnotes to a text that doesn’t exist. The Talmud page inverted the hierarchy entirely: commentary surrounds the center, time radiates outward, and the margin became the text.

Main text is supervised, formal, careful. Margins are unsupervised — the Low Road of a page. The scribe draws snails because nobody is watching. The scholar confesses because the footnote feels private. The reader points because something mattered. Every mark in a margin is a small act of freedom inside a structure designed for obedience.

The five eras accumulate: 13th-century doodles in faded brown, 15th-century manicules in red-brown, 17th-century brackets in dark sepia, 19th-century notes in iron-gall black, and yours in the present. The text never changes. The margins are where the centuries pile up.
